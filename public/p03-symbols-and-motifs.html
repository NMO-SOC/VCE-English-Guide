Symbols and Motifs

Symbols

SymbolWhere is it seen? Scenes & QuotesWhat does it represent?
Encyclopedias“The encyclopedia Britannica. Your entree to the world of learning, a world of discovery, a world of interesting facts…’’ Pg 133 “Encyclops boy and he knows nothing’’ Pg 146 “You mean you’ve got to pay for those things?’’ “Could you give us a hand with the encyclopedia set?’’ “They’re only possessions. And what do they matter? People are all that matter’’ Pg 176For Gladys- The embodiment of the family’s aspirations; they provide a sense of hope, particularly for the future. For Nan- Wary of the way governmental institutions have commercialized and chipped away at the knowledge of the Aboriginal people. Errol- has little need for the books, highlighting the fact that he has privilege as a white male, even though he doesn’t have the same vocabulary and knowledge. The cost of the encyclopedia collection alludes to the cost of Westernised knowledge at the expense of Indigenous knowledge. Even though the encyclopedias are destroyed, the Dear’s continue on with they life, emphasising that Western knowledge is not essential.
‘Que Sera, Sera’“Que Sera Sera, Whatever will be will be, the futures not ours to see. Que Sera, Sera.’’ Pg 124, pg 199 “I asked my sweetheart what lies ahead’’ Pg 175Que Sera Sera describes an attitude of cheerful fatalism. The lyrics of the song suggest that events are predetermined in advance and humans are powerless in determining their future. Each verse of the song, represents the character being taken through the journey of their life and as one ages and grows up. The story is bookended at the beginning and end of the story, the song has the role of opening and closing the play. At the end of the play, the song plays again, this time the song is suggesting that events are not predetermined if people take control of their own circumstances. The song explains that fate is a detriment of human destiny.
The Cork Trees“Dolly walks past the cork trees. She sees someone in the shadows’’ Pg 139 “That can be our special meeting spot’’ [cork tree] Pg 149 “Well hello Dolly… thought I’d catch up with you’’ Pg 172The cork trees represent a place of separation between the innocence of Dolly/her youth and the outside world. While the cork trees are associated with good memories such as her time in the Flats and her special spot with Errol, this location is now sinister and traumatic for Dolly. At the cork trees Goomeys try to coerce Dolly to drink. The cork trees are also where she is sexually assaulted by a cousin. Pivotal good and bad moments in Dolly’s life are associated with the cork trees. The cork trees are also a danger for Aunty Ester who begins to find solace in sex and alcohol after her children are taken away.
Ajax“Ajax contains bleach, you’ll stop paying elbow tax’’ Pg 155 “Honestly their mad about whiteness’’ Pg 155The Ajax bleach solution is used to explain an obsession with “whiteness” and a European society. Ajax is heavily advertised on the radio. Gladys eventually comes to reject this ideology by the middle of the play, switching off the radio. “mad about whiteness’’
Radio“The radio is heard featuring a description of the Queens; 1954 Royal Tour of Australia’’ Pg 127 “Bob Dyers quiz show, Pick-a-Box on the radio’’ Pg 128 “Dolly out of sight, is singing to the radio’’ Pg 154 “We resume our broadcast of the Rodney Shire Council Meeting’’ Pg 180The radio represents the connection the women have with the world outside the humpy and what is happening around the local shire. It offers an insight into a world they are barred from by Europeans. The radio station is one of the few things indigenous Australians and European settlers share. The radio always appears to be ominously playing in the Dear’s household. The radio is the external pressure which promotes discriminatory information. The radio serves as a parallel narrative to that of the lives of the Indigenous women. Gladys eventually has her dream of being on the radio, but not in the context of appeasing to a British radio show Pick a Box. Instead, Glady’s appearance on the radio is her fighting for her voice an indigenous Australian woman.
Swan Eggs“I’m gonna go back and die there’’ [Murray River] Pg 125 “And I’ll have a feed of swan eggs’’ Pg 125 “Swan eggs? You’re okay aren’t you Nan?’’ Pg 157The swan eggs are a representation of Nans dream, to go back to her country on the Murray River and feast on swan eggs before she dies. The swan eggs show Nans longing for a desire to return home and her connection to country. Swan eggs becomes imminent when Nan’s passing is discussed
The humpy“rebuilding after a flood has devastated their home’’ Pg 123 “Dolly is really embarrassed that Gladys has allowed Errol into the humpy’’ Pg 144 “Gladys is going around the humpy emptying the mouse traps of dead mice.’’The humpy is the shelter Nan and Gladys have turned into a “home” for Dolly. Nan and Gladys go to immense lengths to provide a comfortable and loving home for Dolly this is shown by them laying linoleum on the dirt floor and repairing the house when needed. The humpy symbolises the resilience of the women. Despite countless floods, they make the humble shelter a “home”
The house at Rumbalara- ‘Rainbow’s End’“From riverbank humpy to whitehouse is quite a step’’ Pg 177 “This is the most vigorous attempt yet to solve Aboriginal housing’’ Pg 178 “Concrete, small, white and featureless? Any thing but loveable’’ Pg 178 “Not quite the ‘new deal’’’ Pg 178Rumbalara is the new housing that was provided to the Indigenous Australians following the devastation of the floods. The name “Rumbalara” means at the “end of the rainbow”. The name suggests that this housing is adequate for Indigenous Australians. Unlike a rainbow, the concrete house is bare and white. Once again Gladys and Nan must try to make Rumbalara a stable home for Dolly and her child.
The pram“the lights come up on Dolly pushing a pram’’ Pg 132 “Gladys appear with a crampy old bookcase balanced on the pram’’ Pg 142 “Triumphantly, she pulls an obviously used and bashed can from the pram.’’ Pg 143 “They both look over at the pram and smile.’’ Pg 192 “He sits between her and Dolly and peeks in the pram’’ Pg 192 “Dolly and Errol, pram in the middle gets hitched.’’ Pg 198Dolly is seen carrying the pram at numerous stages during the text. Dolly is seen pushing the pram when she first meets Errol, foreshadowing the end of the text when she is with him and the same pram holds her child. This pram was initially used to carry scraps and materials from Daich’s Paddock, the pram now carry’s the effects of Dolly’s assault, a child
The ball“There’s a dance on in Shepparton next Saturday’’ Pg 148 “Not just any ball. The Miss Mooroopna-Shepparton Ball’’ Pg 159 “Re this ball, why are you saying if she can step out? She’s my daughter, I have brought up 3 others before her.’’ Pg 162 “She’s going to the ball. And Errol Fisher is walking her home’’ Pg 162The ball is an event Dolly longs to attend with Errol. Dolly associated the ball with finally being recognised by the European society. The ball is a way for Dolly to feel like she is not some ordinary girl from the Flats. Attending the ball signifies the one time in the text where Dolly is not protected by Gladys or Nan. Unfortunately, her attendance causes a chain of events resulting in her ending the relationship with Errol and being sexually assaulted by Leon. The ball is no longer a memory of happiness by one of pain and regret.
The Family Tree“I need a list of all our family members… our parents, their parent and so on… but not cousins’’ Pg 124 “You need to know who your cousins are so you don’t marry ‘em’’ Pg 125 “Reginald Harold Dears parents are?’’ Pg 126The family trees shows the importance and the interconnectedness of the community. Nan challenges Dolly’s perception and family when she is reluctant to include cousins in the family lineage. However, the family tree is also a reminder of Nan’s past and the secret she holds onto. Nan cannot continue the family tree on Gladys paternal side due to her sexual assault and her lack of knowledge about Gladys’ real father. The incomplete and complicated family tree is the consequence of the Stolen Generations.
Dolly’s dress“Can you make me a new dress Nan? A gorgeous one with lots of fabric- pretty fabric.’’ Pg 160 “A big skirt. And a peplum’’ Pg 160 “It’s not house of Biba’’ Pg 160 “Dolly is swirling and floating around the dance floor in her full-length skirted dress’’ Pg 165 “courtesy of the town bloody tip’’ Pg 166 “sobbing her dress is slightly ripped’’ Pg 167Dolly’s dress represents her desire to emulate white, European ideals and mimic those like Nancy Woolfthorpe. The resourcefulness of the family is shown with their ability to construct a beautiful dress from the remains of curtains. The dress later becomes associated with a terrible and traumatic night. At the dress rips and damages further, it signifies how Dolly is torn into by Nancy, Leon and Errol.

Motifs

MotifWhere is it seen? Scenes & QuotesWhat does it represent?
Music“Que Sera Sera’’ “Catch a falling star’’ “Somewhere over the rainbow’’In Que Sera, Sera. Each verse takes the audience though a key stage in the characters lives in which they are at the mercy of outside forces. Catch a falling star represents the very minimal luck that the family has, and that Dolly is unlikely to get the traineeship. Rather that catching stars, she is destined to be catching fruit like her mother and grandmother. Somewhere over the rainbow alludes to First Nations yearning to return home after having lost their land and being relocated to Rumbalara, which is “over the rainbow.” This song is seen in the Wizard of Oz when Dorothy longs to return home, however, she has a happy ending, suggesting such outcomes are only fiction of Hollywood.
Water“Nan Dear and Gladys are rebuilding after a flood has devastated their home’’ Pg 123 “A tap appears from nowhere and from it flows blue jewels in an approximation of water.’’ Pg 155 “I’d have to leave the river…’’ Pg 171 “Rain, thunder, darkness’’ Pg 175 “The water was dried away but devastation has been wrought’’ Pg 176Water is associated with both destruction and change. While water had the capability to devastate the Dears, it resulted in the move to Rumbalara, with water flowing from a tap. Furthermore, the Yorta Yorta people, prevalent in the region Rainbow’s End is set in, have a strong connection to waters and rivers.
DreamsDream sequence of Gladys meeting the Queen (Gladys) Pg 126 Gladys being a guest on Pick-A-Box (Gladys) Pg 130 Dolly parading with a robe and clapboard hat (Gladys) Pg 134 Dolly buying luxurious goods in a designer store (Dolly) Pg 139 Dream sequence of Papa Dear returning home (Gladys) Pg 143 Water flowing from the housing and Rumbalara (Gladys) Pg 155 Dolly winning Miss Mooroopna- Shepparton Ball (Dolly) Pg 166 Dolly getting married to Errol and them looking after the baby (Nan) Pg 198The dream sequences reveal the unspoken desires and hopes of the characters that they often do not admit to themselves. The vivid nature of the dream sequences suggests how greatly the characters long for these outcomes. The characters frequently has dream sequences, suggesting the most optimistic and hopeful. Nan having a dream sequence towards the end of the text suggests that she too is now hopeful for Dolly’s future with Errol and her unexpected child. Dream sequences become far less frequent following Dolly’s assault, indirectly conveying the impact of this event on the characters. They become less hopeful for Dolly and her outcomes.
BeatPg 183, 194, 186, 187, 188, 190The beat is a moment which propels the story forward. It aims to compel the audience to take stock of what may happen next while propelling the story forward. The beat emphasises what has been said and what is going to be said next. The beat helps to recapture the audience’s attention thought the play.
House Décor“Gladys appears with a crappy old bookcase balanced on a pram’’ Pg 142 “Emptying the mice traps of dead mice and setting new ones’’ Pg 160 “Dolly hoist the bolt iron over her shoulder as the salesman fades away’’ Pg 139 “Dolly passes Gladys the jam tins. One at a time’’ Pg 144 “Just boiled up in a Kero tin, with velvet soap and a blue bag, same as everyone.’’ Pg 152 “We don’t go much for presents’’ Pg 170The Dears are frequently seen collecting scraps and discarded material from Daiches paddock. Despite it’s condition, the women are still able to transform this décor into elements that make their humpy a home. Although the furniture is not elaborate and expensive, it is functional.
