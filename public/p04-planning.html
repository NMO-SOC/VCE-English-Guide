Planning

Deconstructing the Prompt

When first planning your essay, you must directly respond to the prompt that is presented to you, and consider the key authorial messages that could help you respond with insight. This involves three important steps:

  • Find the key concepts. This will most likely correlate with the key concepts you have studied throughout the unit, you should also make sure to identify synonyms of these concepts too!

  • Find any instructional verbs. These are words that instruct you to answer the prompt in a certain manner, in English the main verbs to look out for is discuss, do you agree, to what extent or how.

  • Identify any expectations/limitations. These ore words that limit what you con include in your essays and set on expectation on what you should be discussing.

Example prompt: ‘The dream sequences in Rainbow’s End are the characters’ only escape from the depressing reality of life.’ Do you agree? This is a very straightforward prompt—you need to demonstrate a multiple-sided response to what Harrison perceives as the role of dreams and aspirations in her characters’ lives. There are no limits to what should be discussed here, but you are expected to focus your discussion on the ideas of dreams, escape, and reality as they relate to the values, identities, and choices of Nan Dear, Gladys, and Dolly. Consider the historical context of 1950s assimilationist Australia, racism, class, and the characters’ evolving sense of agency throughout the play.

Contention

Now that you understand what the prompt is asking, we can begin to think about what our interpretation of Harrison’s message is. Let’s consider the first prompt again: ‘The dream sequences in Rainbow’s End are the characters’ only escape from the depressing reality of life.’ Do you agree? We can approach this in multiple ways. Here are three simplistic interpretations (agree, disagree, somewhere in between):

  • Harrison suggests that dreams offer the characters comfort, but they cannot change the reality of their circumstances.

  • Harrison implies that dreams are the characters’ only refuge from a society that denies them dignity and opportunity.

  • Harrison demonstrates that it is action, not dreaming, that ultimately allows the characters to escape their circumstances.

However, these interpretations are quite general and lack specificity. They don’t tell the reader exactly what you are arguing. Let’s improve them with more precise language and deeper knowledge of the text:

  • Harrison portrays dreams as both sustaining and limiting, suggesting that Gladys’s fantasies of meeting the Queen preserve her dignity while simultaneously revealing how deeply colonial ideals of acceptance have been internalised.

  • Harrison implies that, for a family denied housing, education, and respect, the imagination becomes a vital site of survival and self-worth in a society that offers no material escape.

  • Harrison critiques passive dreaming by charting Gladys’s transformation from daydreamer to activist, suggesting that genuine escape from injustice comes not from fantasy but from finding one’s voice and demanding change.

These improved interpretations use specific language, engage with the complexity of the characters’ motivations, and demonstrate a sophisticated understanding of the play’s ideas.

Extending Sentences - Connectives

To create a ’sophisticated’ idea you need to avoid single ideas, all of the contentions above are singular and lack connections to other ideas. We can connect them together using three simple connectives (and their advanced synonyms!)

The sentence should be focused on an authorial message, not just a concept or character­ make the author active -the most common start is the author+ verb: E.g Harrison explores/communicates/illustrates etc.

Specificity and Relevancy

Examiners punish vague or general statements, especially those that lack clear links to the topic or the author’s message. For example: By emphasising the importance of dreams in the play, Harrison shows that they matter to Gladys. This is not technically incorrect—but it is far too general and superficial. It doesn’t demonstrate insightful analysis, nor does it show a clear understanding of Harrison’s authorial intent. This kind of vague sentence would not receive full marks. Here’s an improved version: By emphasising Gladys’s fantasies of royal recognition, Harrison portrays how dreams function not as an escape from reality, but as a fragile shield of dignity for those denied respect and opportunity by an assimilationist society. This is better. It is clear, specific, well-structured, and shows awareness of both the theme (dreams vs reality), character motivation, and authorial message. It also directly links to the contention: that dreams sustain the characters, even in circumstances that offer no material escape. The issue you will face is that there is only so much you can discuss in your paragraph from here, as you have only examined one idea—dreams sustain dignity. To fix this, lets’ go back to an old friend:

Because, But, So - Make a Complex Sentence!

Too often, students fail to complete long paragraphs because they are stuck repeating themselves or have run out of things to say about their topic they introduced. Therefore, if we have two ideas in our topic sentence, we can ’hinge’ these sentences together which demonstrate complex understanding, writing and structure - all of them are key parts of the rubric you are judged on! Here’s an example:

By emphasising Gladys’s daydreams of royal recognition, Harrison conveys how, for a family denied dignity and opportunity, the imagination becomes a vital refuge from an unjust reality—ultimately suggesting that dreams alone cannot deliver change, and that true escape demands the courage to speak and act.

Now, you have two linked ideas:

  • Dreams preserve dignity and hope for the characters in an unjust society.

  • Dreaming alone, without action, cannot deliver genuine change.

This provides a clear structure for an entire paragraph and sets you up to use a hinge sentence later to develop further layers—e.g., how this ’dreaming” can also reveal internalised colonial ideals or delay confrontation with injustice. By planning your paragraph in this way, you’ll ensure:

  • Specific and sophisticated analysis

  • Strong authorial intent

  • Complex understanding of the characters’ motivations and Harrison’s critique of assimilationist policies in 1950s Australia
