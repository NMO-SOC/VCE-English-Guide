Theme Analysis

Love and Belonging

Harrison explores love and belonging in rainbows end, with Nan and Gladys creating a warm, loving home for Dolly. When Errol offers Dolly a “better life” in the city with a “real home,” Dolly is taken aback by his naivety to her culture. Even though the humpy had its downside, Dolly acknowledged that it’s the fierce love and loyalty that made living there easier.

Nan. Sometimes, you have to move on. Leave things behind. Even things you love. (P.187) Dolly. No matter how they come into the word, you still love’em the same. (P.186) Errol. What matters is [her] / not [her] address (p. 149). Nan. Oh, for heaven’s sake, Dolly marry that boy, before someone else does. (P.198)

Leadership

Leadership is explored through Gladys’ speech where she took Papa Dears place at the petition. Harrison displays not only the leadership in the family dynamic but also the government taking leadership over the indigenous Australians and believing that they know what is best for them and displaying leadership in a very different way. This is displayed by the Inspector as being the character showing the government in ‘Rainbow’s End’.

Inspector: “but someone must take leadership” Gladys: “I will present the petition” Gladys: “She needs to know the world is bigger than just this.” (pg 131) Gladys: “I’m not an interloper- I belong here –this is my land!” Gladys: “We’ve been humble for too long.” Gladys: “I make the decisions regarding Dolores, thank you.” Nan Dear: “Grab the flour, Glad, and the tea.” Gladys: “Oi, Re the so-called ‘housing problem’, it is only a problem because of us Aboriginals-” Nan Dear: “I don’t care what you think! You and your visiting hours. Your rules. No singalongs after dark. Your spying. You, mister, can go to blazes! I’ll give you ‘one’...” Gladys: “We demand to be heard.”

The Intersection of Class and Race

- The intersection of race and class in Rainbow’s End is exemplified by the dynamic between the white population and the Indigenous population. This relationship is explored in scenarios such as Dolly’s search for job, in which it is seen as unlikely she will be able to receive employment. This creates the inescapable cycle that is forged when race meets class, that to escape a lower class, it’s required to achieve a job that could be considered higher status to society. Race hinders this progress due to the prejudices and barriers held against Indigenous people. - The inspector’s view in regard to the flood, as he seems to have no awareness of the effect this will take upon first nations families, particularly the Dear family. - Gladys having to save every penny to afford the encyclopedias just to invest in the cultural capital. - Profit from proximity to whiteness (town Aboriginals) - Government policies Inspector: “property can be replaced” “I’d kind of like to be a nurse”- Dolly, which she believes to be silly. Types of Knowledge and Ways of Knowing

Much of the family’s energy is expended on searching for access to the institutions of the white majority. Gladys never had time for learning, to busy to earn a living to have time to make up for the education that she was denied when she was removed from her home. Gladys has knowledge that far eclipses that of the ‘encyclops boy’. Harrison addresses the sparce of the colonial model of education and the aboriginal knowledge and ways of learning have faced since colonisation.
