Scene Summaries and Analyses

Scene 1 : The Corpse (0:00:0 - 0:02:38)

Summary : Opening Credits; a body is discovered in the pool of a Hollywood Mansion. 1. The opening music and the long shot of the road in the film contribute to creating a mood of dramatic, lonely, mystery, tension, apprehension, terrified and shocked. 2. When the voice-over explains that the ‘poor dope’ always wanted a pool, the price ‘turned out to be too high’, the purpose of this voice-over to refer to the main character Joe Gillis, the outcome of his desires and decision to the dark side of Hollywood. Other answer : The voice meant that the man always wanted a pod. But ironically he ended up dead in it, the price he paid was life. 3. Joe Gillis voice-over is a key storytelling tool. It not only leads us through the film’s scenes but also gives us a deeper focus into Joe’s character, his challenging, his struggling and the tough realities he encounters in the competitive Hollywood environment.’ Other answer : We learn that the narrator is a young man who is nobody important, just a movie script-writer.

Scene 2 : The Corpse (0:02:38 - 0:09:55)

Summary : The finance company threatens to repossess Joe’s car. Joe tries unsuccessfully to seek the job and borrow the money from his friend to save his car from the finance company. 1. My first impressions of Joe Gillis are of a character who is disillusioned and somewhat cynical about Hollywood. He appears to be a struggling screenwriter who feels overlooked and underappreciated by the industry. 2. The entrance gates to Paramount Studios illustrate the attraction and exclusivity of Hollywood. They represent the gateway to fame, success, and the charming world of the movie industry. (represent wealth and power) 3. Schwab’s Pharmacy is a drugstore, serving as a multifaceted symbol of Hollywood’s allure, desire, and the stark contrast between illusion and reality within the film construction. 4. Betty Schaefer’s appraisal of Joe’s script ‘Bases Loaded’ has a particular impact on Joe both personally and professionally and Joe is furious with Betty because the way she said to Joe, it can affect him to seek his job. (being resentful, uncomfortable, frustrated and saying she was just a message kid.)

Scene 3 : A White Elephant of a House (0:09:55 - 0:12:55)

Summary : Joe is pursued by the finance company who wants to obtain his car, he escapes by landing himself in the driveway of some big mansion and storing his car into the huge garage at that mansion. 1. The music score during the car chase supplies a powerful tool for conveying Joe’s emotions, as well as amplifying the suspense, drama, and darkly comedic elements of the film. After he is landing himself in the driveway in a big mansion, the music slows down which shows Joe’s emotion of peace. In addition, the panicky energy of the car chase, the surrounding area in the driveway may provide the risk of serious car accident. 2. Joe narrates about the house that ‘a neglected house gets an unhappy look. This one had it in spades’, it evoked a sense of disquiet and desolation which makes the audience assume that the people who live in this mansion might be struggling with personal or emotional challenges. 3. Joe’s plan to stash the car displays his resourcefulness, street smarts and cautiousness, all of which are essential aspects of his character as he navigates the complexities of life in Hollywood.

Scene 4 : ‘I am big. It’s the picture that got small’ (0:12:55 - 0:17:04)

Summary : Joe is mistaken for walking into this old huge mansion of his curiosity. He meets the isolated former silent film star and recognises her as Norma Desmond. 1. We first see Norma from a low angle shot looking up at her in the window watching Joe, which impresses with power, mystery, weakness, and greatness. 2. Norma’s bedroom is a luxury designed space that serves as a reflection of her past fame, present circumstances, and complex inner world. 3. Wilder productively presents Norma Desmond’s belief that she is still ’ big ’ in Hollywood through a combination of visual storytelling, dialogue, character interactions, and narrative development. 4. Despite their contrasting personalities and conditions, Joe and Norma share common themes and experiences related to their desires, struggles, and illusions in the world of Hollywood.

Scene 5 : The Salome Script (0:17:04 - 0:23:01)

1. Norma comments about her comeback, ‘A return to the millions of people who’ve never forgiven me for destroying the screen’, she means her complicated relationship with fame, legacy, possessiveness and self-perception. 2. After Joe reads through Norma’s script, he views it as outdated, melodramatic, and unsuitable for modern audiences. 3. The close-up on Norma’s face when she suggests Joe stay the night reveals her intricate emotions of loneliness, dependency, desperation and optimism about returning to stardom again. 4. The mise-en-scène in Norma’s salon is accurately crafted to reflect her personality, status, history, mindset and the overall atmosphere of her character’s world.

Scene 6 : An Invitation to Stay (0:23:01 - 0:28:04)

Summary : Joe spends the night in Norma’s garage due to the need to modify Norma’s script, Max takes Joe’s belongings from his apartment as what Norma desires. 1. The burial ceremony for the chimpanzee provides insight into Norma Desmond’s life, highlighting her extravagance, eccentricity, and underlying feelings of loneliness. 2. Norma’s decision to pay Joe’s unpaid rent is driven by a combination of infatuation, manipulation, fear, and a desire for control. 3. Billy Wilder illustrates Norma as the one in control through her artful behaviour, domineering presence, financial influence and emotional manipulation.

Scene 7 : The Face of a Star (0:28:04 - 0:31:47)

Summary : Joe works on Norma’s script. Joe and Norma watch a movie in the living room together about Norma’s silent movie film in which she is a main actor. 1. When Joe attempts to change her script, Norma reacts irritated, terrified and shocked due to Joe removing some scenes which relate to her acting. In addition, Norma wants to have her in all scenes of the movie because she believes that her fans obsess to see her every episode. 2. The panning of the photos of Norma in her mansion serves to illustrate her obsession with her past stardom and her desperate attempt to hold onto her fading fame. 3. The irony in Norma showing the ‘idiot producers’ ’ what a star looks like underlines the themes of delusion, self-confidence, and tragedy. She stopped working at the Paramount studio and she didn’t have a beautiful face anymore like before. 4. Norma’s clutching on Joe’s arm conveys a beginning of relationship emotions, including desire, fear, insecurity, control, and longing.

Norma plays a little bridge game with Norma’s actor friends. She takes Joe to a men’s clothing shop to buy the shirt for Joe as he’s wearing an old suit everyday. 1. The ‘waxworks” were former silent film stars who, like Norma, have fading their careers, delusional, and isolated. 2. The quote that shows Joe is becoming a kept man is ‘He was the kept boy of a now somewhat lady-like but very rich lady, about fifteen years older than he was.’ which Joe said to reflect his own situation. Scene 9 : The Husbands’ Bedroom (0:36:10 - 0:39:31)

Summary : Joe moves into the main house to live in Norma’s ex-husbands room. 1. Joe’s move into ‘the room of the husband’ which indicate various possibilities for the track of their relationship and Joe is increasingly becoming a part of Norma’s destiny. 2. This scene makes us believe that Norma is living in a fantasy world and not facing reality based on the fact that she has moments of sadness which in her mansion doesn’t have locks, sleeping pills, razor blades and shut off the gas in her room. 3. The mise en scene in Norma’s bedroom is carefully crafted to convey themes of longing, decay, and fantasy. In addition, the bedroom represents a place that reflects Norma’s emotion. Satin, drapes, bedding, ruffles, bed like a gilded rowboat (covered in gold), old fashioned, old furniture. Norma’s living in the past. Keep her lavish lifestyle.

Joe escapes Norma after they have an argument between each other about their relationship and Joe returns because when he calls Max to back up his belongings, someone will pick them up, but Max tells him that the doctor has come to Norma’s mansion because Norma wants to kill herself. 1. The gold engraved cigarette case symbolises Norma’s present to Joe for New Year’s eve and about her emotion of love with Joe. 2. New Year’s Eve from Max’s perspective in which he remembers his old love memories with Norma when they were husband and wife. 3. The turning point for Joe is when he returns to the mansion, after Max calls him to tell about Norma wants to murder herself because Joe rejects her love for him, then Joe decides to become Norma’s partner from that night. NOTE: Norma slit her wrists.

Scene 11 : A Kept Man (0:53:50 - 0:58:38)

Summary : Betty is trying to persuade and encourage Joe to collaborate on a screenplay with her, Norma sends Max to ask Joe to come back to the car and Betty wants Joe to work with her in a new story. 1. The interactions between Norma and Joe are characterised by power struggles, complicated emotions and dependency. 2. In this scene, we learn about Betty’s hopefulness, working hard and she wants to be a screenwriter rather than read scripture. In addition, she persuades Joe to work with her in a new movie that she creates by herself. 3. When Norma gives money to Joe to buy cigarettes, the emphasis of Joe doesn’t have enough money to buy cigarettes. Moreover, Joe is dependent on Norma’s wealth and Norma has the power to control Joe.

Scene 12 : The Norma Desmond Follies (0:58:38 - 1:01:24)

Summary : Norma shows a Charlie Chaplin comedy performance to Joe when she suspects that Joe’s getting bored with her and Norma receives a telephone call from the Paramount studio. 1. Norma Desmond entertains Joe Gillis as Charlie Chaplin because she doesn’t want Joe to get bored with her, so she tries to show every comedy’s famous movie to make Joe enjoy living with her and she is scared that Joe will leave her one day. 2. The contrast between Joe when he first came to Hollywood and now is when Joe first arrives at Hollywood, he is hopeful and believes that he can find a great job and be a successful screenwriter. However, until now he becomes disillusioned with Hollywood and lives a life which is dependent on Norma’s wealth.

Scene 13 : Parading to Paramount 9 (1:01:24 - 1:04:42)

Summary : Norma visits Paramount studio after urgent calls from Paramount. 1. The emphasis of the difference between past and present in this scene is the homesickness and romanticism of Hollywood and the harsh realities of ageing and unimportance faced by its former stars. For example, after 20 years, when Norma goes back to Paramount Studio the new generation no one knows her except old people who work with her in that time and also no one in that studio wants her anymore due to her age and obsession. 2. Norma Desmond and Isotta Fraschini share similarities in their representation of faded their famous, delusions of beauty and they both serve as powerful symbols in the past.

Scene 14 : A Meeting with Mr. Demille (1:04:42 - 1:12:48)

Summary : Norma speaks to Mr. Demille about her script and someone calling her, Joe talks with Betty. 1. The high angle shot of Stage 18 at Paramount Studios provides viewers with a perspective of the studio that emphasises its greatness and glory. When the camera is looking down on Norma Desmond it signifies her fading of fame and powerlessness. 2. When Norma says to Demille, ’ And remember, darling. I don’t work before 10 in the morning and never after 4:30 in the afternoon”, she is failing to realise the extent of her own delusion, unreasonableness and the reality of her situation in the modern film industry. 3. In this scene, we learn that Max is loyal and intelligent because he found out the reason for all those phone calls from Paramount, they’re not want Norma but they want Norma’s car and he didn’t tell Norma about it because he is scared that Norma will be furious and disappointed. Moreover, he is also a former employee who worked in Paramount studio as a director. 4. The close-ups of Joe and Betty talking, allow Wilder to express the depth and beginning of their relationship, complicated for them to communicate to each other and the emotional difficulty of their interactions.

Scene 15 : Joe Sneak Out (1:12:48 - 1:17:56)

Summary : Norma is going through a merciless series of treatment. Joe meets Betty Schafer at night to write a screenplay together. 1. Norma’s efforts to prepare for her ‘return’ to the screen are characterised by hopefulness, obsession and she thinks that there are a lot of fans who are waiting for her. 2. Wilder shows the power Norma has over Joe though she doesn’t allow Joe to go anywhere what he wants, when Joe goes somewhere he needs to tell Norma and Joe doesn’t have freedom. She said ’ You went out last night, didn’t you Joe ?”. 3. Joe desires to get free from his dependence on Norma and control over his own life which is the symbol for it being taken away represents his demise. 4. The irony in Joe’s statement ‘That’s the trouble with you reader, you know all the plots.” Joe’s statement carries irony in its criticism of Betty’s understanding of plots while at the same time reflecting his own fault as a writer and his final fall within the imprison of the Hollywood industry.

Scene 16 : Untitled Love Story (1:17:56 - 1:26:19)

Summary : Joe and Betty become closer friends. Max reveals to Joe that he was the first husband of Norma. Norma discovers that Joe and Betty declare they have feelings for each other. 1. Wilder uses the collaborative process of writing a screenplay at night to illustrate the slow growth of Joe and Betty’s relationship, from writers’ partners to close friends and, eventually, romantic interests. 2. Joe and Max have a confrontation about Joe going out every night to write a screenplay with Betty which can make Norma furious and jealous about it. Max, a former husband who still loves her and protects Norma by writing fan letters to Norma and lying to Norma about her comeback to the studio. In addition, when Norma was young, he was one of the directors who made Norma become a famous silent film star and he didn’t want to destroy her. 3. Wilder uses camera angles and framing in the last scene between Joe and Betty to represent their true love and deep connection between each other but they can’t be together due to a lot of complicated drama. Ultimately, Joe and Betty’s relationship can’t continue anymore after Betty knows the truth about Joe living with Norma Desmond.

Scene 17 : Betty’s Exit (1:26:19 - 1:34:35)

Summary : Norma tells the truth about Joe and her living arrangement to Betty. Betty comes to Norma’s mansion to find out about it and Joe introduces/leaves/ Norma’s house to Betty. 1. Joe’s decision to invite Betty Schaefer to Norma Desmond’s mansion shows his growing sense of honesty, responsibility, acceptance of reality and he tells Betty about his situation that he has a good deal in this house and he doesn’t want to go to one room apartment which he can’t pay for and even the screenplay which they write together. 2. The techniques that Wilder uses to show a shift in power and build up of tension is cinematography, lighting, sound design, dialogue, set design, editing and creating a scene rich with dramatic intensity, emotional depth and low angels light camera between Joe and Norma which makes him become more powerful on Norma. 3. Betty’s feelings of curiosity, anxiety, concern for Joe,support him through whatever challenges they may face and she deals with that by always staying on Joe’s side whatever happens to him. 4. Norma’s actions and feelings in this scene is when she calls Betty to tell her the facts about Joe and her relationship and their living arrangement because she is increasingly jealous and scared that Joe will leave her to live with another girl one day who is younger than her. Norma Desmond says ‘men of his sort’, she is likely talking to men who are selfish, cowardly and take advantage of others for their own benefits . 5. It is ironic that Joe turns the lights on in the pool because he wants to tell Betty about the danger, deception and darkness of his life with Norma in her mansion.

Summary : The house is surrounded by the policies, the reporter neighbours and passer-by. Joe’s body is floating in the swimming pool. Norma has a mental shock after she kills Joe. 1. The narrative style adds depth to the storytelling, allowing the audience to empathise with Joe’s experiences while also providing important scenes through which to view the themes of fame, obsession, examining the dark side of Hollywood and disillusionment within the film. 2. Black humour is evident when the voice over says ’ Here was an item everybody could have fun with”, which is referring to Joe’s body floating in the swimming pool who wishes to go into Hollywood, as a result end up his life in the water. 3. Norma won’t come down the stairs until she hears the word ‘camera” Illustrate her deep-emotions of delusions and obsession with her past fame as a silent film star. Norma has become isolated and disconnected from reality, living in a huge mansion filled with memories of her fame days in Hollywood. 4. Norma’s feelings as she walks down the stairs are a mix of pride, hopefulness, insecurity, loneliness, and delusion because she thinks she has come back to her place which she waited for 20 years. 5. Yes, I do. Wilder conveys a sense of pity, shallowness and compassion for Norma Doesmond through inviting the audience to empathise with her challenges and struggles.
