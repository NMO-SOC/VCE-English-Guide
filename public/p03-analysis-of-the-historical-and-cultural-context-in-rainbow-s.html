Analysis of the Historical and Cultural Context in Rainbow’s End

Overview

Jane Harrison’s Rainbow’s End emerges from a landscape shaped by colonisation, segregation, and resilience. Set in 1950s Australia, the play reflects both the harsh realities and quiet triumphs of Aboriginal families living under systemic discrimination. The historical context is indispensable to understanding the characters’ identities, the injustices they face, and the endurance that defines their humanity. Harrison’s work gives voice to the unsung heroes—especially women—who sustained their communities despite persistent marginalisation.

Acknowledgement and Purpose

The play’s thematic foundation begins with an acknowledgment of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples as the traditional custodians of the land. This recognition situates Rainbow’s End within an ethical framework that honours history and memory. Harrison’s intent is not merely to tell a story but to preserve a cultural legacy and challenge erasure. Commissioned to highlight Aboriginal women’s experiences, the play transforms domestic life into political narrative, demonstrating that resistance often begins within the home.

Analysis

By embedding acknowledgment into the narrative fabric, Harrison bridges past and present. The act of remembering becomes both art and activism, inviting audiences to reflect on how storytelling can restore dignity to those history overlooked. Her focus on Aboriginal women redefines heroism as endurance and nurturing in the face of dispossession.

Historical Background: Colonisation and Segregation

The origins of the play’s conflict trace back to the legacy of colonisation and government policies such as the Protection Acts, which sanctioned segregation and the forced removal of children. Aboriginal families were confined to reserves like Kamarra Ganja, where strict management and limited access to resources entrenched poverty. These lived realities form the backbone of Harrison’s narrative world, revealing how state control sought to diminish autonomy while families fought to preserve it.

Analysis

Harrison transforms historical record into lived drama. The reserve system becomes a stage for both oppression and resilience. By drawing from documented injustices, she crafts a narrative that demands empathy and accountability. The audience confronts how legislative cruelty translated into generational trauma.

Life on the Reserves and the Flats

After the walk-off from Kamarra Ganja, many Aboriginal families relocated to makeshift settlements known as “the flats.” These spaces, though impoverished, became hubs of culture, laughter, and communal strength. The families’ persistence in maintaining cultural traditions symbolises defiance against systemic neglect. In this environment, children learned resilience as a form of inheritance, and hope became both a necessity and an act of protest.

Analysis

The depiction of life on the flats captures a paradox — material deprivation coexists with emotional and cultural richness. Harrison celebrates community cohesion as a radical response to marginalisation. The domestic setting transforms into a microcosm of resistance where identity is preserved through humour, storytelling, and maternal leadership.

Government Policy and the Metaphor of the Rainbow

The government housing schemes of the 1950s, such as those in Rumbalara, promised integration but perpetuated segregation through inadequate living conditions. The metaphor of the rainbow recurs as a powerful symbol throughout the play. To the characters, the “end of the rainbow” represents dreams of equality and acceptance — visions that remain just out of reach. This unattainable horizon mirrors the historical struggle for recognition endured by Aboriginal Australians.

Analysis

The rainbow encapsulates the duality of hope and despair. It is both a dreamscape and a mirage — a reflection of the distance between aspiration and reality. Harrison employs this image to expose the cruel optimism embedded within promises of assimilation. The characters’ pursuit of the rainbow becomes a metaphor for the broader national pursuit of reconciliation still incomplete.

The 1950s Social Landscape

Post-war Australia was marked by conservative values, economic expansion, and racial inequality. For Aboriginal peoples, this period intensified the contradictions between visibility and exclusion. Women, in particular, bore the burden of navigating these intersecting oppressions. Through Gladys, Dolly, and Nan Dear, Harrison portrays how Aboriginal women negotiated identity, motherhood, and dignity within a society determined to silence them.

Analysis

Harrison’s representation of 1950s Aboriginal womanhood dismantles stereotypes of passivity. Her characters inhabit a world of limited choices yet wield moral and emotional power. Their courage lies not in grand rebellion but in persistence — in cleaning, teaching, loving, and speaking truths that white Australia refused to hear.

Conclusion

Understanding the historical and cultural context of Rainbow’s End enriches every reading of the play. Harrison’s depiction of segregation, displacement, and resilience transforms history into human experience. The struggles of the Banks and Dear families are not isolated but emblematic of a broader narrative of survival. The play stands as both testimony and challenge: to remember, to acknowledge, and to act. In recognising the truths of the past, Harrison invites audiences to see the enduring strength of Aboriginal identity — not as tragedy, but as triumph.

Structure of the Play

Rainbow’s End opens in medias res, plunging the audience directly into the aftermath of a flood. This sudden beginning creates an atmosphere of crisis and displacement, immediately reflecting the instability that defines the characters’ world. The play’s structure is unconventional—beginning with a prologue, followed by scenes divided into parts A and B, reflecting fragmentation and lack of control in the characters’ lives. Harrison integrates dream sequences that blur the boundary between fantasy and reality. These imaginative interludes, such as Gladys’s visions of meeting the Queen or Dolly’s aspirations for education, express both hope and futility. The juxtaposition of dreams and deprivation highlights the gap between aspiration and circumstance. Two narrative threads underpin the play: Gladys’s political awakening and Dolly and Errol’s romance. Each culminates in an epiphany—Gladys’s courageous confrontation with the Shire Council and Dolly’s recognition of Errol’s unconscious racial assumptions. The play concludes with two significant symbols: Nan’s dream of Dolly’s wedding and the song “Que Sera, Sera.” The latter, appearing at both the beginning and end, transforms from a cynical reflection of government indifference into a subtle anthem of hope and change. Harrison’s structural choices mirror her thematic concerns. The fragmented narrative echoes the disrupted lives of Aboriginal families, while the interweaving of dreams and realism underscores the endurance of hope amid systemic oppression. The dual climaxes—Gladys’s activism and Dolly’s assertion of independence—affirm the possibility of transformation both personal and political. The cyclical use of “Que Sera, Sera” redefines fatalism as faith: a belief that the future, though uncertain, can still hold renewal.
