Values in Sunset Boulevard

Social Values

Traditional Gender Roles

The era upheld traditional gender roles, with men as breadwinners and women primarily responsible for homemaking and child-raising. The suburban housewife became an iconic image of the time. Norma’s character highlights the fading role of ageing actresses in Hollywood, mirroring the limited opportunities for women beyond traditional roles as wives and mothers.

Consumerism

The 1950s were characterised by a post-war economic boom, and there was a strong emphasis on consumerism. Owning a home, car, and various consumer goods became aspirational goals. The opulent mansion on Sunset Boulevard and Norma’s extravagant lifestyle represent a distorted version of the consumerist dream, where wealth and possessions mask deeper issues.

Cultural Values

Hollywood’s Golden Age

Hollywood’s Golden Age is a term used to describe a period in the history of the American film industry that roughly spans from the late 1920s to the early 1960s. It was a time of unprecedented creativity, innovation, and commercial success in Hollywood. The Studio System – a way of making movies where big companies, like Warner Bros. and Paramount, had total control over everything. They decided which actors to use, what movies to make, and even how the movies were shown in theatres.

Technological Advancements

This era witnessed significant advancements in filmmaking technology, including the transition from silent films to ‘talkies’, the development of colour cinematography, and the use of widescreen formats.

The Star System

The method of creating, promoting and exploiting stars in Hollywood films. Movie studios would select promising young actors and glamourise them through various promotion tactics.
