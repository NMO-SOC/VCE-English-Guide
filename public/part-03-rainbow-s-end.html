Introduction and Context

Author Background

Jane Harrison, a descendant of the Muruwari people, is an acclaimed playwright, novelist, and critic. As a child, she “always loved books” but was disheartened by the absence of Aboriginal representation in Australian literature. In writing Rainbow’s End, Harrison sought to give voice to those experiences that had long been silenced or marginalised. Drawing upon oral histories from Aboriginal Elders, she captures the resilience and humour of families who lived on “The Flats” and later at Rumbalara—the First Nations housing estate in Mooroopna.
Harrison’s work strongly condemns the substandard housing and systemic neglect endured by First Nations families during the 1950s. Through her writing, she positions herself as a conduit for truth-telling, giving dramatic expression to both the hardships and triumphs of Aboriginal life. Her aim, as she has stated, is to use fiction as “an important way for First Nations people to have a voice.”

Analysis

Harrison’s authorial perspective intertwines personal heritage and political conviction. By dramatizing the everyday realities of Aboriginal families, she reclaims history from the margins and rehumanises voices often reduced to statistics or stereotypes. Her compassionate yet incisive storytelling challenges audiences to confront both the past and its lingering presence in modern Australia.

Historical Context

The colonisation of Australia, beginning with the arrival of the First Fleet in 1788, unleashed devastating consequences for First Nations peoples. The ensuing Frontier Wars—violent conflicts between Aboriginal people and British settlers—resulted in the massacre and displacement of countless Indigenous communities. Throughout much of the twentieth century, government policy continued to pursue assimilation and cultural eradication.
One of the most tragic outcomes of these policies was the forced removal of Aboriginal children from their families. These children, later known as the Stolen Generations, were stripped of their language, names, and cultural identity. Many were placed in institutions or adopted into white families, where they were exploited as unpaid labour and suffered emotional and physical abuse.
In Rainbow’s End, Harrison threads this history through the lives of Nan and Gladys, whose trauma reflects the generational wounds inflicted by colonial intervention. Gladys’s experience of being taken from her family and made to work as a domestic servant mirrors the broader policy of forced assimilation. Her obsession with Dolly’s education becomes a personal act of restitution—an attempt to reclaim what was taken from her by systemic injustice.

Analysis

By embedding the historical trauma of dispossession within intimate family narratives, Harrison compels her audience to recognise the enduring emotional and social consequences of colonisation. Her portrayal of the Stolen Generations not only condemns past wrongs but also foregrounds the resilience and agency of those who survived them.

The Flats and Cummeragunja

Cummeragunja, founded in 1888 on Yorta Yorta/Bangerang Country, represented both refuge and resistance for Aboriginal people. Initially a self-sufficient farming community, it was later undermined by the New South Wales Protection Board, which seized control in 1915. Aboriginal workers were paid in meagre rations, and funds generated by their labour were appropriated by the Board. By the 1930s, disease and poverty had decimated the community.
In 1939, the residents of Cummeragunja staged a historic protest—the first mass strike by Aboriginal Australians. Many left the mission and settled on “The Flats,” a floodplain near Mooroopna and Shepparton. Despite harsh living conditions, including humpies made from “flattened kero tins” and exposure to extreme weather, the community retained its cultural vitality and kinship ties. By the early 1950s, around 300 people lived there.

Analysis

The Flats serve as both a literal and symbolic setting in Rainbow’s End. They embody dispossession yet also community, poverty yet perseverance. Through this setting, Harrison contrasts the instability imposed by colonial structures with the cultural rootedness of Aboriginal families. The Flats become a testament to the capacity of a people to adapt, resist, and endure despite systemic neglect.

Analysis of the Historical and Cultural Context in Rainbow’s End

Overview

Jane Harrison’s Rainbow’s End emerges from a landscape shaped by colonisation, segregation, and resilience. Set in 1950s Australia, the play reflects both the harsh realities and quiet triumphs of Aboriginal families living under systemic discrimination. The historical context is indispensable to understanding the characters’ identities, the injustices they face, and the endurance that defines their humanity. Harrison’s work gives voice to the unsung heroes—especially women—who sustained their communities despite persistent marginalisation.

Acknowledgement and Purpose

The play’s thematic foundation begins with an acknowledgment of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples as the traditional custodians of the land. This recognition situates Rainbow’s End within an ethical framework that honours history and memory. Harrison’s intent is not merely to tell a story but to preserve a cultural legacy and challenge erasure. Commissioned to highlight Aboriginal women’s experiences, the play transforms domestic life into political narrative, demonstrating that resistance often begins within the home.

Analysis

By embedding acknowledgment into the narrative fabric, Harrison bridges past and present. The act of remembering becomes both art and activism, inviting audiences to reflect on how storytelling can restore dignity to those history overlooked. Her focus on Aboriginal women redefines heroism as endurance and nurturing in the face of dispossession.

Historical Background: Colonisation and Segregation

The origins of the play’s conflict trace back to the legacy of colonisation and government policies such as the Protection Acts, which sanctioned segregation and the forced removal of children. Aboriginal families were confined to reserves like Kamarra Ganja, where strict management and limited access to resources entrenched poverty. These lived realities form the backbone of Harrison’s narrative world, revealing how state control sought to diminish autonomy while families fought to preserve it.

Analysis

Harrison transforms historical record into lived drama. The reserve system becomes a stage for both oppression and resilience. By drawing from documented injustices, she crafts a narrative that demands empathy and accountability. The audience confronts how legislative cruelty translated into generational trauma.

Life on the Reserves and the Flats

After the walk-off from Kamarra Ganja, many Aboriginal families relocated to makeshift settlements known as “the flats.” These spaces, though impoverished, became hubs of culture, laughter, and communal strength. The families’ persistence in maintaining cultural traditions symbolises defiance against systemic neglect. In this environment, children learned resilience as a form of inheritance, and hope became both a necessity and an act of protest.

Analysis

The depiction of life on the flats captures a paradox — material deprivation coexists with emotional and cultural richness. Harrison celebrates community cohesion as a radical response to marginalisation. The domestic setting transforms into a microcosm of resistance where identity is preserved through humour, storytelling, and maternal leadership.

Government Policy and the Metaphor of the Rainbow

The government housing schemes of the 1950s, such as those in Rumbalara, promised integration but perpetuated segregation through inadequate living conditions. The metaphor of the rainbow recurs as a powerful symbol throughout the play. To the characters, the “end of the rainbow” represents dreams of equality and acceptance — visions that remain just out of reach. This unattainable horizon mirrors the historical struggle for recognition endured by Aboriginal Australians.

Analysis

The rainbow encapsulates the duality of hope and despair. It is both a dreamscape and a mirage — a reflection of the distance between aspiration and reality. Harrison employs this image to expose the cruel optimism embedded within promises of assimilation. The characters’ pursuit of the rainbow becomes a metaphor for the broader national pursuit of reconciliation still incomplete.

The 1950s Social Landscape

Post-war Australia was marked by conservative values, economic expansion, and racial inequality. For Aboriginal peoples, this period intensified the contradictions between visibility and exclusion. Women, in particular, bore the burden of navigating these intersecting oppressions. Through Gladys, Dolly, and Nan Dear, Harrison portrays how Aboriginal women negotiated identity, motherhood, and dignity within a society determined to silence them.

Analysis

Harrison’s representation of 1950s Aboriginal womanhood dismantles stereotypes of passivity. Her characters inhabit a world of limited choices yet wield moral and emotional power. Their courage lies not in grand rebellion but in persistence — in cleaning, teaching, loving, and speaking truths that white Australia refused to hear.

Conclusion

Understanding the historical and cultural context of Rainbow’s End enriches every reading of the play. Harrison’s depiction of segregation, displacement, and resilience transforms history into human experience. The struggles of the Banks and Dear families are not isolated but emblematic of a broader narrative of survival. The play stands as both testimony and challenge: to remember, to acknowledge, and to act. In recognising the truths of the past, Harrison invites audiences to see the enduring strength of Aboriginal identity — not as tragedy, but as triumph.

Structure of the Play

Rainbow’s End opens in medias res, plunging the audience directly into the aftermath of a flood. This sudden beginning creates an atmosphere of crisis and displacement, immediately reflecting the instability that defines the characters’ world. The play’s structure is unconventional—beginning with a prologue, followed by scenes divided into parts A and B, reflecting fragmentation and lack of control in the characters’ lives.
Harrison integrates dream sequences that blur the boundary between fantasy and reality. These imaginative interludes, such as Gladys’s visions of meeting the Queen or Dolly’s aspirations for education, express both hope and futility. The juxtaposition of dreams and deprivation highlights the gap between aspiration and circumstance.
Two narrative threads underpin the play: Gladys’s political awakening and Dolly and Errol’s romance. Each culminates in an epiphany—Gladys’s courageous confrontation with the Shire Council and Dolly’s recognition of Errol’s unconscious racial assumptions. The play concludes with two significant symbols: Nan’s dream of Dolly’s wedding and the song “Que Sera, Sera.” The latter, appearing at both the beginning and end, transforms from a cynical reflection of government indifference into a subtle anthem of hope and change.
Harrison’s structural choices mirror her thematic concerns. The fragmented narrative echoes the disrupted lives of Aboriginal families, while the interweaving of dreams and realism underscores the endurance of hope amid systemic oppression. The dual climaxes—Gladys’s activism and Dolly’s assertion of independence—affirm the possibility of transformation both personal and political. The cyclical use of “Que Sera, Sera” redefines fatalism as faith: a belief that the future, though uncertain, can still hold renewal.

Structure and Themes in Rainbow’s End

Overview

Jane Harrison’s Rainbow’s End is constructed as a two-act play divided into short, interlinked scenes that illuminate the resilience, struggles, and dreams of First Nations families in 1950s Australia. The fragmented structure mirrors the fractured social and political realities of Aboriginal life, where moments of humour and hope coexist with hardship and prejudice. Harrison’s manipulation of stage direction, song, and dialogue builds dramatic tension and emotional intimacy, guiding the audience through a tapestry of generational experience.

Act Structure and Thematic Design

Unlike traditional plays divided by chapters, Rainbow’s End unfolds through acts and scenes that offer glimpses into domestic and emotional moments. This episodic format reflects the uncertainty that governs the Dear and Banks families’ lives. Scenes often end abruptly or transition during moments of crisis, symbolising the instability caused by poverty and discrimination. The two major narrative threads — Gladys’s political awakening and Dolly’s coming-of-age romance — develop in parallel, converging at the climax where both women redefine their strength and identity.

Analysis

Harrison’s two-act structure underscores her social commentary. The brevity and rhythm of scenes intensify audience empathy while exposing the invisible pressures imposed by systemic inequality. By structuring the play around fragmented lives, Harrison creates a dramatic form that mirrors cultural and emotional dislocation — yet also reveals endurance and connection within the Aboriginal matriarchal family.

In Media Res and Dramatic Openings

The play begins in media res amid the chaos of a flood, a moment both literal and symbolic. Accompanied by the recurring song “Que Sera, Sera,” the opening sequence immediately juxtaposes hope with helplessness. This song returns at the play’s conclusion, binding the narrative through a motif that evokes the tension between aspiration and acceptance.

Analysis

Beginning in the middle of action thrusts the audience into the characters’ reality without exposition, reflecting the immediacy of their struggle for survival. The flood motif encapsulates emotional and social inundation, while the musical refrain serves as both comfort and irony — a lullaby masking pain.

Dream Sequences and Juxtaposition

Dream sequences punctuate the play, revealing the characters’ inner lives and unfulfilled desires. Gladys’s fantasy of meeting the Queen becomes a recurring image of colonial aspiration — an impossible vision shaped by internalised subservience and social exclusion. These dreams contrast sharply with the family’s reality in a riverside shack, where dignity must be defended daily.

Analysis

Through dream imagery, Harrison critiques the illusion of social mobility within a racist society. The juxtaposition of fantasy and deprivation exposes the psychological toll of colonisation, while simultaneously affirming the imagination as a site of survival and self-worth.

Character Growth and Narrative Resolution

Both major storylines reach their culmination in acts of self-realisation. Dolly challenges her mother’s fixation on external validation, forcing Gladys to confront the limits of her own deference. This confrontation propels Gladys toward activism, culminating in her powerful declaration at the Shire meeting: “We demand proper schooling … The white people too — they need to be educated about us” (p.197). Though the play ends with personal growth, systemic barriers remain unresolved, reflecting the persistence of structural inequality.

Analysis

The dual transformation of Gladys and Dolly demonstrates Harrison’s belief in intergenerational empowerment. Their evolution symbolises a shift from private endurance to public assertion — a necessary transition for community progress. The incomplete resolution emphasises realism over idealism, reinforcing that the struggle for equality continues beyond the stage.

Dramatic Intensity and Unseen Forces

Harrison’s use of short, tightly constructed scenes sustains dramatic tension and emphasises the relentlessness of external pressures. Many of the most significant events occur offstage, their impact conveyed through dialogue and reaction. The small cast heightens intimacy while unseen antagonistic figures — landlords, officials, and employers — personify the faceless nature of institutional discrimination.

Analysis

By keeping oppression unseen yet omnipresent, Harrison amplifies its psychological weight. The absence of visible villains shifts focus toward resilience, love, and humour as tools of survival. The minimal staging and interwoven dialogue allow audiences to inhabit the emotional pulse of the characters’ world, where silence often speaks louder than confrontation.

Conclusion

The play’s structure functions as both narrative device and political metaphor. Through acts, scenes, dreams, and songs, Harrison crafts a world where fragmented experiences reveal collective truth. The rhythm of the play mirrors the cycles of struggle and renewal within Aboriginal communities, inviting readers and audiences to engage with the ongoing dialogue between past injustices and present resilience. Understanding the form of Rainbow’s End thus deepens appreciation for its portrayal of identity, endurance, and hope in mid-twentieth-century Australia.

Audience and Purpose in Rainbow’s End

Overview

Jane Harrison’s Rainbow’s End (2005) was written to educate and engage both Indigenous and non-Indigenous audiences through its portrayal of three generations of Aboriginal women — Nan Dear, Gladys, and Dolly — living in 1950s Victoria. Harrison’s purpose extends beyond recounting hardship; she celebrates resilience, dignity, and familial love amidst oppressive social structures. The play invites audiences to confront the consequences of colonisation and assimilation while recognising the strength of community and cultural continuity.

Purpose of the Play

Harrison’s central purpose is to foreground the untold stories of Aboriginal women and their everyday heroism. Through the intimate domestic setting of the Dear and Banks family, she challenges the invisibility imposed upon First Nations peoples by historical narratives. Her play functions as both cultural preservation and political statement — illustrating that Indigenous survival is not defined by victimhood but by persistence and pride.
Harrison’s writing embodies the principle that art can both memorialise and mobilise. By humanising historical trauma, she transforms policy and prejudice into personal experience, allowing audiences to empathise rather than merely observe. The play’s tone oscillates between warmth and sorrow, comedy and critique, mirroring the emotional complexity of survival.

Analysis

Through Rainbow’s End, Harrison asserts that storytelling is a mode of reclamation. Each scene restores voices historically silenced by government policy and social discrimination. Her purpose is not only to depict resilience but to invite truth-telling — to reposition Aboriginal women as custodians of memory, agency, and identity.

Intended Audience

Rainbow’s End addresses a dual audience. For non-Indigenous viewers, it acts as an awakening — revealing the lived experiences obscured by whitewashed Australian history. For Indigenous audiences, it affirms continuity and pride, validating intergenerational strength and the endurance of culture.
By positioning humour and tenderness alongside hardship, Harrison ensures accessibility for classroom and theatre audiences alike. The play encourages educational engagement, prompting critical reflection among students while offering Aboriginal spectators the comfort of recognition. The inclusion of intergenerational female perspectives fosters empathy across age groups and cultural divides.

Analysis

Harrison’s nuanced approach ensures that the play operates on multiple emotional and intellectual levels. Non-Indigenous audiences are confronted with complicity in structural injustice, while Indigenous audiences are offered representation that transcends trauma. This balance of challenge and affirmation defines the play’s enduring impact within Australian theatre and education.

Historical Awareness and Cultural Responsibility

The play’s authenticity stems from Harrison’s meticulous research and personal connection to her Murrawari heritage. Drawing upon oral histories, newspaper archives, and community interviews, she constructs a historically grounded yet emotionally resonant narrative. Her inclusion of real figures, such as Pastor Doug Nichols, situates the fictional world within an authentic cultural framework.
This fidelity to truth underscores Harrison’s responsibility as both playwright and cultural custodian. Her depiction of the 1950s assimilation era reflects an ethical commitment to remembrance — to ensuring that audiences understand the policies that fractured families and communities, and the resilience that rebuilt them.

Analysis

Harrison’s creative purpose merges documentation with dramatisation. She transforms history into a living dialogue between past and present, ensuring that Aboriginal experience is neither mythologised nor forgotten. By embedding historical authenticity within intimate storytelling, she teaches audiences that understanding cultural identity begins with acknowledging historical reality.

The Role of Representation in Theatre

For Harrison, representation is activism. The play reclaims space within the Australian canon for Aboriginal voices, particularly those of women. Through humour, vernacular speech, and emotional candour, she dismantles stereotypes and restores humanity to communities too often reduced to statistics. Rainbow’s End thereby becomes a vessel for truth-telling, reconciliation, and education.

Analysis

Representation in Rainbow’s End is not symbolic but lived — an act of cultural assertion. Harrison’s purpose is fulfilled when audiences see Aboriginal women not as passive subjects of history but as authors of their own narratives. The play’s enduring presence in school curricula ensures that these voices continue to educate, inspire, and transform generations of Australians.

Conclusion

Harrison’s Rainbow’s End unites purpose and audience in a dialogue of empathy, education, and recognition. Her portrayal of three Aboriginal women transcends their fictional identities to stand as symbols of cultural endurance. By crafting a story grounded in truth and delivered with compassion, Harrison reshapes the Australian stage into a site of reconciliation — where understanding begins through listening, and hope endures through storytelling.

Language in Rainbow’s End

Overview

Language in Jane Harrison’s Rainbow’s End is a powerful vehicle for expressing identity, class, and cultural conflict. The characters’ speech patterns reveal their personal histories and social positions, while Harrison’s authorial voice is embedded within their dialogue, reflecting her own commentary on racial inequality, resilience, and generational change. Language, therefore, functions not only as a mode of communication but also as an indicator of power, prejudice, and pride within the 1950s Australian context.

Nan Dear

Nan’s language is colloquial, humorous, and often sharply ironic. When she informs Gladys that she “won’t be needing any of them encyclops” (p.136), she dismisses education’s relevance to her family’s daily struggles, revealing both her pragmatic worldview and the limited opportunities afforded to her generation. Her unpolished speech reflects her lower-class status and lack of formal education—an outcome of systemic exclusion faced by Aboriginal Australians.
Yet Nan’s language also injects comedy into the play. Her dry humour and literal phrasing create moments of levity that highlight resilience in hardship. When mocking Gladys’s excitement about meeting the Queen, Nan’s irony underscores the absurdity of colonial class structures. Through Nan’s speech, Harrison reveals both the pain and pride of a woman grounded in survival, whose humour becomes an act of quiet rebellion against social inequality.

Analysis

Nan’s dialect situates her within a marginalised social class while asserting authenticity and self-respect. Her vernacular speech becomes an emblem of identity, opposing assimilationist pressures that sought to erase Aboriginal voice. Harrison uses Nan’s language to celebrate oral tradition and defiant humour, challenging notions that education alone defines intelligence.

Dolly Banks

Dolly’s language reflects her education and confidence but also her awareness of the social hierarchies constraining her. She is direct and assertive, particularly when confronting racism. Her rebuke of Errol’s remark about “you people” (p.141) exposes his unconscious bias and the normalisation of racial division. Likewise, her challenge to her mother — “You’re always telling me to stick up for myself, but when do you, eh?” (p.162) — forces Gladys to examine her internalised inferiority.
Dolly also code-switches, adopting Indigenous vernacular when she threatens to “kick Ester’s boys up to moon” (p.137), using humour and familiarity to assert belonging within her community. Her fluency, both in educated English and colloquial Aboriginal dialect, embodies her dual identity as a bridge between two worlds. Her closing assertion to Errol — that she would never trade her family or heritage for a “better life” (p.172) — epitomises pride and defiance.

Analysis

Dolly’s language signifies empowerment through articulation. Harrison crafts her speech to demonstrate that mastery of language is not assimilation but assertion. Dolly’s ability to speak for herself—and against injustice—makes her a vehicle for Harrison’s advocacy of Indigenous self-determination. Her linguistic versatility becomes symbolic of cultural strength rather than concession.

Errol Fisher

Errol’s halting and awkward speech reflects both his nervousness and his ignorance. As an encyclopedia salesman who depends on persuasion, his incompetence is ironically comic. When he awkwardly notes that Dolly’s glass is “made out of a can” (p.140), he unwittingly insults her. His language often reveals class and racial privilege cloaked in politeness — exemplified in his use of “you people” (p.140) to generalise Aboriginal Australians.
His verbal missteps and tone-deaf expressions highlight his sheltered upbringing and lack of cultural awareness. Over time, however, Errol’s language softens and becomes more considerate, mirroring his emotional growth and respect for Dolly’s world.

Analysis

Harrison uses Errol’s speech to critique the well-meaning but ignorant paternalism of mid-century white Australia. His progression from patronising to empathetic reflects the broader social journey toward understanding and equality. His linguistic awkwardness thus serves as a tool of both comedy and commentary.

Gladys Banks

Gladys’s voice develops from timid dreamer to assertive activist. During the Rodney Shire meeting, her passionate declaration — “We demand proper schooling … The white people too — they need to be educated about us, and our ways” (p.197) — becomes a turning point where her speech echoes Harrison’s own political message.
Her earlier language is often infused with fantasy and aspiration: daydreams of the Queen’s attention or Dolly’s graduation soften her struggles. Yet by the play’s end, Gladys has found a political and moral voice that embodies the collective frustrations of Indigenous Australians denied dignity. Her transition from domestic to public speech represents empowerment through language.

Analysis

Through Gladys, Harrison demonstrates the transformative power of speech as activism. The act of “speaking up” replaces passive dreaming with agency. Gladys’s voice becomes that of her people — articulate, righteous, and unafraid — asserting the necessity for visibility and reform.

The Royals

The Queen’s language is formal, elevated, and exclusionary. Her reference to Australia as “the birthplace of the nation” (p.124) erases 65,000 years of Aboriginal civilisation, while her statement about standing “at last among Australians” implicitly refers only to white citizens. This polished rhetoric embodies colonial arrogance.
By contrast, the Duke’s language is more spontaneous and unguarded. His offhand question — “Where’s Father?” (p.127) — humanises him but also underscores the royal couple’s detachment from the lived realities of those they visit.

Analysis

Harrison employs the Royals’ diction as social critique. Their words — ceremonious, detached, and self-congratulatory — expose the gulf between imperial symbolism and the Indigenous suffering it conceals. The Queen’s non-inclusive language mirrors the nation’s non-inclusive identity at mid-century.

The Radio Announcer

The radio announcer’s language is superficially cheerful yet profoundly condescending. In describing the royal visit, he mentions a demonstration of “how to throw a boomerang” and marvels that it “really does come back” (p.128). His tone trivialises a complex cultural artefact, reducing Indigenous skill to entertainment for the royal couple.

Analysis

The announcer’s commentary encapsulates institutional racism disguised as enthusiasm. Harrison’s use of irony highlights how mainstream media perpetuated ignorance, framing Aboriginal culture as novelty rather than heritage. His words parody the way national pride excluded First Nations people.

Structural and Symbolic Language

Beyond dialogue, Harrison uses language in her stage directions and symbolism to reinforce meaning. Descriptions of the Dear family’s humpy — “waterlogged” and “mud-spattered” (pp.123, 176) — visually communicate poverty and marginalisation. The title itself, Rainbow’s End, draws on the myth of a pot of gold, suggesting the illusion of prosperity and the unattainability of a promised better life.
The reference to “Somewhere Over the Rainbow” from The Wizard of Oz introduces an ironic hopefulness. Its lyrics — “the dreams that you dare to dream really do come true” — echo the family’s optimism but also question the realism of those dreams in a society unwilling to grant equality.

Analysis

Harrison’s manipulation of both literal and figurative language constructs a layered critique of Australia’s racial landscape. The interplay between hope and futility in her metaphors underscores the central tension of the play — between aspiration and limitation, imagination and reality. Language, whether spoken, written, or symbolic, thus becomes both a weapon and a wound.

Character Analysis

Dolly Banks

Dolly is the teenage daughter of Gladys and granddaughter of Nan Dear. Throughout the play, she serves as a receptacle for her mother’s dreams and her grandmother’s fears. Gladys fantasies often about what a better life might look like, but she doesn’t believe that such a life is possible for her. Instead, she dreams on Dolly’s behalf, and she tries to encourage Dolly to pursue goals that seem impossible, like getting a job at a bank that does not want to hire Aboriginal employees. Nan Dear, meanwhile, views Dolly as vulnerable to the same discrimination and violence that Nan Dear and Gladys have experienced. She is especially wary when Dolly begins seeing Errol, a White boy. Dolly’s relationship with Errol is complicated by Errol’s unconscious biases, and she temporarily breaks things off with him when he is unable to put aside his assumption that White middle-class life is superior to the more community-based life Dolly wants. Her relationship with Errol also enrages Dolly’s cousin Leon, who sexually assaults her and leaves her pregnant. Despite the violent origin of the pregnancy, Dolly loves her baby and names it after Papa Dear, cementing baby Regina’s status as a beloved member of the family included in the traditions and legacy it carries.

Analysis

Dolly Banks is a crucial character in Jane Harrison’s play "Rainbow’s End," capturing the experiences of a young Aboriginal woman during the 1950s, a time rife with changes and challenges for her community. The daughter of Gladys Banks and the granddaughter of Nan Dear, Dolly is a character who embodies the bridge between the prior generation’s traditional views and the new generation’s aspirations.
Throughout the play, Dolly asserts her desires and individuality in a society that would typically dismiss them. In one scene, she interacts with Errol, a character symbolising the broader society’s views, displaying her self-assured yet personable character. When Errol calls her "Miss Banks," she promptly corrects him, saying, "Dolly. We’re not too fussed about fancy titles," indicating both her lack of pretense. Errol complements her name, but once he learns her full name is Dolores, Dolly momentarily moves away, reflecting a subtle consciousness of boundaries and self-respect as she navigates her interactions with a non-Aboriginal man.
Dolly’s aspirations and accomplishments are a point of pride for her mother, Gladys, who notes that Dolly is the first in the family to complete her Leaving Certificate, a testament to her intelligence and drive. The circumstances in which this achievement is presented, however, bring to light the stark realities of racial prejudices they confront. Gladys holds a photo of Dolly, which prompts the bank manager to acknowledge Dolly’s physical appearance but does not immediately understand the reason for Gladys’s visit. The scene showcases

Dolly through her mother’s eyes—as capable and promising— and reveals the obstacles that she would face in achieving her potential in a society that does not readily afford opportunities to Aboriginal people.
Dolly’s character arc within the play reveals the nuanced complexities of identity, as she must balance the expectations of her cultural heritage with her own personal ambitions in a society that often sees her and her community as lesser. Her actions and decisions throughout the play demonstrate the younger generation’s determination to both honour their heritage and pursue a future they define for themselves. Through Dolly, Harrison presents themes of cultural identity, generational change, and the possibilities that lie ahead for Aboriginal women in a changing world.

Gladys Banks

Gladys is Nan Dear’s middle-aged daughter and Dolly’s mother. As a child, Gladys was taken away from her family by the government and put to work as a maid for a White family. As such, she never learned to read, and the only way she could make a life for herself was to follow in her mother’s footsteps as a manual labourer. Despite the hardship she has faced, Gladys is relentlessly optimistic. She frequently enters into fantasy sequences where the world is just, her family is happy, and Dolly has endless possibility before her. Even outside those fantasy sequences, Gladys believes that Dolly can live a better life than the generations of women before her. She is steadfast in this belief, even when Dolly herself doubts it. Gladys’s optimism proves a vital tool for her eventual activism. After Dolly accuses Gladys of projecting her own dreams onto Dolly, Gladys commits herself to working toward making the world a better place for the sake of herself and her family. She confronts the city council on the radio about Aboriginal housing, and she takes over Papa Dear’s speech at a public presentation of a petition when he fails to arrive on time. Gladys’s optimism lets her imagine and believe in a better future, which in turn empowers her to fight for that future.

Analysis

Gladys Banks represents the aspirations and challenges of an Aboriginal woman in 1950s Australia. This multi-dimensional character is caught between her cultural heritage and the desire for progress and recognition within the broader, often unfriendly, society.
As the daughter of Nan Dear and mother to Dolly, Gladys mediates between the old ways and new possibilities that her daughter may have. A scene in the play that captures her ambitions and protective instincts as a mother is her confrontation with the bank manager where she presents her daughter Dolly for a teller’s position. Proudly asserting "She’s just completed her Leaving Certificate—the first in the family—with real good grades… ’N’ top of her class in algebra," Gladys is met not with recognition but indifference, with the bank manager pouring himself tea but pointedly ignoring her. The scene captures the societal barriers that Gladys and her daughter face, as well as Gladys’s determination to push against them.
Gladys’s agency is further highlighted in her firm stance on her daughter’s autonomy and social life. Despite Nan Dear’s apprehensions, Gladys declares decisively, "I’ll make the decisions regarding Dolores thank you. She’s going to the ball. And Errol Fisher is walking her home." This reveals her resolve and the assertion of her role as a parent, as well as the modernising viewpoint she holds compared to Nan Dear, who is more rooted in traditionalist concerns. Moreover, Gladys’s readiness to confront and challenge Nan Dear’s objections demonstrates her strong will as she endeavours to protect her children’s rights to forge their own paths.
Gladys’s complexity extends into her views on housing and respect. When discussing the Queen’s visit and the humiliation of having their poor living conditions concealed behind hessian curtains, Gladys expresses upset not just over the cosmetic cover-ups but the deeper issue of decent housing and respect for her community. This highlights her awareness of the broader socio-political context and her aspiration for improved conditions for her people. Yet, she also values the autonomy they possess in their current living situation, preferring the freedom they enjoy at the shanty on the river to the restrictions experienced in more controlled environments like Cummeragunja, an Aboriginal settlement she and Nan Dear remember with claustrophobia.
Gladys Banks embodies the transition between tradition and progress, acting as both a nurturer and a pioneer. Her interactions with others showcase her resolve to advance her family’s status despite the systemic challenges imposed by aw orld that often disregards their aspirations and dignity.

Nan Dear

Nan Dear is the matriarch of the family at the centre of the play. She is Gladys’s mother and Dolly’s grandmother, and she takes her duty of care to them seriously. Nan Dear is a bastion of tradition, teaching Dolly about her family history and aspects of their culture that she doesn’t learn at school. She has suffered under institutions of racism and ideologies of White supremacy, especially at the Aboriginal reserve in Cummeragunja, where Nan Dear was abused and her daughter Gladys was taken away from her. As such, Nan Dear has little faith that the world can improve, and she doesn’t understand Gladys and Dolly’s attempts to improve it. She especially fears for Dolly when Dolly begins a romantic relationship with Errol, a White boy with the same last name as a White man who raped Nan Dear years ago. Nan Dear works to separate Dolly and Errol, but at the end of the play she realises that Errol is a considerate and respectful young man. When she finds out for certain that Errol is not related to her attacker, she gives him her blessing and encourages Dolly to marry him, demonstrating that even someone as traditional as Nan Dear can grow and change.

Analysis

Born by the Murray River as her birth certificate states, Nan Dear is steeped in her Aboriginal heritage and connections to her traditional lands. Her identity is closely tied to the Murray River and the land from which she was displaced , specifically from Cummeragunja, marking a bitter and forcible removal from her home. This dislocation is a source of deep-seated resentment and shapes her worldview. Despite the trials she has faced, Nan Dear is characterised by a deep sense of duty and pragmatism . She is concerned with everyday survival and the practical needs of her family—working to put food on the table and keeping her family safe.
As the matriarch, Nan Dear’s relationships with her daughter,Gladys, and granddaughter, Dolly, are a mix of affection and stern guidance. She is quick to offer wisdom to Dolly, such as reminding her of the importance of knowing family connections to avoid unwitting relationships with close relatives. Nan Dear’s knowledge of family history is implied to be extensive, though she may feign ignorance when it suits her, suggesting a complicated relationship with the past.
Her cultural beliefs surface subtly through her reluctance to embrace the visiting monarchy, mocking the idea of needing white gloves for a royal visit and declaring, "She’s not my queen". This resistance to the monarch’s visit and the detachment she expresses towards Gladys’ interest in royalty reveals an underlying rejection of colonial authority and ap reference for the Aboriginal ways of life.
Nan Dear’s resilience is not just a personal trait but serves as symbolic of her community’s endurance. Statements like "Born[by the Murray River] and by crikey I’m gunna go back and die there" demonstrate her strong will to return to her roots despite the adversity faced. This return, for her, is associated with embracing the simplicity and beauty of her origins, like having a feed of swan eggs before she passes, signifying a connection to her birthplace. Her experience of displacement is steeped in historical injustices, as evidenced by her bitter recollection of being forced to leave Cummeragunja. This comment on forced relocation exposes the reality of Aboriginal displacement and the loss of home and culture.
These observations offer a critical perspective on the consequences of colonial policies for Aboriginal people.Nan Dear is a fiercely independent individual whose character lends gravity and authenticity to the narrative of "Rainbow’s End." Her dialogue is delivered with an undercurrent of knowing defiance, revealing a woman shaped by adversity yet undiminished in spirit. She represents a generation of Aborigina lpeople who, despite systematic disenfranchisement,maintained their dignity and cultural identity. Through her words and actions, Nan Dear confronts the audience with the realities of her community’s history, while also displaying an indomitable resolve to withstand and challenge these realities.

Errol Fisher

Errol Fisher is a young White encyclopedia salesman. He encounters Nan Dear, Gladys, and Dolly by accident, after taking a wrong turn on his route selling encyclopedias. Errol falls in love with Dolly and comes to admire her family. Gladys demonstrates a strength that Errol has never seen in his own mother, who is subservient to Errol’s father. The affection that Nan Dear and Gladys show Dolly is also alien to Errol, who has grown up with a strict and unfeeling father. Though he earnestly loves Dolly, he offends her with his ignorance of her culture and his assumption that his way of life must be superior to hers. He assumes she would be eager to leave her family behind to be his wife in the city, and he struggles to understand her insistence that she must remain with her loved ones. Ultimately, though, Errol comes to respect what Dolly wants for her future, and he promises to live any kind of life she wants as long as they can do so together.

Analysis

Fisher Errol Fisher emerges in Jane Harrison’s "Rainbow’s End" as a significant, though less central, character, who embodies the intersection of the Aboriginal community’s life and the broader Australian society during the 1950s. Portrayed initially as somewhat awkward and uncertain, he is introduced to the audience as he nearly falls off his bike upon encountering Dolly, signaling an immediate spark of attraction that complicates his interactions with the family.
This initial interaction with Dolly sets the stage for Errol’s character development. The exchange with Gladys further illustrates his social position and perspective. When he tentatively approaches Gladys as she is chopping wood, mistaking her for a man, he immediately demonstrates cultural ignorance and nervousness. Apologising and stating his name, "Sorry er ma’am. My name is Errol Fisher," he provides a contrast to the women’s more assertive presence. This contrast is emblematic of the interplay between white Australians and Aboriginal people at the time—a dynamic of presumed authority and misunderstanding from the former, contrasted with an under-recognised competence and resilience from the latter.
Errol’s intentions are primarily associated with business—selling encyclopedias as revealed in his conversation with Gladys—yet this transactional engagement serves as a backdrop to more complex intercultural exchanges. Gladys and Errol mention the encyclopedias simultaneously, indicating their previous interactions and the economic realities pressing on Gladys, who states, "We… I… won’t be needing them any more. All the shillings go into the meter box now," highlighting not only financial struggles but also the prioritisation of practical necessities over educational luxuries.
His interactions with Dolly in the play are coloured with romantic interest, albeit fraught with the implications of such a relationship in a racially divided society. He compliments her name and attempts to bridge their cultural gap, however awkwardly. Since Errol also represents other characters in the play, his role is fundamental in bringing out the perspectives and reactions of Nan Dear, Gladys, and Dolly and propelling their narratives forward. Errol’s presence and character serve as a mirror that reflects and refracts the complexities of identity and interpersonal relationships within the stratified, mid-20th century Australia Australian society.

Leon (Cousin)

Leon, often referred to as simply “Cousin,” is Dolly’s cousin. He is aggressive and often drunk, and he disapproves of Dolly’s relationship with Errol. As a twisted form of punishment for that relationship, Leon rapes Dolly, resulting in her pregnancy. Leon’s assault of Dolly speaks to the reality of gendered violence within marginalised communities.

Papa Dear

Papa Dear is Nan Dear’s husband, Gladys’s father, and Dolly’s grandfather. Though he is not Gladys’s biological father, since Gladys was conceived from Nan Dear’s rape by a White man, Nan Dear considers him Gladys’s father. Papa Dear is an activist for Aboriginal rights, and his work takes him all over the country, which means he is rarely there for his family. He never appears in person in the play; he is only mentioned in dialogue and imagined in fantasy sequences. His absence leaves Nan Dear, Gladys, and Dolly alone to create an exclusively feminine space in their home.

Analysis

Dear Papa Dear, as depicted in Jane Harrison’s "Rainbow’s End," is a paternal figure whose presence is felt throughout the narrative despite being physically absent for much of the play. His character is crafted through the recollections and expectations of the other characters, particularly Nan Dear and Gladys. Papa Dear’s role is largely communicated through anecdotes and his influence on the family’s structure and values.
Mentioned by Nan Dear as "busy doing good work. God’s work and hard work," Papa Dear is portrayed as a figure devoted to the betterment of the Aboriginal community. His dedication to activism and community work is further highlighted when Gladys recalls that he is "in Western Australia. Touring the communities there," and has been featured "in the newspaper and all," suggesting his actions and efforts are recognised and appreciated beyond their immediate surroundings.
Papa Dear’s dream visitations reinforce his endearing place within the family, despite his absence. In an imagined scene, he is seen wearing "an old-fashioned hat and coat," hinting at a sense of nostalgia and timelessness associated with him. His brief entrance to kiss Gladys on the top of the head showcases a symbolic and loving connection with his family, despite his lack of direct intervention in their current struggles.
The character of Papa Dear is particularly important to Dolly during a vulnerable moment when she reflects on her family tree, considering naming her unborn child Reg or Regina, "after Papa Dear." This indicates an enduring legacy and respect for him within the family, especially for his commitment to their people.
While Papa Dear does not have a speaking role or much direct action, his presence resonates with the family and the audience, capturing the spirit of a generation of Aboriginal individuals who strived to improve conditions for their communities. Through the memories shared and the respect expressed by the characters, Papa Dear stands as a symbol of resilience, hope, and the ongoing struggle for activism and recognition within the Aboriginal community of the time.

The Inspector

The Inspector is a White man employed by the Australian government to inspect the living conditions of Aboriginal Australians, which includes the family’s humpy. As a representative of the Aboriginal Protection Board, the Inspector regulates and controls the lives of Aboriginal Australians, and that power extends to taking Aboriginal children away from their family. The Board took Gladys away from her family when she was a child, and she and Nan Dear are terrified that the Inspector will take Dolly away from them.

Analysis

In the play "Rainbow’s End," the inspector is a minor but telling character that presents a snapshot of the authority and surveillance that Aboriginal people endured in 1950s Australia. He appears in a scene where Nan and Gladys are inside the humpy, anxiously straightening everything, which sets a tone of tension and unease. The inspector serves as a symbol of the government oversight and control that loomed over the lives of the characters.
Paying a visit to evaluate their living conditions, the inspector enters with a superficially pleasant demeanor that thinly veils the oppressive power dynamics at play. He greets the efforts of Nan and Gladys with an offhand compliment, "I say crocheted pillow shams. Such beautiful work!" which Gladys attributes to her mother’s handiwork. His interaction is polite yet distant, making notes while seemingly ignoring the weight of his presence and authority in the humpy.
As the scene unfolds, it becomes clear that his visit is not a casual or friendly one, but an inspection that could have severe implications for the family. The anxiety Nan and Gladys display is indicative of their vulnerability to the whims of the inspector and, by extension, the colonial power structure. When the inspector inquires after a Mr. Banks, his question underscores the traditional views of family structure and the expectation of a male head of household, hinting at patriarchal values as well.
The inspector’s questions and his mode of taking notes encapsulate the intrusion of government into personal spaces and lives. This scrutiny implies potential consequences if living conditions or behaviors do not meet certain standards, though these criteria are not made explicit. This scene heightens the awareness of the audience to the systematic and normalised regulations that govern the lives of Aboriginal characters in the play, bringing the broader context of racial disparity and control into focus through the actions and attitudes of the inspector.

Mr. Coody

Mr. Coody is the rent collector at Rumbalara. He imposes strict and invasive rules on his tenants, which includes threatening to evict Dolly and her family due to Dolly’s pregnancy. His intrusion into the women’s lives reflects that although Rumbalara is an Aboriginal housing district, it is not a safe haven or a stable place of community because it is run by White men.

Bank Manager

The Bank Manager works at the bank where Gladys wants Dolly to find a job. He resists the idea of hiring an Aboriginal worker, making excuses as to why Dolly would not be qualified for the job, but Gladys finally stands up to him and he offers Dolly an opportunity to apply.

Analysis

Harrison uses the The Bank Manager to showcase the systemic barriers faced by the Aboriginal community in terms of employment and societal inclusion. His interaction with Gladys Banks in her attempt to secure a job for her daughter, Dolly, is telling of the racially prejudiced attitudes of the time. Gladys, with determination, engages the Bank Manager and puts forward Dolly’s qualifications for a teller’s position: "But she’s just completed her Leaving Certificate—the first in the family." Despite the qualifications presented, the Bank Manager dismisses the notion, indicating a lack of openness to hiring an Aboriginal person.
The Bank Manager focuses on Dolly’s appearance rather than her capabilities when he says, "Yes. Very pretty face," after examining a photograph of her. This superficial assessment underscores the racial objectification and segregation of Aboriginal people. His concern regarding how Dolly would "fit in" at the bank delineates the invisible boundaries placed by a society reluctant to accept Aboriginal individuals into professional roles. "In a job such as this reliability is important," he states, questioning how Dolly would commute to work, despite evidence of her punctuality and capability presented by Gladys.
The role of the Bank Manager, while not overly complex, provides a window into both the institutionalised racism of the period and the socioeconomic status of Aboriginal families. His patronising attitude when offering a tin money bank to Gladys, likening it to "jam tins," reflects a condescension towards her and her family’s means of saving money, which is deeply ingrained in the Bank Manager’s and wider society’s view of Aboriginal people.
The character subtly represents the gatekeeping that denies Aboriginal people equal opportunities, serving as a juxtaposition to Gladys’s and Dolly’s aspirations for advancement. Through this interaction, Harrison critiques the wider Australian society’s views on race and the illusory concept of a fair, meritocratic system, thereby reinforcing the themes of racism and discrimination that are central to the play’s message.

Ester

Ester is a neighbour of the family who represents the most vulnerable members of the Aboriginal community. She is regularly abused by her White husband, and her sons are taken away by the Inspector.

Regina

Regina is Dolly’s infant daughter. Her birth marks the beginning of the fourth generation of women in the family.

Character and Relationships

Dolly Banks

Key Quote: “And as far as what you’re offering … no thank you. This is my place. I’m staying right here with my mum and my nan.” (to Errol, p.172)
Dolly’s non-reaction to her ruined magazines indicates a practised stoicism in the face of misfortune. She is studious, completing her Leaving Certificate with all ‘As and Bs’ and coming ‘top of her class in algebra’ (p.163). Remarkably ‘pretty’, she is also ‘clever’, ‘kind’, ‘modest’, and ‘straightforward’ (p.190). Dolly sometimes allows herself to dream, like Gladys winning Pick-A-Box (p.130) or winning the Miss Mooroopna-Shepparton title at the ball (p.166). She dreams fancifully of becoming an actress or model, yet practically of a career in nursing (p.142).
She is unhappily aware that she might become a fruit picker like her mother and grandmother (p.141). Dolly’s exposure to racial discrimination — with ‘stones’ thrown at her and ‘snide remarks’ (p.162) overheard in the street — makes her pessimistic about her future. She has a close and loving relationship with Nan, whose overprotectiveness can sometimes be tedious. Yet their closeness and trust alleviate Dolly’s distress after she becomes pregnant, when Nan confides that Gladys was conceived in similarly traumatic circumstances. The sharing of this dark ‘secret’ brings them both comfort.

Analysis

Dolly Banks embodies the tensions between youthful hope and social limitation. Harrison portrays her as the bridge between aspiration and resignation — a young woman educated enough to imagine a different future but aware of the barriers imposed by race and class. Her determination to remain with her family, rather than abandon them for the city life Errol proposes, symbolises her rejection of assimilation and her embrace of identity. Dolly’s relationship with Nan and Gladys represents the generational continuum of Aboriginal womanhood: endurance, wisdom, and resistance to erasure.

Nan Dear

Key Quote: “Ask him what it says in that there encyclops about the Aboriginies, eh?” (to Gladys, p.135)
Nan Dear is often the cynical voice of experience, reminding Gladys that she is a lowly bean picker unworthy of the Queen’s notice (p.124). Unafraid to speak out, she angrily confronts the Rent Collector for criticising their supposed ‘flouting’ of rules, throwing eggs at him (pp.185–6). Capable and energetic, she works as a fruit picker for the canning factory, tackles physical labour, and can make a stew out of nettles when money is short.
She is a loving and protective grandmother who knows the danger of being ‘[taken] advantage of’ by ‘a whitefella’ (p.187). Set in her ways, she mistrusts outsiders — seeing Bob Dyer as ‘one of them flash Yanks’ (p.131) and regarding Errol as a ‘snake-oil salesman’ (p.136). Initially doubtful, she later recognises Errol’s goodness and advises Dolly to marry him ‘before someone else does’ (p.198). Nan also clashes with Gladys over Dolly’s upbringing, criticising her monarchist sympathies and sceptical of education’s value. Yet when she hears Gladys speak up on the radio, she dances with pride around the room.

Analysis

Nan Dear represents endurance, tradition, and the persistence of Aboriginal matriarchal strength. Her scepticism toward authority and colonial structures masks deep compassion and wisdom. Through her character, Harrison celebrates the resilience of older generations who, despite trauma and displacement, preserve dignity and family unity. Nan’s humour, practicality, and protectiveness make her both a guardian of the past and an emblem of cultural survival.

Gladys Banks

Key Quote: “We demand proper schooling. And not just for us. The white people too – they need to be educated about us, and our ways.” (p.197)
Initially a fervent royalist, Gladys goes to extraordinary lengths to glimpse the Queen, only to be blocked by hessian screens hiding Indigenous homes from sight (p.127). Clever and capable, she answers all questions correctly on the quiz show Pick-A-Box but knows a ‘black’ contestant would be unwelcome (pp.129–130). She manages her household with efficiency and intelligence — chopping wood, skinning rabbits (p.129), and managing coins in jam tins — making the Bank Manager’s condescension (p.164) absurd.
Having been sent away as a child (p.184) and denied an education, Gladys feels the sting of illiteracy and wants her daughter to succeed where she could not. Her daydreams of Dolly in academic regalia or being hugged by the Queen (pp.126, 134) express both hope and escapism. When Dolly challenges her to ‘fix’ her own life (p.179), Gladys redirects her energy toward activism — storming a council meeting and demanding fair treatment for Aboriginal families (p.181).

Analysis

Gladys is a figure of transformation — from dreamer to activist. Her evolution from naive royalist to outspoken advocate mirrors the awakening of Indigenous political consciousness. She bridges the domestic and political spheres, proving that even an ordinary woman’s voice can challenge entrenched injustice. Harrison uses her courage to illustrate the theme of self-determination and the belief that change begins within the individual.

Errol Fisher

Key Quote: “This is my first presentation. Well, the first one I got all the way through, anyhow.” (p.137)
Errol is awkward, well-meaning, and naïve — a White encyclopedia salesman who stumbles into the Flats by mistake. His clumsy sales pitch and fearful reaction to a harmless carpet snake (p.145) reveal both humour and ignorance. Despite his flaws, his sincerity sets him apart from other male characters. His relationship with Dolly follows the familiar rhythm of romance fiction — a high at the ball, when he rescues her from Leon (p.167), and a low when he offends her by suggesting she abandon her family (p.171). Misunderstandings later see him wrongly blamed for Dolly’s assault, yet his determination to return and embrace her world reflects genuine love.

Analysis

Errol’s character illustrates the possibility of cross-cultural understanding, albeit imperfect and halting. Through him, Harrison explores the limits of good intentions within systems of inequality. Errol’s willingness to change, to “move to Mooroopna,” and adopt Indigenous expressions (‘snotty googles’, p.191) suggests that reconciliation begins not with assimilation but humility and empathy.

Leon (Cousin)

Key Quote: “She’s no lady, she’s just a little –” (to Errol, referring to Dolly, p.167)
Leon associates with the rough ‘cork-tree lads’ who treat women as objects. His violence toward Dolly reflects both personal corruption and societal neglect. Nan’s comment that the boys have ‘had it hard’ (p.139) hints at systemic roots — poor education, limited work, and widespread alcoholism. Mentioned as a ‘motherless child’ (p.131), Leon represents a generation damaged by dispossession and lack of support.

Analysis

Leon embodies the tragic consequences of internalised oppression and the failures of state policy. While his assault on Dolly is unforgivable, his existence indicts the colonial system that left many young Indigenous men alienated, impoverished, and angry. He is both victimiser and victim — a figure who underscores the intergenerational damage caused by racism and inequality.

Papa Dear

Key Quote: “That’s how important [the Queen] is – getting a meeting with the busiest Aboriginal around!” (Nan, referring to Papa Dear, p.128)
Papa Dear never appears on stage but looms large through others’ stories. A pastor who does ‘good work’ (p.143) and meets the Queen, he has gained status in white society, yet his activism yields little improvement for his people. His absence during family crises — floods, pregnancy, and poverty — highlights the emotional toll of political duty and the limits of symbolic representation.

Analysis

Papa Dear symbolises a generation of Aboriginal leaders who sought reform through institutional acceptance rather than confrontation. While his work is well-intentioned, Harrison implies that activism divorced from community risks perpetuating absence rather than alleviating suffering. He is a figure of both hope and disillusionment.

The Inspector

The Inspector is a smug bureaucrat who prides himself on “bringing change” (p.152), blind to the harm of assimilation. His hollow encouragement that the women should “take leadership” reveals a profound ignorance of their reality. His officiousness exemplifies paternalism — well-dressed oppression masquerading as progress.

Analysis

The Inspector represents state surveillance and control — the polite face of systemic violence. Harrison uses his character to expose how government policy framed Aboriginal suffering as a problem to be managed rather than a wound to be healed.

Mr. Coody (The Rent Collector)

Key Quote: “Your arrangements will need to be re-evaluated, with the impending new arrival.” (p.185)
Mr. Coody’s condescension and moral judgment expose the hypocrisy of colonial authority. He condemns Dolly’s pregnancy and questions Nan’s decency, despite representing a system that denies Aboriginal families adequate housing. His “re-evaluation” of their tenancy embodies the state’s power to intrude upon private life.

Analysis

Through Mr. Coody, Harrison critiques bureaucratic arrogance and the pretence of moral superiority that underpinned racist policies. Nan’s defiant act — throwing eggs at him — becomes symbolic rebellion, an assertion of dignity in the face of systemic degradation.

The Bank Manager

Arrogant and self-satisfied, the Bank Manager embodies white patriarchal privilege. He refuses Gladys’s request for her daughter’s employment, patronises her with a vocabulary lesson, and reinforces racist stereotypes about Aboriginal unreliability (p.164).

Analysis

He stands as a caricature of institutional prejudice — polite yet poisonous. Harrison uses him to illustrate how everyday racism operates behind the veneer of civility, undermining merit and silencing ambition.

Nancy Woolthorpe

Key Quote: “Love your dress, Dolly. Love the fabric. My mother quite liked it too. When it was our sunroom curtains.” (p.166)
Nancy is the archetypal ‘mean girl’ — wealthy, pretty, and cruel. She belittles Dolly at the ball, symbolising class snobbery and racial arrogance. Yet her hollow luxury contrasts sharply with the warmth and humour of Dolly’s family, suggesting that material comfort cannot replace love or community.

Analysis

Nancy Woolthorpe serves as a foil to Dolly — shallow, privileged, and spiritually impoverished. Harrison’s satire of her behaviour underscores how racism is perpetuated not only through policy but through everyday social exclusion.

Ester and Her Boys

Ester is a young Aboriginal mother living at Rumbalara with her three mischievous sons, and she is pregnant again. Her ‘whitefella husband’ (p.132) has given her a black eye, highlighting the cycles of violence and instability experienced by many Indigenous families of the time. When the Inspector visits, Nan fears that Ester’s children will be taken away (p.153) — and indeed, this fear becomes reality. Ester’s boys are removed, ostensibly for “assimilation”, but in truth to be institutionalised or adopted, separated from their family, community, and culture.

Analysis

Ester’s brief appearance is a powerful allusion to the Stolen Generations. Her character encapsulates the vulnerability of Aboriginal mothers under the Assimilation Policy, whose children were taken “for their own good.” Harrison’s inclusion of Ester and her boys exposes the cruelty and hypocrisy of a government that dismantled families under the guise of progress. The boys’ removal functions as both a narrative tragedy and a historical indictment, reminding the audience that these policies perpetuated trauma across generations.

The Policeman

The local policeman (the “Jungi”) appears following the devastating flood that destroys the women’s home and possessions. He exhibits little empathy for Dolly’s distress, and his irritation when she disobeys him reveals his insensitivity. Although he promises to protect their belongings, placing the encyclopaedias “high and dry,” he instead leaves them on the flooded ground (p.174), symbolising negligence and institutional indifference.

Analysis

The policeman’s character demonstrates the absence of meaningful protection for Aboriginal people from authorities. His behaviour reflects the wider neglect of Indigenous welfare by institutions meant to safeguard all citizens. Harrison uses his actions as a metaphor for systemic disregard — the literal and figurative abandonment of Aboriginal people during times of crisis.

The Shire Councillors

The Shire Councillors appear through a radio broadcast, discussing the “shanties” on Crown Land and claiming that Aboriginal families pose a sanitary risk to the town (p.180). Their rhetoric is filled with euphemisms and coded prejudice: they describe the Indigenous community as an “itinerant minority” while ignoring that these families are long-established residents. When Gladys interrupts their meeting to protest, the councillors order her ejection, branding her an “interloper” (p.181).

Analysis

The councillors epitomise bureaucratic racism and the erasure of Aboriginal voices from public discourse. Their paternalistic tone and false concern for “public health” disguise the real motive of displacement and segregation. Gladys’s interruption becomes an act of defiance against a political structure that silences Indigenous women. Harrison’s portrayal of this scene transforms a moment of exclusion into one of empowerment, as Gladys reclaims her right to be heard.

Scene Summary and Analysis

Prologue: Aftermath

Summary

In 1950s Australia, Nan Dear and Gladys rebuild their humpy (another word for a bush hut) after a flood. Gladys is Nan Dear’s middle-aged daughter and the mother of Dolly, a teenager. The women are Aboriginal Australians. Nan Dear and Gladys tear up Dolly’s magazines to repair the walls. Dolly returns home from school and almost protests when she sees her ruined magazines, but ultimately she says nothing, resigned. Nan Dear and Gladys hug Dolly.

Analysis

The play introduces readers and viewers to its main characters as they rebuild their home. This establishes the home as something that must be tended to and maintained, and it also highlights the often invisible domestic labor that goes into maintaining a home. That labor requires sacrifices, like Dolly’s magazines, but the women are willing to put their personal wants aside for the sake of the family and their home.

Act 1, Scene 1 (A)

Summary

Gladys listens to Queen Elizabeth on the radio and prepares to travel to town to see Queen Elizabeth, the first reigning monarch to visit Australia. Nan Dear dislikes the monarchy and pokes fun at Gladys for being a “loyal subject.”
Dolly asks for help on a family tree she is making for school. Nan Dear is bewildered that Dolly is only supposed to list direct relatives and exclude her cousins. She evades Dolly’s questions about her great-grandparents and reminisces about her home by Murray River, which she was forced to leave. Gladys continues to make herself presentable for the Queen, and she fantasises about presenting a bouquet of flowers to Elizabeth before finding herself holding a handful of weeds.

Analysis

Nan Dear disdains the British monarchy as the ultimate colonisers, who are responsible for the oppression of Aboriginal Australians on their own land. Gladys, on the other hand, is more assimilated into White society and thus shares White Australians’ excitement to see their queen. Nan Dear criticises her for this loyalty to a violently racist government.
The school’s assumption that only direct relatives are significant enough to include on a family tree demonstrates how conceptions of family depend on culture. The school system is built around White Australian culture, which does not place the same value on extended family as Aboriginal culture. Meanwhile, Gladys loses herself in the fantasy of meeting Queen Elizabeth, and the sharp contrast between her imagined flowers and the reality of weeds suggests that Gladys indulges in fantasies as a form of escapism.

Act 1, Scene 1 (B)

Summary

Gladys returns home after the Queen’s visit, exhausted. She explains to Nan Dear and Dolly that her taxi never arrived, leaving her to walk along the road hoping for a glimpse of the Queen. Gladys failed to see the Queen, though, because the roads have been lined with burlap to prevent Elizabeth from seeing the humpies along the riverbank.
Gladys complains about the state of their housing, but Nan Dear insists that life is better than it was in the last days at Cummeragunja by the Murray River. Nan Dear tells Gladys that Papa Dear had a meeting with the Queen to discuss improving life for Aboriginal people.

Analysis

The burlap-lined streets are a deliberate effort to keep Queen Elizabeth from seeing Aboriginal Australians and vice versa, and it indicates that the Australian government does not regard Aboriginal Australians as true Australian citizens. Through this racist mindset, Aboriginal Australians and their humpies are seen as an embarrassing reminder of the past on an otherwise modern and civilised land.
Papa Dear’s role as a travelling activist introduces a gendered element to the racism that the family faces. Gladys, as a woman, is tied to her duties at home, so she can’t travel the country to fight for her people’s rights the way her father can.

Act 1, Scene 2 (A)

Summary

Gladys listens to a radio quiz show and answers the questions along with the contestants, getting every one of them correct even as the contestants make mistakes. Dolly suggests Gladys try to become a contestant on the show, but Gladys laughs off the idea of an Aboriginal contestant.
Nan Dear asks Dolly about a boy she was talking to. Dolly frustratedly explains that she won’t be marrying him because he is her cousin, as are most boys her age in the community. Gladys insists that Dolly won’t marry anyone until she finishes her studies, which prompts Dolly to ask how old Nan Dear was when she married. Nan Dear doesn’t answer. Dolly leaves to study, and Nan Dear questions Gladys for “put[ting] ideas” into Dolly’s head about the possibilities for her life.
Nan Dear leaves to tend to a neighbour, Ester, who is pregnant and injured after an altercation with her abusive White husband. Gladys confesses she didn’t know about Ester’s predicament, and Nan Dear recommends Gladys pay less attention to the radio and more attention to the world around her.

Analysis

Though Gladys fantasies about a better life, she is also well aware of the limitations she faces. She is familiar enough with racism to know that the radio quiz show would never accept her, despite her intelligence.
Nan Dear believes optimism is dangerous, since she is convinced that the world will never improve enough for Dolly to have the kind of life Gladys dreams for her. At the same time, Nan Dear’s inquiry into the boys Dolly spends time with highlights that she is concerned about Dolly’s future in her own way.
Ester’s situation demonstrates how gendered and racial dynamics play out within the home. As an Aboriginal woman, Ester has almost no power against the White man she is married to, with her only option being to turn to the members of her community for help.

Act 1 Scene 2 (B)

Summary

A young White man, Errol Fisher, gets lost and ends up at the humpy. He encounters Dolly, and though the two don’t speak to each other, mutual attraction sparks between them. Errol then approaches Gladys and tries to sell her the encyclopedias he is peddling. Gladys turns him away at first, but she takes pity on Errol and allows him to present his rehearsed pitch. As Errol presents the encyclopedias, Gladys becomes interested in them, especially when Errol notes that a previous radio quiz show winner succeeded by studying an encyclopedia. He adds that encyclopedias are good for school-aged children, and Gladys has a fantasy of Dolly graduating in a cap and gown.
Nan Dear enters and is skeptical of Errol and his encyclopedias. She asks what the books say about Aboriginal people, and Errol doesn’t know the answer. Gladys considers buying a set of encyclopedias, but Nan Dear stops her as Dolly comes in and helps Errol sort out his map. She explains that he should go to the White neighbourhood to sell his encyclopedias, and Gladys and Nan Dear agree. Errol departs, but he leaves his calling card, and Gladys is charmed by his manners and his descriptions of the encyclopedia.
As Errol prepares to leave, Dolly asks him about his home in Melbourne and teases him for coming out to her family’s humpy in The Flats. She is confused and uncertain whether he returns her attraction to him. She helps him retrieve his bicycle from some local little boys, then returns to her family. Nan Dear advises Dolly to be careful what kind of men she spends time with. Gladys considers Errol’s card.

Analysis

Errol is out of place in the humpies among The Flats, but he nervously recites the same rehearsed pitch he presents to potential White buyers. His sales pitch appeals to Gladys’s curiosity and thirst for knowledge as well as her desire for a better life for Dolly, and she once again loses herself in a fantasy sequence. Her imagined future for Dolly as a graduate seems as impossible as a dream, but Gladys believes in it.
Nan Dear’s implication that the encyclopedias likely contain untrue and offensive information about Aboriginal people highlights how structures of knowledge and learning are not separate from the racism that informs other cultural institutions. Nan Dear, Dolly, and Gladys all agree that White people are the intended audience for encyclopedias, but Gladys is still tempted by the books’ promise of knowledge.
Neither Dolly nor Errol are fully comfortable making conversation with someone of a different race, but Dolly finds herself enjoying Errol’s company and helps him when local boys see him as an easy target for theft. Her attraction to Errol leaves Dolly confused, and Nan Dear and Gladys’s differing reactions to Errol don’t help that confusion. Gladys is intrigued by Errol, while Nan Dear sees him as a threat.

Act 1, Scene 3

Summary

Dolly rummages for linoleum at the town dump, where she fantasies about buying linoleum from an attractive salesman as a posh customer. Her fantasy fades and she returns home, passing a drunk cousin of hers (Leon) who invites her to a party. She turns him down and continues to the humpy, where Nan Dear scolds her for taking an unsafe route home. She scolds her further when Dolly refers derisively to her cousins who live along the route, insisting that “they’ve had it hard” but refusing to explain further.
Errol returns to the humpy to see if Gladys wants to buy an encyclopedia set. Gladys is at work, so Errol invites himself to wait as an excuse to spend time with Dolly. She won’t let him inside the humpy, but they make conversation outside. After he unthinkingly makes a racist remark, he apologies and asks if Dolly wants to ride his bike with him. She gets on the bike by herself and rides around him, humming, and the two sing together.
Dolly and Errol talk about their lives. Errol enjoys the freedom of being a travelling salesman, while Dolly has no sense of that freedom; she expects she will pick fruit or work at the cannery like everyone else in her community. When Errol asks what Dolly dreams of doing, she muses that she would like to be a model or a nurse, but she knows her mother has other dreams for her.
Gladys arrives with a bookshelf from the dump, and, not noticing Dolly’s embarrassment, explains to Errol that the landfill plot is a desirable piece of land and should have been designated for Aboriginal housing but was instead made into the dump. Dolly leaves, humiliated, and Errol tries to salvage the situation by assessing that the bookshelf just needs a coat of paint. Gladys agrees, and the two laugh.

Analysis

Like her mother, Dolly escapes into fantasy sequences to imagine a world where more possibilities are open to her. Nan Dear, on the other hand, remains unwaveringly practical. She both sympathies with Dolly’s cousins and recognises some of them (such as Leon) as a threat to Dolly’s safety. Nan Dear believes in the importance of family, and that demands empathy, even for relatives she dislikes.
Errol likes Dolly, but he was born and raised in a culture that fundamentally disrespects her and her culture. As such, he carries racist biases that he must consciously try to unlearn. Dolly, for her part, forgives Errol’s casual racism and opens herself up to enjoying her time with him, seemingly in part because he tries to shed his racist biases.
The freedom that Errol enjoys, and Dolly’s lack of that same freedom, comes from both racism and sexism. The racism of Australian society prevents Dolly and her community from holding any kind of job besides manual labour, while sexist expectations of a woman’s role in the home keep Dolly tied to her house and unable to travel like Errol can.
Dolly is embarrassed that Errol knows her family gets furniture from the dump. She is already aware that, according to the commonplace racism in their society, she and her family are of a lower status than Errol, and their secondhand furniture is a symbol of that to her. However, reclaiming abandoned furniture from a spot that should have been made into Aboriginal housing is actually a small act of resistance, as the family finds a way to use that land to make a home despite it being zoned as a landfill.

Act 1 Scene 4

Summary

Dolly sets the table as Gladys serves dinner. Nan Dear complains that they haven’t set a place for Papa Dear. Dolly and Gladys point out that Papa Dear has been preaching across Western Australia for three months, but Dolly sets a place for him anyway. In a fantasy sequence, Papa Dear returns home and kisses Gladys on the top of the head before disappearing.
Dolly tells Gladys and Nan Dear about a job as a cashier that she hopes to get, but Nan Dear dismisses this and declares that Dolly will continue to work with them at the cannery. She sends Dolly out of the room and tells Gladys that a store in town will never hire an Aboriginal girl from The Flats.

Analysis

Gladys’s fantasy sequence about Papa Dear makes clear that she shares Nan Dear’s belief in the importance of family. Gladys wants her family to be reunited, and she wants her father’s affection. This emphasises the family dynamics present in the play; Gladys is a mother, but she is also a daughter, and she misses her father.
Gladys and Nan Dear continue to butt heads over Dolly’s future. Nan Dear sending Dolly out of the room reveals that even though Nan Dear previously chastised Gladys for giving Dolly false hope for the future, Nan Dear doesn’t want Dolly to hear how limited Nan Dear believes her prospects really are.

Act 1, Scene 5

Summary

Errol is in the humpy, much to Dolly’s embarrassment, as he sells Gladys an encyclopedia set to be paid for in instalments. She recounts earning money by picking fruit and remembers Dolly sleeping peacefully as a baby among the carpet snakes in the tree paddocks. Errol asks if the snakes hurt young Dolly, and the women are confused by the question since carpet snakes are harmless. Errol gives Gladys Volume A of the encyclopedia, and she displays it proudly on her new bookcase.
Dolly talks to Errol outside the humpy, and he asks why the family lives in The Flats. As Dolly replies that Nan Dear and Gladys like to live among their community, Gladys steps outside and gestures encouragingly to Errol. He stammers nervously until Gladys tells Dolly that Errol wants to invite her to a dance in town. Dolly refuses, knowing that the townspeople won’t accept Errol bringing an Aboriginal girl from The Flats as a date, but Errol insists he doesn’t care. Dolly cautiously agrees to the date before Nan Dear comes outside and sternly tells Errol to leave.

Analysis

Errol’s misunderstanding of the story about the carpet snake emphasises how removed he is from the cultural context that informs the way Gladys is telling the story. Errol’s cultural context is expressed in the encyclopedias rather than through oral stories or practical knowledge, and it is positioned as the default culture that Aboriginal Australians must assimilate to.
The family’s decision to remain in The Flats despite the constant flooding highlights how home, for these women, is not only a matter of physical shelter but also of community. Gladys continues to extend her approval of Errol by encouraging the relationship between him and Dolly, while Nan Dear continues to make clear that she does not want to include Errol in their community. The abuse the neighbour Ester experiences at her White husband’s hands perhaps informs Nan Dear’s opinion.

Act 1, Scene 6

Summary

A White Inspector surveys the living conditions in the humpy, making strained conversation with an anxious Gladys and Nan Dear. He is a representative of the government of Victoria, making a report on how Aboriginal people live, and he patronisingly tells Gladys and Nan Dear that they must assimilate to White culture. Dolly enters and the Inspector sees her before Gladys and Nan Dear can shoo her away. After he leaves, Gladys and Nan Dear discuss the possibility of the government taking Dolly away.

Analysis

Gladys and Nan Dear’s fear of the Inspector comes from his status as a representative of the Aborigines Protection Board, which held absolute authority over the Aboriginal population of New South Wales. It had, among other privileges, the right to displace Aboriginal people from their homes and the right to remove Aboriginal children from their families. Gladys and Nan Dear are keenly aware that the Inspector has the power to break their family apart, which is why they try to shoo Dolly away before he can see her.

Act 1, Scene 7

Summary

Gladys gets dressed for a fundraiser. Gladys’s uncle intends to raise money to negotiate for Daish’s, the dump, in order to build Aboriginal housing. He plans to call the housing Rumbalara, which means “end of the rainbow.” Meanwhile, Dolly gets dressed for her date with Errol, and Nan Dear and Gladys are astonished at how beautiful she looks. When Nan Dear learns that Dolly is going out with Errol, however, she pretends to have a coughing fit, and Dolly cancels her plans to tend to Nan Dear.

Analysis

Though the local government denied Daish’s to the Aboriginal community, the community continues to fight for it and for access to stable housing. Meanwhile, Nan Dear makes clear her distrust of Errol and her dislike of his burgeoning relationship with Dolly. Nan Dear believes that a relationship with a White boy will endanger Dolly, so she keeps her from the dance in an attempt to protect her granddaughter.

Act 1, Scene 8

Summary

Dolly returns from school to find Nan Dear doing the laundry. Dolly reports that Ester’s sons were not at school, and that everyone suspects it’s because of the Inspector. Gladys gets home and tells Dolly about a trainee program she heard about, and Dolly is frustrated that her mother wants her to act like a White person. Gladys asserts that she wants for Dolly what any mother wants.
Dolly asks Nan Dear why they can’t have “a normal life,” to which Nan Dear responds that their life is normal and there is no point in trying to change it. They discuss the upcoming Miss Marroopna-Shepparton Ball, and Nan Dear promises that she will make Dolly a new dress for the occasion.

Analysis

Ester once again represents the level of powerlessness that Aboriginal women, as doubly marginalised people, must contend with. The government takes away her children, robbing her of her status as a mother. Gladys tries to be the mother Dolly needs, but Dolly is frustrated that her mother is teaching her to get by in White society rather than how to thrive in Aboriginal society.
Dolly faces the constant expectation to assimilate into White culture, which makes her resentful of her own Aboriginal identity. Nan Dear encourages her to embrace her identity and traditions, but she does this by denying that any other kind of life is possible.

Act 1, Scene 9

Summary

Gladys talks to Dolly about the latest book of the encyclopedia to arrive. She tells Dolly that Errol asked about her and encourages Dolly to go to the ball with him. The conversation gets tense as Gladys urges Dolly “not to let them shame you.” Dolly counters that Gladys should take her own advice. Nan Dear enters, and Gladys asserts to her that Gladys is Dolly’s mother, and she wants Errol to walk Dolly home from the ball.

Analysis

Gladys and Dolly both struggle with the conflicting impulses to assimilate into White society and to honor their Aboriginal identities. This tension is complicated by the mother-daughter relationships at play. In this scene, Dolly stands up to Gladys while Gladys does the same to Nan Dear.

Act 1, Scene 10

Summary

Gladys speaks to a bank manager and asks him to hire Dolly as a teller. Elsewhere, Dolly picks fruit and sings to herself. The bank manager questions Dolly’s qualifications and continues to come up with reasons to deny her the job even as Gladys rebuts each of his points. Gladys remembers Dolly asking if she lets White people shame her, and Gladys insists that the bank manager offer Dolly a job. He agrees to interview Dolly.

Analysis

Gladys has high hopes for Dolly’s future, and she sets out to make those dreams a reality by visiting the bank and confronting the manager. Her desire to do right by Dolly inspires Gladys to voice her frustration with the racist systems around her, and she is further empowered by her belief in her and her family’s capability to make a better world for themselves.

Act 1, Scene 11

Summary

Dolly dances with Errol at the ball. She is uncomfortably aware that the White partygoers are staring at them, but Errol comforts her, and she imagines winning the title of Miss Mooroopna-Shepparton Ball. Instead, the winner is a White girl who mocks Dolly for wearing a dress made of fabric scrounged from the dump. Dolly, humiliated, flees the dance and is confronted by her drunk cousin Leon. To punish Dolly for acting as if she is too good for her community, Leon attacks her and Errol, tearing Dolly’s dress and leaving Errol with a black eye.

Analysis

Dolly is uncomfortable and conscious of the danger around her, but she lets herself dream about a world in which racism wouldn’t prevent her from winning Miss Mooroopna-Shepparton Ball. Though the play usually depicts this kind of daydreaming as an act of protective optimism, in this case, Dolly’s hope for a better world is cruelly punished by humiliation and violence.

Act 1, Scene 12

Summary

At the humpy, Gladys thinks happily of how much fun Dolly must be having. However, Nan Dear senses that something is wrong.

Analysis

Just as the violence that ensued after Dolly’s fantasy sequence shows the hazards of losing oneself in imagination, Nan Dear now demonstrates that her cynicism and wariness are sometimes more realistic than Gladys’s optimism.

Act 1, Scene 13

Summary

Errol finds Dolly crying at the river, and she helps treat his black eye. Though her dress is ruined, Errol reassures Dolly that she is still beautiful. They tell each other about their families, and Dolly explains that she grew up with three older brothers and 27 male cousins, which taught her how to fight. Errol marvels at her stories of her family. His father is strict and traditional, and he insists that Errol’s mother is “too delicate” to get a job.
Errol and Dolly get closer, but Dolly pulls away, unwilling to subject either of them to the trials of an interracial relationship. Errol counters that the two of them could move to the city together for a better world. But Dolly argues that Errol’s version of a better world is different from hers; her priority is staying with her family, since that is what home means to her. They fight, but she definitively turns him down and at last Errol accepts this. Dolly starts to walk home, but she is intercepted by Leon.

Analysis

Errol and Dolly have grown up with vastly different conceptions of family, both of which are shaped by their race and culture. Errol’s version of family is the strict nuclear family that is prioritized and rewarded by White Australian culture. He was born into his family but has no real attachment to it. Dolly, on the other hand, has meaningful connections with her entire extended family. She is also used to manual labor being expected of everyone, including women, while Errol’s family embodies a kind of White middle-class gender roles that forbid women from working.
Errol’s unthinking and casual racism has not yet hindered his relationship with Dolly because she knows he is well-meaning. However, it proves the undoing of their relationship as Errol can’t wrap his head around the fact that Dolly doesn’t want to leave her community to live an assimilated life with him in the city. She is also more aware of the violence interracial couples face, since she is generally more aware of racism than Errol.

Act 1, Scene 14

Summary

Nan Dear and Gladys pack up the humpy, preparing to evacuate for a flood. They worry about Dolly, who is still not home. A jungi (policeman) arrives to escort them to the dump, which is the evacuation site since it is above the flood line. They are about to leave when Dolly arrives, clearly traumatised. She “half screams” when the jungi tries to touch her, and she refuses to answer when Gladys and Nan Dear ask her what happened.

Analysis

The flood mirrors Dolly’s implied rape by her cousin Leon. The flood will destroy the humpy, emphasising the instability of Dolly’s home. Meanwhile, Leon’s assault destabilises not only Dolly herself but also her connections to her community. Her trauma is obvious to those around her, but she refuses to share the truth with Gladys and Nan Dear, with whom she is usually so open.

Act 2, Scene 1

Summary

The women return to the humpy after the flood. Dolly, still “shell-shocked,” continues to wear the dress she wore to the ball, which is now badly torn and muddied. Nan Dear and Gladys clean up the wreckage, and Gladys finds that all her encyclopedias are ruined. She insists she doesn’t mind. Nan Dear muses that moving to the Rumbalara housing development might be a good idea for Dolly. Nan Dear and Gladys decide not to tell Papa Dear what happened to Dolly, since it’s “women’s business.”
Errol arrives to apologise to Dolly, but Nan Dear and Gladys violently drive him away, assuming that he is the one who assaulted Dolly. Errol is devastated that they could think him capable of such a thing, and after he leaves, Dolly finally speaks, confirming that Errol was not the one who raped her.

Analysis

Dolly in unable to move past the horror of being assaulted, and her refusal to change her dress reflects how stuck she is in the moment of her trauma. Nan Dear and Gladys, meanwhile, try to rebuild both Dolly and the humpy. The family lives in The Flats to be with their family and community, but they are prepared to move to Rumbalara now that Nan Dear believes Rumbalara might help Dolly. Nan Dear and Gladys agree that the attack on Dolly, and the work of helping her recover, are “women’s business,” which reflects that Papa Dear might be an important activist, but he is not privy to the equally important work that goes on in his own home.
Gladys’s affection for Errol is gone now that she suspects him of hurting her daughter, which emphasises how committed she is to protecting Dolly. Dolly, on the other hand, retains some loyalty to Errol, breaking her silence to make sure her family knows that he is innocent.

Act 2, Scene 2

Summary

A bulldozer destroys the family’s humpy, and they move into a sterile, small, and unwelcoming concrete house at Rumbalara. Nan Dear develops a cough, but she promises to make curtains for the new house.

Analysis

The family leaves behind the humpy, which was unstable but familiar and made by the women themselves, for the distinctly unhomelike Rumbalara housing. The contrast between the humpy and Rumbalara illuminates the injustice of needing to choose between safe housing and emotionally fulfilling housing. Nan Dear’s promise to liven up the house with curtains also underscores the importance of domestic labour in making a house a home.

Act 2, Scene 3

Summary

Gladys brings Dolly a letter from the bank, but Dolly is uninterested in the job as teller. She tells her mother that this job is Gladys’s dream, not Dolly’s, and angrily suggests Gladys “fix [her] own house.” Gladys storms out.
Nan Dear pays the rent to the rent collector Mr. Coody and helps Dolly prepare a meal. They listen to the radio and are surprised to hear Gladys arguing with city councilors about building Aboriginal housing at Daish’s. Gladys is removed from the council meeting, but Dolly and Nan Dear are still proud of her.

Analysis

Though the family has left the humpy, the image of rebuilding a house returns as Dolly uses a person’s house as a symbol for their life. This metaphor emphasises the importance Dolly and her family place on home. It also signifies how the sterile Rumbalara housing is causing fractures within the home, which was previously a space where the family is united.
Mr. Coody’s arrival to collect rent makes clear that the family’s home is not their own anymore; it is owned and controlled by Mr. Coody. In light of this lack of control, Gladys asserts her dignity and a level of control by refusing to let the city councillors debate her life without her input.

Act 2, Scene 4

Summary

Errol finds Gladys cutting wood outside the Rumbalara housing. They discuss the encyclopedias. Gladys doesn’t want to buy any more sets of encyclopedias, and even though she isn’t supposed to be able to cancel her contract, Errol offers to help her do so. Gladys reveals she can’t read or write, so Errol helps her compose the required letter. Gladys apologies for assuming that Errol raped Dolly, and Errol asks if he can see Dolly. Gladys gently but firmly tells him that Dolly won’t want to see him.
Gladys asks Errol to teach her to read and write. She explains that Nan Dear attended school when the family lived at Cummeragunja, but the mission managers were cruel, and Gladys was sent off at a young age to do domestic work for a large wealthy family. After that, she married Dolly’s father and was too busy working and raising children to learn. Errol agrees to teach Gladys to read. He still hasn’t given up hope of reuniting with Dolly, and he hopes to prove that he “can be worthy of her.”

Analysis

Gladys has always liked Errol as a boy who might be good for Dolly, but now their relationship begins to develop on its own terms. She priorities Dolly’s emotional well being over Errol’s desire to see her, but she nonetheless treats Errol with gentle respect. Gladys is the kind of strong and caring mother that Errol never had, and she allows herself to be vulnerable with him when she reveals she can’t read or write.
The reasons behind Gladys’s illiteracy are a combination of racism and sexism. She didn’t learn to read as a child because the Aborigines Protection Board removed her from her family, who were already being mistreated at Cummeragunja. By the time she was old enough to escape the most controlling parts of the Aborigines Protection Board, Gladys found herself where she is now––bound by the domestic responsibilities expected of her as a woman.

Act 2, Scene 5

Summary

The rent collector Mr. Coody comes by for the rent and finds Nan Dear cooking eggs for a heavily pregnant Dolly. He notes that he will re-evaluate their lease in light of Dolly’s pregnancy, but Nan Dear takes him to task for consistently invading his tenants’ privacy and imposing too many rules. She throws eggs at him, and he flees.
Dolly and Nan Dear look at Dolly’s old family tree assignment, and Dolly shares that she plans to name her baby after Papa Dear. She confesses that she is worried she won’t love the baby. Nan Dear says that Dolly is finally a woman and thus ready to hear the truth: Gladys’s biological father is a White man who raped Nan Dear when she was a teenager. This is why Nan Dear has been so opposed to Dolly’s relationship with Errol––especially because Errol has the same surname as Nan Dear’s attacker. When Dolly shares that she broke things off with Errol because he wants to take her away from her family, Nan Dear encourages her to consider leaving. At the same time, she insists that Dolly mustn’t resume her relationship with Errol, and Dolly agrees.

Analysis

Mr. Coody represents how White authority infringes on every moment of Aboriginal life by denying Aboriginal people privacy and self-determination within their own homes. Nan Dear resists this facet of White supremacy, asserting her home as a space she and her family have the right to control.
While Dolly’s belief in the importance of family led to tension with Errol, it connects her to Nan Dear, who believes the same thing. Dolly’s decision to name the baby after Papa Dear reaffirms her devotion to her family and her desire to honor her relative’s traditions and legacy. Nan Dear values tradition as well, but she also wants Dolly to make a meaningful future for herself, which is why she encourages Dolly to leave. Nan Dear’s dislike of Errol is also explained as not simply a general protectiveness of her granddaughter but a reaction to trauma that she now chooses to share with Dolly in a moment of honesty and vulnerability.

Act 2, Scene 6

Summary

Errol is teaching Gladys to read, and they practice on the Inspector’s report of Aboriginal living conditions. Gladys encourages Errol to call her “Aunty” and asks about his feelings for Dolly. He admits that he still has feelings for her, but he doesn’t know what she wants. Gladys invites Errol to a public meeting in Melbourne the following week, where Papa Dear will be speaking on Aboriginal housing. He accepts the invitation, and Gladys tells Errol that even if he and Dolly don’t end up together, she likes Errol.

Analysis

Errol loves Dolly, but that love has been hindered by a lack of understanding of where she comes from. Now Errol is developing a love for Dolly’s family and an appreciation for her community that mirror Dolly’s own loyalty to her loved ones. Gladys’s request that Errol call her “Aunty” is an invitation to join the family, which demonstrates that her understanding of family is inclusive and based on personal bonds. This contrasts Errol’s previous understanding of family as something constraining and dependent only on blood ties.

Act 2, Scene 7

Summary

Nan Dear, Gladys, and Dolly sit in the audience at the public meeting. With them is Dolly’s baby, Regina. Dolly says that Gladys should be giving a speech, but Nan Dear scoffs that speechmaking is “men’s business.” Dolly gave up on the bank interview, but she has been awarded a scholarship to a nursing program in Melbourne, and Gladys is thrilled for her.
Errol arrives and sits with the women. Gladys pulls Nan Dear away to go find Papa Dear. Errol is startled to see Regina, and he finally realises what happened the night of the flood. He apologies to Dolly “for everything,” and she apologies in turn. He offers to move to the country so they can be together and stay near Dolly’s family, but she tells him that she is going to Melbourne and not ready for a romantic relationship. He respects her decision, and they agree to remain friends.
Nan Dear returns, feeling ill, and Dolly hurries off to get her medicine. Errol offers to drive her to the hospital, and when she refuses, he offers to drive her home. She is touched. Nan Dear asks about Errol’s surname, and he explains that his parents adopted it after immigrating from Germany. Nan Dear is relieved that Errol is not related to the Fisher who once assaulted her. She kisses Errol on the cheek. Dolly sees this and is stunned.
Papa Dear fails to arrive to present the petition, so Gladys does it in his stead. She makes a speech petitioning Queen Elizabeth to give Aboriginal Australians decent housing and to finally treat them like Australian citizens. She begins to read from the petition and includes her own opinions, demanding equal education and job opportunities for Aboriginal people. She concludes with a point that is not on the petition: she wants White people to treat Aboriginal people with respect. The audience applauds.
In a fantasy sequence, Nan Dear imagines Dolly and Errol, with Regina between them, getting married. She returns to reality and tells Dolly to marry Errol. She tells Errol to call her “Nan,” and he thanks her for her blessing. Papa Dear arrives.

Analysis

Dolly has grown up from a girl to a woman. She has chosen a future for herself and will be leaving the protection of her mother and grandmother, while she herself is tasked with raising the family’s next generation. Her family has faith in her ability to pursue a career, but Nan Dear still believes that woman and men’s work are divided categories, and that public activism belongs to men.
Errol makes clear his devotion to Dolly in this scene. He unequivocally apologies, recognising his own wrongdoing, and promises to prioritise Dolly’s needs over his own wants. His desire for a relationship is not hindered at all by Dolly’s baby, which emphasises that he is ready to commit not only to Dolly but to her family. When Dolly turns him down, he respects her ability to choose what she wants and is just glad to stay in her life as a friend.
Errol proves to Nan Dear that despite his occasional moments of unthinking racism, he is fundamentally kind and respectful. However, she cannot let herself like him until she knows for certain that he isn’t related to her rapist. When she learns that Errol has no connection to her past trauma, Nan Dear embraces him as a good choice for Dolly.
Papa Dear’s work is important, as his speech provides Gladys the foundation to begin her public activism. However, Gladys soon steps out of Papa Dear’s shadow, asserting that politics and activism are just as much a place for women as they are for men. Her concerns about housing are informed by the domestic labour she has spent her life performing, but she can also see beyond that. Her demand that White people respect their Aboriginal countrymen indicates a holistic understanding of how microaggressions, and seemingly small moments of interpersonal disrespect, are the building blocks of White supremacist ideologies that lead to racist government policies.
Tellingly, the final moment of the show is centred around the intimate workings of the family rather than the broader societal issues the play also explores. Nan Dear finally breaks free of her cynicism and indulges in a fantasy sequence, like her daughter and granddaughter often do, and that fantasy inspires her to invite Errol to join the family. As Errol, Dolly, and Regina come together to form the next generation of parents and children, Papa Dear arrives as a reminder of the family’s lineage.

Sample 1 - High Scoring Rainbow’s End Essay

Topic: How does Rainbow’s End suggest that everything will be alright?
In the 2005 Indigenous play ‘Rainbows End’, playwright Jane Harrison suggests everything will be alright for the Dear family, despite the challenges they combat. This is shown through the portrayal of self-determination, coupled with the continued hope and optimism of the Dears. However, the heavily damaging colonial attitudes to the Indigenous community act as a constant threat looming over the community and their futures.
Throughout the play, Harrison highlights the unwavering self-determination of the Indigenous community, suggesting everything will be alright due to the strong resolve of the Dear family. This is depicted most vividly through the growth of Gladys, initially reserved and acquiescent, but steadily becoming more outspoken and steadfast in her beliefs for a better future. This culminates in Gladys taking a bold stand for the indigenous community at the town meeting, stating “I’m not an interloper – I belong here! - this is my land!”. Gladys’ open defiance in the meeting, challenging cultural norms and expectations, acts as a pivotal switch from Gladys’ belief everything will be alright, to taking direct action in pursuit of a better future for her family. Similarly, Dolly’s stoicism and resilience in the face of adversity and her assault serve as an emphatic representation of selfdetermination. After her assault, Dolly’s honest and true nature in showing her baby to Errol, despite severe shame, depicts her growing as an individual through hardship and pain through these qualities of self-determination. Stating “Yes, I’m okay” when questioned by Errol about her baby, Dolly embraces her struggles, remaining determined and continuing to seek a better future for herself and her baby. Overall, Harrison suggests everything will be alright for the Dear family, despite great hardship, through the continued determination of both Gladys and Dolly.
Harrison further suggests that everything will be alright due to the stalwart hope of the Dear family, embodied through Papa Dear, dream sequences and the prospect of education. Throughout, Papa Dear acts as a symbol of a positive future and hope for the family and broader Indigenous community, travelling across Australia fighting for their rights. This is shown through Nan, who states “its Papa Dears mission to make things better for the Aboriginal people”, suggesting that Papa Dear acts as a figurehead and an unseen force for good in the Indigenous community. Similarly, Papa Dear exists as a projection of hope through dream sequences with Gladys, who observes “Papa Dear, in old fashioned hat and coat, dances in, throws his hat on the hatstand, kisses Gladys on the top of the head, and dances out”. This suggests an omnipresent quality of Papa Dear as a symbol of hope for the members of the Dear family; as a constant reminder that everything will be alright. Further, the prospect of education offers hope for the future for Dolly, and is first presented in the form of encyclopaedia’s from Errol, to which Gladys exclaims “my daughter, a graduate”. This is further propagated by Dolly’s ambitions of becoming a nurse in Melbourne, as a catapult to a better and more prosperous future. Ultimately, Harrison suggests that the unwavering hope of the Dear family, through Papa Dear and the prospect of education, are a reminder that everything will be alright.
However, Harrison also indicates the highly destructive nature of colonial attitudes, and their ability to prevent the Dear family from a better future, as a direct challenge to the sentiment that everything will be alright. Harrison portrays this primarily through the housing inspector, representing a hostile Australian government policy and its corrosive effect on the lives and futures of the Indigenous communities. This is demonstrated through the removal of Aunty Esthers children at the behest of the inspector, and further the repeated visits he makes to the Dear family home. Stating “your whites are so white, with river water no less”, the inspector highlights to close scrutiny of every detail of the Dear’s lives, and the constant threat to their future looming over them. Similarly, Harrison suggests the continued resentment of the broader community actively works to dissuade the Dears and Indigenous community from seeking a better future. This is displayed through the town council, whilst attempting to silence Gladys challenging societal norms, stating “the Chair does not recognise this…. Eject this interloper!”. Further, Harrison displays the negative affects of such attitudes through Dolly, exclaiming “it’ll never change…. Some things never do” after being scrutinised by Nancy at the Shepparton ball. Overall, Harrison suggests that colonial attitudes can directly challenge and threaten the hopes and self-determination of the Dear family and broader indigenous community, and further the prospects of everything being alright.
On balance, Harrison displays the potential for everything to be alright for the Dear family and broader Indigenous community through the qualities of self-determination and hope, but also the constant threat of colonial attitudes to the future.

Sample 2 - High Scoring Rainbow’s End Essay

Topic: ’Harrison’s play celebrates characters who show strength through adversity.’ Discuss.
Set in Victorian town, Shepparton, Jane Harrison’s 2007 play, Rainbow’s End, explores the lives of three generations of Koori women as they navigate the setbacks and challenges faced by First Nations people in 1950s Victoria. At a time where the government regulates the policies of Assimilation of the Indigenous community, these women are vulnerable to the impacts of racism, colonisation and suppression of knowledge. But despite facing discrimination at every turn, Harrison commemorates their resilience in the face of adversity. The women are able to become symbols of hope as they refuse to allow their struggles to define them.
Throughout Rainbow’s End, the struggle for the Indigenous community to pursue their dreams due to racial prejudice is highlighted in order to spotlight their ability to overcome it. This is demonstrated in scene ten in the bank managers office. Gladys tries to apply Dolly for a job as the bank teller, assuring the bank manager that she is fit for the job by advocating for her brains and wits. But despite Dolly being a good candidate, the manager is hesitant about letting her apply. He argues that, “reliability is important,” implying that Dolly is unreliable despite never having met her. He makes this assumption due to his personal prejudice to people of Dolly’s heritage. This demonstrates the racial discrimination that limits opportunities of First Nation’s people. Regardless of the bank manager’s doubts, Gladys pushes for the job as she is reminded of Dolly’s words, “have you learnt not to be shamed by them?”. Through this, Harrison highlights how the women’s power to overcome setbacks comes from within themselves. She champions these women for their ability to stand up for themselves in spite of the racially biased attitudes that are meant to limit them. This theme of hope is prevalent throughout the play and intends to inspire strength from within to tackle problems.
Moreover, Harrison explores the intricacies of cultural identities and belonging, highlighting the strong connection to culture despite the colonisation that the characters face. Nan Dear is a compelling example of this. Nan’s frequent allusions to spirits or warnings from her “ancestors” demonstrate the strong indigenous traditions that survive and carry on even after the effects of colonisation. Intergenerational wisdom is significant in indigenous culture and Nan Dear’s frequent references to guidance from her ancestors attempts to pass that down through her children, who may continue to succumb to assimilation but nevertheless is still deeply rooted in their cultural identities. The reminders of tradition allows Harrison to assert that cultural identity cannot be erased and is represented through characters like Nan Dear who have been through cultural erasure but continues to be a strong image of indigenous culture.
Furthermore, resilience is present in overcoming adversity from the suppression of knowledge that characters throughout the play go through. Gladys is a representation of this as it is revealed to the reader that Gladys is illiterate. This was due to the assimilation policy that separated Gladys from her family and forced her into working at a young age rather than getting an education. Harrison demonstrates Gladys overcoming this in the last scene of the play where Gladys delivers a speech at the public meeting. Not only does she deliver it flawlessly, she delivers it with confidence, which Gladys has mentioned that she “gets nervous in front of people.” Harrison acknowledges the optimistic and determined attitude that Gladys uses as a tool to fight for her ambitions despite the systemic racism that has denied her basic human rights. Through Gladys Harrison aims to write about the ‘unsung’ heroes who were not expected to be a hero but was able to become one in spite of the adversity that she faces.
In conclusion, “Rainbows End” champions the characters for their ability to overcome adversity due to their unwavering determination, offering a powerful testament to the power of human spirit when up against hardship. Through the use of quotes and direct references to the characters, Harrison tells a compelling story that aims to bring hope to the audience, reminding them that the difficulties that humanity faces is incomparable to the resilience that lies within each of us.

Sample 3 - High Scoring Rainbow’s End Essay

Topic: ‘Least here we do things our way- no one breathin’ down our necks’
Home is central to identity in Rainbow’s End. To what extent do you agree?
Amongst the racism and oppression, it is clear that ‘home’ is of the utmost importance to the identity of Harrison’s Indigenous characters. Set against a backdrop of white Australian superiority in the 1950s, ‘Rainbow’s End’ by Jane Harrison explores the central theme of ‘home’ in the context of identity. For the three generational main characters, their ‘humpy’ alongside the riverbank of the Mooroopna flats is integral to the make up of who they are and essential for Harrison to showcase the strength and resilience needed for First Nations to fight their marginalised status. Beyond the physical ‘home’, identity is shaped by the family and culture surrounding the characters through the lens of white and Indigenous Australians.
Throughout the first part of the play, ‘home’ is physically represented as the ‘humpy’, a hut with a dirt floor and hessian-covered walls. Despite the poverty of the main character’s living conditions, it isn’t the place where they sleep that is important to them as much as the culture surrounding them. For the character of Nan, "the Murray…Is your place isn’t it” due to the immense sense of belonging she feels after her oppressive experience with the Cummeragunja mission and the close proximity of her Aboriginal ‘family.’ In this way Harrison highlights the role culture and a physical ‘home’ plays in an individual’s identity. In addition, the pride taken in the appearance and homeliness of the ‘humpy’ is a further aspect of identity illustrated by Harrison. When in the play it comes time for the inspector to visit to the ‘humpy’, Harrison has Nan and Gladys ‘nervously’ straightening the cushions and draws attention to the ‘beautiful work’ of the crocheted pillows, emphasising the the importance of ‘home’ to the characters. This is brought to the attention of the audience again during the move to the ‘white and featureless’ Rumberala housing, and the characters immediately think of ‘making curtains’ as a way to reassociate their identity with the new housing. The contrast between the ‘home’ of the white Australian characters such as Errol and Nancy, and the housing in Rumbalara and the flats is reflective of the identities of the characters and how they feel entitled to act. For example, Nancy is portrayed as wealthy and privileged and therefore acts in a privileged and entitled way, whilst the main characters are substantially underprivileged and act humble, with kindness despite adversities.
The playwright suggests ‘home’ is more than a physical establishment but extends into a ‘home’ found within family and culture. The notion of family and culture as a ‘home’ is a central theme throughout the play, explicitly explored between the misalignment of Errol and Dolly’s definition of a ‘home’. For the character of Errol, he believes a ‘home’ is a place to live, where only the wealth and geographical opportunities are relevant, however, Harrison utilises his conversation with Dolly to pinpoint to ideal that “a real home is where people are looking out for each other,” regardless of how many ‘presents’ are under the tree. Furthermore, Errol reflects on the differences of their families, expressing to Dolly that his Dad “expects me to call him sir” and ultimately concluding that despite the poverty of the flat’s he bets ‘you have more fun than we do’. This conversation was pivotal to underlining the impact of a ‘home’ with a supportive family and rich culture in creating a luster for life and powerful sense of identity.
For the characters in the play, ‘home’ is closely intertwined with colonial suppression, the white Australian’s control over Aboriginal housing had a heavy influence on the identity of the characters. Throughout the play, Harrison makes reference to a difference in the perception of Aboriginals from the flat’s and ‘townie types of cross-over Aborigines’ depicting those who lived in town as slightly better off, with better opportunities. This differentiation underpins the impact of location on identity as the ‘white fellas’ feel the Indigenous characters need to ‘be absorbed into the community’ in order to be ‘assimilated’ and forced to act in alignment with white culture. The control that the white characters, such as the inspector, assert over housing is detrimental to the identity of the characters, it withdraws their control and forces them to abandon their culture in order to be accepted. Moreover, Harrison suggests that a ‘home’ can affect social mobility, Gladys in particular is obsessed with ‘fitting in’ and being accepted and it becomes a large part of her identity. The ‘white gloves’ and ‘Auntys shoes’ are a symbol of her need to assimilate to white culture, acting in direct contrast to the Aboriginal culture that makes up her ‘home’ and thereby her identity.
Throughout the entirety of the play, Harrison brings forth the different types of ‘home’, both physical and as a culmination of family and culture. In this way it is evident to the audience that home is closely entwined with the identity of all the characters in the play, from Errol who has never experienced a ‘home’ within a family, to Dolly whom does not associate a ‘real home’ with a fancy establishment but instead with a loving family and cultural traditions. The exploration into the impacts of oppressive attempts from the ‘white fellas’ to force ‘assimilation’, displays how a ‘home’ is more than a roof over one’s head, but instead an embodiment of an individual’s unique identity as formed by relationships, culture, pride and resilience.

Topic: Harrison’s play celebrates characters who show strength through adversity. Discuss.
Jane Harrison’s play Rainbows End is a powerful exploration of the lives of three generations of Indigenous women, Nan Dear, her daughter Gladys, and granddaughter Dolly, in 1950s rural Australia. Harrison presents their daily struggles with poverty, limited access to education, and systemic racism, not to victimise them, but to celebrate their resilience and determination. Through her portrayal of these women, Harrison critiques the entrenched hierarchies that marginalise Indigenous communities, while affirming the strength required to survive and resist. Ultimately, the play celebrates the capacity of Indigenous women to endure adversity, even as it recognises the costs of their survival.
Harrison portrays how racial discrimination restricts Indigenous Australians’ opportunities and dreams. This is evident in Scene Ten, when Gladys visits the bank in the hope of securing Dolly a position as a teller. Gladys proudly emphasises her daughter’s academic success, noting that Dolly has completed her “leaving certificate” and is “top of her class,” in an attempt to counteract the stereotypes imposed upon Indigenous people. However, the bank manager dismisses Dolly’s achievements, questioning how she would “fit in” and whether she could be “relied on” to attend work daily, despite never having met her. Through the portrayal of the bank manager, Harrison highlights how deep-seated structural racism embedded in Australian institutions causes harmful stereotypes that prevent Indigenous communities from fulfilling their full potential in society, denying them equal access to employment. By juxtaposing the women’s ambitions with the bank manager’s prejudices, Harrison critiques the injustice of a culture that confines Indigenous families to “picking” and other forms of low-status labour regardless of merit. Yet she also affirms Gladys’ resilience, showing her refusal to abandon her daughter’s dreams despite repeated setbacks. In doing so, Harrison not only exposes how systemic racism denies equality but also celebrates the persistence of Indigenous women who continue to assert dignity and demand recognition against such barriers.
Harrison exposes the entrenched poverty endured by Indigenous families to critique the systemic marginalisation of First Nations communities. Rather than presenting poverty as a personal failing, Harrison illustrates how social and economic exclusion force the Dear family into degrading conditions and low-cost labour, while simultaneously highlighting their resilience in preserving dignity and hope. Nan Dear’s ironic description of their riverbank “humpy” as “our own little palace” re-frames deprivation as pride, transforming humiliation into humour. Similarly, Gladys expresses frustration at Dolly’s reliance on “hand-me-downs,” revealing her determination to secure independence for her daughter. By infusing moments of humour and defiance into depictions of poverty, Harrison refuses to allow Indigenous identity to be defined by shame. Instead, she asserts the capacity of women like Nan and Gladys to maintain agency and self-respect in the face of adversity. Dolly’s dream sequence after her visit to the town tip symbolises a longing for a future beyond dispossession, foregrounding aspirations that colonial systems sought to deny. Through these portrayals, Harrison critiques structural inequalities, particularly the exploitation of Indigenous people as fruit pickers and scavengers, while simultaneously celebrating the resilience and strength of Indigenous women to endure and reimagine possibility. Thus, Harrison’s authorial intent lies not only in exposing the material realities of Indigenous poverty but also in affirming the power of humour, pride and aspiration as acts of survival against systemic injustice.
Knowledge is presented in the play as both a pathway to empowerment and a means of representing the differences in wisdom. Gladys has a strong belief that schooling will elevate Dolly beyond ingrained disadvantage, insisting that she “get an education” so she can “be someone.” This determination highlights her strength as a mother, willing to challenge generational cycles of poverty. Harrison deliberately frames this ambition with irony: while Dolly’s academic success is undeniable, she remains judged through stereotypes and institutional racism, suggesting that education alone cannot dismantle systemic barriers. At the same time, Harrison complicates the notion of knowledge by staging contrasts between Indigenous oral tradition and Western authority. When Errol dismisses Dolly’s experience with snakes, Gladys’ clarification that it was a harmless “carpet snake” undermines the authority of the encyclopedia and asserts that lived experience offers forms of wisdom inaccessible to formal schooling. By juxtaposing these knowledge systems, Harrison critiques the way white institutions privileged their own frameworks as universal while marginalising Indigenous perspectives. Her authorial intent is doubled to expose how education in the 1950s was arranged by race and class, yet also to celebrate the resilience of families like the Dears, who embraced education without relinquishing pride in their cultural knowledge. Harrison both honours the courage of Indigenous families who value education as a tool for advancement and critiques the system of schooling planted between classes in the 1950s.
Harrison’s Rainbow’s End critiques the systemic racism that restricted Indigenous Australians while celebrating the resilience of women who refused to be silenced. By exposing institutional prejudice, entrenched poverty and competing forms of knowledge, she highlights both the barriers that denied equality and the strength of Indigenous families who asserted dignity despite them. In doing so, Harrison affirms that hope, humour and cultural pride are not just survival strategies but powerful acts of resistance against injustice.

Cemented within a world enveloped by overpowering white culture, Rainbows End seeks to explore the lengths of condemnation expressed by Indigenous Australians, juxtaposed with the desire to be included. Within the play, Harrison blatantly reveals consistent resistance and dislike aimed towards white authority yet contrasts this with younger generations who desire integration and inclusion in society. Overall, Harrison criticises the unjust dilution of Indigenous culture, simultaneously advocating for Indigenous Australians to continue fighting to be heard over white dominance.

Harrison evidence this though Nan Dear showing her despise for Errol’s encyclopedias, asking him what it says, “in that there encyclops about Aboriginals”. The dream sequence represents Gladys’ subconscious suggestion that her daughter may never be able to be “a graduate” due to her needing to follow Nan’s beliefs as the eldest of the family, being constrained to the limitations rightful morals to uphold Indigenous independence. Harrison champions the pursuit of education despite society restraining full ability yet criticises the neglect of protecting Indigenous culture and forgetting true identity.
Harrison impatiently lambasts the evident erasure of Indigenous culture, through white authorities directly and indirectly pushing it, which exposes their never-ending desire to abolish Aboriginal knowledge and existence. Harrison represents this through the humpy inspector asking if Gladys has heard of “the concept of assimilation”, as a promise that “things will change.” The contradictory nature of the inspectors’ intentions and promises represent white ignorance towards Indigenous perspectives and opinions, as the inspector didn’t let Gladys answer if she has heard of the “concept” of assimilation, continuing to talk about it as if she isn’t there. Conclusively, Harrison continuously exhibits justified resistance to colonisation of Indigenous culture yet remains hopeful that younger generations may have the courage to break the discriminatory cycle. Harrison encapsulates this through continued vilification of white knowledge and education, and the injustice of institutional authority, while postulating a juxtaposed change in views through the acceptance of colonisation within younger generations. Harrison ultimately condemns the unjust erasure of Indigenous culture and identity, while pushing the notion that the amalgamation of cultures may lead to increased opportunities for Indigenous Australians and should not be seen as an entirely demonised occurrence.
