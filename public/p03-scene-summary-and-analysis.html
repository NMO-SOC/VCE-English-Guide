Scene Summary and Analysis

Prologue: Aftermath

Summary

In 1950s Australia, Nan Dear and Gladys rebuild their humpy (another word for a bush hut) after a flood. Gladys is Nan Dear’s middle-aged daughter and the mother of Dolly, a teenager. The women are Aboriginal Australians. Nan Dear and Gladys tear up Dolly’s magazines to repair the walls. Dolly returns home from school and almost protests when she sees her ruined magazines, but ultimately she says nothing, resigned. Nan Dear and Gladys hug Dolly.

Analysis

The play introduces readers and viewers to its main characters as they rebuild their home. This establishes the home as something that must be tended to and maintained, and it also highlights the often invisible domestic labor that goes into maintaining a home. That labor requires sacrifices, like Dolly’s magazines, but the women are willing to put their personal wants aside for the sake of the family and their home.

Act 1, Scene 1 (A)

Summary

Gladys listens to Queen Elizabeth on the radio and prepares to travel to town to see Queen Elizabeth, the first reigning monarch to visit Australia. Nan Dear dislikes the monarchy and pokes fun at Gladys for being a “loyal subject.” Dolly asks for help on a family tree she is making for school. Nan Dear is bewildered that Dolly is only supposed to list direct relatives and exclude her cousins. She evades Dolly’s questions about her great-grandparents and reminisces about her home by Murray River, which she was forced to leave. Gladys continues to make herself presentable for the Queen, and she fantasises about presenting a bouquet of flowers to Elizabeth before finding herself holding a handful of weeds.

Analysis

Nan Dear disdains the British monarchy as the ultimate colonisers, who are responsible for the oppression of Aboriginal Australians on their own land. Gladys, on the other hand, is more assimilated into White society and thus shares White Australians’ excitement to see their queen. Nan Dear criticises her for this loyalty to a violently racist government. The school’s assumption that only direct relatives are significant enough to include on a family tree demonstrates how conceptions of family depend on culture. The school system is built around White Australian culture, which does not place the same value on extended family as Aboriginal culture. Meanwhile, Gladys loses herself in the fantasy of meeting Queen Elizabeth, and the sharp contrast between her imagined flowers and the reality of weeds suggests that Gladys indulges in fantasies as a form of escapism.

Act 1, Scene 1 (B)

Summary

Gladys returns home after the Queen’s visit, exhausted. She explains to Nan Dear and Dolly that her taxi never arrived, leaving her to walk along the road hoping for a glimpse of the Queen. Gladys failed to see the Queen, though, because the roads have been lined with burlap to prevent Elizabeth from seeing the humpies along the riverbank. Gladys complains about the state of their housing, but Nan Dear insists that life is better than it was in the last days at Cummeragunja by the Murray River. Nan Dear tells Gladys that Papa Dear had a meeting with the Queen to discuss improving life for Aboriginal people.

Analysis

The burlap-lined streets are a deliberate effort to keep Queen Elizabeth from seeing Aboriginal Australians and vice versa, and it indicates that the Australian government does not regard Aboriginal Australians as true Australian citizens. Through this racist mindset, Aboriginal Australians and their humpies are seen as an embarrassing reminder of the past on an otherwise modern and civilised land. Papa Dear’s role as a travelling activist introduces a gendered element to the racism that the family faces. Gladys, as a woman, is tied to her duties at home, so she can’t travel the country to fight for her people’s rights the way her father can.

Act 1, Scene 2 (A)

Summary

Gladys listens to a radio quiz show and answers the questions along with the contestants, getting every one of them correct even as the contestants make mistakes. Dolly suggests Gladys try to become a contestant on the show, but Gladys laughs off the idea of an Aboriginal contestant. Nan Dear asks Dolly about a boy she was talking to. Dolly frustratedly explains that she won’t be marrying him because he is her cousin, as are most boys her age in the community. Gladys insists that Dolly won’t marry anyone until she finishes her studies, which prompts Dolly to ask how old Nan Dear was when she married. Nan Dear doesn’t answer. Dolly leaves to study, and Nan Dear questions Gladys for “put[ting] ideas” into Dolly’s head about the possibilities for her life. Nan Dear leaves to tend to a neighbour, Ester, who is pregnant and injured after an altercation with her abusive White husband. Gladys confesses she didn’t know about Ester’s predicament, and Nan Dear recommends Gladys pay less attention to the radio and more attention to the world around her.

Analysis

Though Gladys fantasies about a better life, she is also well aware of the limitations she faces. She is familiar enough with racism to know that the radio quiz show would never accept her, despite her intelligence. Nan Dear believes optimism is dangerous, since she is convinced that the world will never improve enough for Dolly to have the kind of life Gladys dreams for her. At the same time, Nan Dear’s inquiry into the boys Dolly spends time with highlights that she is concerned about Dolly’s future in her own way. Ester’s situation demonstrates how gendered and racial dynamics play out within the home. As an Aboriginal woman, Ester has almost no power against the White man she is married to, with her only option being to turn to the members of her community for help.

Act 1 Scene 2 (B)

Summary

A young White man, Errol Fisher, gets lost and ends up at the humpy. He encounters Dolly, and though the two don’t speak to each other, mutual attraction sparks between them. Errol then approaches Gladys and tries to sell her the encyclopedias he is peddling. Gladys turns him away at first, but she takes pity on Errol and allows him to present his rehearsed pitch. As Errol presents the encyclopedias, Gladys becomes interested in them, especially when Errol notes that a previous radio quiz show winner succeeded by studying an encyclopedia. He adds that encyclopedias are good for school-aged children, and Gladys has a fantasy of Dolly graduating in a cap and gown. Nan Dear enters and is skeptical of Errol and his encyclopedias. She asks what the books say about Aboriginal people, and Errol doesn’t know the answer. Gladys considers buying a set of encyclopedias, but Nan Dear stops her as Dolly comes in and helps Errol sort out his map. She explains that he should go to the White neighbourhood to sell his encyclopedias, and Gladys and Nan Dear agree. Errol departs, but he leaves his calling card, and Gladys is charmed by his manners and his descriptions of the encyclopedia. As Errol prepares to leave, Dolly asks him about his home in Melbourne and teases him for coming out to her family’s humpy in The Flats. She is confused and uncertain whether he returns her attraction to him. She helps him retrieve his bicycle from some local little boys, then returns to her family. Nan Dear advises Dolly to be careful what kind of men she spends time with. Gladys considers Errol’s card.

Analysis

Errol is out of place in the humpies among The Flats, but he nervously recites the same rehearsed pitch he presents to potential White buyers. His sales pitch appeals to Gladys’s curiosity and thirst for knowledge as well as her desire for a better life for Dolly, and she once again loses herself in a fantasy sequence. Her imagined future for Dolly as a graduate seems as impossible as a dream, but Gladys believes in it. Nan Dear’s implication that the encyclopedias likely contain untrue and offensive information about Aboriginal people highlights how structures of knowledge and learning are not separate from the racism that informs other cultural institutions. Nan Dear, Dolly, and Gladys all agree that White people are the intended audience for encyclopedias, but Gladys is still tempted by the books’ promise of knowledge. Neither Dolly nor Errol are fully comfortable making conversation with someone of a different race, but Dolly finds herself enjoying Errol’s company and helps him when local boys see him as an easy target for theft. Her attraction to Errol leaves Dolly confused, and Nan Dear and Gladys’s differing reactions to Errol don’t help that confusion. Gladys is intrigued by Errol, while Nan Dear sees him as a threat.

Act 1, Scene 3

Summary

Dolly rummages for linoleum at the town dump, where she fantasies about buying linoleum from an attractive salesman as a posh customer. Her fantasy fades and she returns home, passing a drunk cousin of hers (Leon) who invites her to a party. She turns him down and continues to the humpy, where Nan Dear scolds her for taking an unsafe route home. She scolds her further when Dolly refers derisively to her cousins who live along the route, insisting that “they’ve had it hard” but refusing to explain further. Errol returns to the humpy to see if Gladys wants to buy an encyclopedia set. Gladys is at work, so Errol invites himself to wait as an excuse to spend time with Dolly. She won’t let him inside the humpy, but they make conversation outside. After he unthinkingly makes a racist remark, he apologies and asks if Dolly wants to ride his bike with him. She gets on the bike by herself and rides around him, humming, and the two sing together. Dolly and Errol talk about their lives. Errol enjoys the freedom of being a travelling salesman, while Dolly has no sense of that freedom; she expects she will pick fruit or work at the cannery like everyone else in her community. When Errol asks what Dolly dreams of doing, she muses that she would like to be a model or a nurse, but she knows her mother has other dreams for her. Gladys arrives with a bookshelf from the dump, and, not noticing Dolly’s embarrassment, explains to Errol that the landfill plot is a desirable piece of land and should have been designated for Aboriginal housing but was instead made into the dump. Dolly leaves, humiliated, and Errol tries to salvage the situation by assessing that the bookshelf just needs a coat of paint. Gladys agrees, and the two laugh.

Analysis

Like her mother, Dolly escapes into fantasy sequences to imagine a world where more possibilities are open to her. Nan Dear, on the other hand, remains unwaveringly practical. She both sympathies with Dolly’s cousins and recognises some of them (such as Leon) as a threat to Dolly’s safety. Nan Dear believes in the importance of family, and that demands empathy, even for relatives she dislikes. Errol likes Dolly, but he was born and raised in a culture that fundamentally disrespects her and her culture. As such, he carries racist biases that he must consciously try to unlearn. Dolly, for her part, forgives Errol’s casual racism and opens herself up to enjoying her time with him, seemingly in part because he tries to shed his racist biases. The freedom that Errol enjoys, and Dolly’s lack of that same freedom, comes from both racism and sexism. The racism of Australian society prevents Dolly and her community from holding any kind of job besides manual labour, while sexist expectations of a woman’s role in the home keep Dolly tied to her house and unable to travel like Errol can. Dolly is embarrassed that Errol knows her family gets furniture from the dump. She is already aware that, according to the commonplace racism in their society, she and her family are of a lower status than Errol, and their secondhand furniture is a symbol of that to her. However, reclaiming abandoned furniture from a spot that should have been made into Aboriginal housing is actually a small act of resistance, as the family finds a way to use that land to make a home despite it being zoned as a landfill.

Act 1 Scene 4

Summary

Dolly sets the table as Gladys serves dinner. Nan Dear complains that they haven’t set a place for Papa Dear. Dolly and Gladys point out that Papa Dear has been preaching across Western Australia for three months, but Dolly sets a place for him anyway. In a fantasy sequence, Papa Dear returns home and kisses Gladys on the top of the head before disappearing. Dolly tells Gladys and Nan Dear about a job as a cashier that she hopes to get, but Nan Dear dismisses this and declares that Dolly will continue to work with them at the cannery. She sends Dolly out of the room and tells Gladys that a store in town will never hire an Aboriginal girl from The Flats.

Analysis

Gladys’s fantasy sequence about Papa Dear makes clear that she shares Nan Dear’s belief in the importance of family. Gladys wants her family to be reunited, and she wants her father’s affection. This emphasises the family dynamics present in the play; Gladys is a mother, but she is also a daughter, and she misses her father. Gladys and Nan Dear continue to butt heads over Dolly’s future. Nan Dear sending Dolly out of the room reveals that even though Nan Dear previously chastised Gladys for giving Dolly false hope for the future, Nan Dear doesn’t want Dolly to hear how limited Nan Dear believes her prospects really are.

Act 1, Scene 5

Summary

Errol is in the humpy, much to Dolly’s embarrassment, as he sells Gladys an encyclopedia set to be paid for in instalments. She recounts earning money by picking fruit and remembers Dolly sleeping peacefully as a baby among the carpet snakes in the tree paddocks. Errol asks if the snakes hurt young Dolly, and the women are confused by the question since carpet snakes are harmless. Errol gives Gladys Volume A of the encyclopedia, and she displays it proudly on her new bookcase. Dolly talks to Errol outside the humpy, and he asks why the family lives in The Flats. As Dolly replies that Nan Dear and Gladys like to live among their community, Gladys steps outside and gestures encouragingly to Errol. He stammers nervously until Gladys tells Dolly that Errol wants to invite her to a dance in town. Dolly refuses, knowing that the townspeople won’t accept Errol bringing an Aboriginal girl from The Flats as a date, but Errol insists he doesn’t care. Dolly cautiously agrees to the date before Nan Dear comes outside and sternly tells Errol to leave.

Analysis

Errol’s misunderstanding of the story about the carpet snake emphasises how removed he is from the cultural context that informs the way Gladys is telling the story. Errol’s cultural context is expressed in the encyclopedias rather than through oral stories or practical knowledge, and it is positioned as the default culture that Aboriginal Australians must assimilate to. The family’s decision to remain in The Flats despite the constant flooding highlights how home, for these women, is not only a matter of physical shelter but also of community. Gladys continues to extend her approval of Errol by encouraging the relationship between him and Dolly, while Nan Dear continues to make clear that she does not want to include Errol in their community. The abuse the neighbour Ester experiences at her White husband’s hands perhaps informs Nan Dear’s opinion.

Act 1, Scene 6

Summary

A White Inspector surveys the living conditions in the humpy, making strained conversation with an anxious Gladys and Nan Dear. He is a representative of the government of Victoria, making a report on how Aboriginal people live, and he patronisingly tells Gladys and Nan Dear that they must assimilate to White culture. Dolly enters and the Inspector sees her before Gladys and Nan Dear can shoo her away. After he leaves, Gladys and Nan Dear discuss the possibility of the government taking Dolly away.

Analysis

Gladys and Nan Dear’s fear of the Inspector comes from his status as a representative of the Aborigines Protection Board, which held absolute authority over the Aboriginal population of New South Wales. It had, among other privileges, the right to displace Aboriginal people from their homes and the right to remove Aboriginal children from their families. Gladys and Nan Dear are keenly aware that the Inspector has the power to break their family apart, which is why they try to shoo Dolly away before he can see her.

Act 1, Scene 7

Summary

Gladys gets dressed for a fundraiser. Gladys’s uncle intends to raise money to negotiate for Daish’s, the dump, in order to build Aboriginal housing. He plans to call the housing Rumbalara, which means “end of the rainbow.” Meanwhile, Dolly gets dressed for her date with Errol, and Nan Dear and Gladys are astonished at how beautiful she looks. When Nan Dear learns that Dolly is going out with Errol, however, she pretends to have a coughing fit, and Dolly cancels her plans to tend to Nan Dear.

Analysis

Though the local government denied Daish’s to the Aboriginal community, the community continues to fight for it and for access to stable housing. Meanwhile, Nan Dear makes clear her distrust of Errol and her dislike of his burgeoning relationship with Dolly. Nan Dear believes that a relationship with a White boy will endanger Dolly, so she keeps her from the dance in an attempt to protect her granddaughter.

Act 1, Scene 8

Summary

Dolly returns from school to find Nan Dear doing the laundry. Dolly reports that Ester’s sons were not at school, and that everyone suspects it’s because of the Inspector. Gladys gets home and tells Dolly about a trainee program she heard about, and Dolly is frustrated that her mother wants her to act like a White person. Gladys asserts that she wants for Dolly what any mother wants. Dolly asks Nan Dear why they can’t have “a normal life,” to which Nan Dear responds that their life is normal and there is no point in trying to change it. They discuss the upcoming Miss Marroopna-Shepparton Ball, and Nan Dear promises that she will make Dolly a new dress for the occasion.

Analysis

Ester once again represents the level of powerlessness that Aboriginal women, as doubly marginalised people, must contend with. The government takes away her children, robbing her of her status as a mother. Gladys tries to be the mother Dolly needs, but Dolly is frustrated that her mother is teaching her to get by in White society rather than how to thrive in Aboriginal society. Dolly faces the constant expectation to assimilate into White culture, which makes her resentful of her own Aboriginal identity. Nan Dear encourages her to embrace her identity and traditions, but she does this by denying that any other kind of life is possible.

Act 1, Scene 9

Summary

Gladys talks to Dolly about the latest book of the encyclopedia to arrive. She tells Dolly that Errol asked about her and encourages Dolly to go to the ball with him. The conversation gets tense as Gladys urges Dolly “not to let them shame you.” Dolly counters that Gladys should take her own advice. Nan Dear enters, and Gladys asserts to her that Gladys is Dolly’s mother, and she wants Errol to walk Dolly home from the ball.

Analysis

Gladys and Dolly both struggle with the conflicting impulses to assimilate into White society and to honor their Aboriginal identities. This tension is complicated by the mother-daughter relationships at play. In this scene, Dolly stands up to Gladys while Gladys does the same to Nan Dear.

Act 1, Scene 10

Summary

Gladys speaks to a bank manager and asks him to hire Dolly as a teller. Elsewhere, Dolly picks fruit and sings to herself. The bank manager questions Dolly’s qualifications and continues to come up with reasons to deny her the job even as Gladys rebuts each of his points. Gladys remembers Dolly asking if she lets White people shame her, and Gladys insists that the bank manager offer Dolly a job. He agrees to interview Dolly.

Analysis

Gladys has high hopes for Dolly’s future, and she sets out to make those dreams a reality by visiting the bank and confronting the manager. Her desire to do right by Dolly inspires Gladys to voice her frustration with the racist systems around her, and she is further empowered by her belief in her and her family’s capability to make a better world for themselves.

Act 1, Scene 11

Summary

Dolly dances with Errol at the ball. She is uncomfortably aware that the White partygoers are staring at them, but Errol comforts her, and she imagines winning the title of Miss Mooroopna-Shepparton Ball. Instead, the winner is a White girl who mocks Dolly for wearing a dress made of fabric scrounged from the dump. Dolly, humiliated, flees the dance and is confronted by her drunk cousin Leon. To punish Dolly for acting as if she is too good for her community, Leon attacks her and Errol, tearing Dolly’s dress and leaving Errol with a black eye.

Analysis

Dolly is uncomfortable and conscious of the danger around her, but she lets herself dream about a world in which racism wouldn’t prevent her from winning Miss Mooroopna-Shepparton Ball. Though the play usually depicts this kind of daydreaming as an act of protective optimism, in this case, Dolly’s hope for a better world is cruelly punished by humiliation and violence.

Act 1, Scene 12

Summary

At the humpy, Gladys thinks happily of how much fun Dolly must be having. However, Nan Dear senses that something is wrong.

Analysis

Just as the violence that ensued after Dolly’s fantasy sequence shows the hazards of losing oneself in imagination, Nan Dear now demonstrates that her cynicism and wariness are sometimes more realistic than Gladys’s optimism.

Act 1, Scene 13

Summary

Errol finds Dolly crying at the river, and she helps treat his black eye. Though her dress is ruined, Errol reassures Dolly that she is still beautiful. They tell each other about their families, and Dolly explains that she grew up with three older brothers and 27 male cousins, which taught her how to fight. Errol marvels at her stories of her family. His father is strict and traditional, and he insists that Errol’s mother is “too delicate” to get a job. Errol and Dolly get closer, but Dolly pulls away, unwilling to subject either of them to the trials of an interracial relationship. Errol counters that the two of them could move to the city together for a better world. But Dolly argues that Errol’s version of a better world is different from hers; her priority is staying with her family, since that is what home means to her. They fight, but she definitively turns him down and at last Errol accepts this. Dolly starts to walk home, but she is intercepted by Leon.

Analysis

Errol and Dolly have grown up with vastly different conceptions of family, both of which are shaped by their race and culture. Errol’s version of family is the strict nuclear family that is prioritized and rewarded by White Australian culture. He was born into his family but has no real attachment to it. Dolly, on the other hand, has meaningful connections with her entire extended family. She is also used to manual labor being expected of everyone, including women, while Errol’s family embodies a kind of White middle-class gender roles that forbid women from working. Errol’s unthinking and casual racism has not yet hindered his relationship with Dolly because she knows he is well-meaning. However, it proves the undoing of their relationship as Errol can’t wrap his head around the fact that Dolly doesn’t want to leave her community to live an assimilated life with him in the city. She is also more aware of the violence interracial couples face, since she is generally more aware of racism than Errol.

Act 1, Scene 14

Summary

Nan Dear and Gladys pack up the humpy, preparing to evacuate for a flood. They worry about Dolly, who is still not home. A jungi (policeman) arrives to escort them to the dump, which is the evacuation site since it is above the flood line. They are about to leave when Dolly arrives, clearly traumatised. She “half screams” when the jungi tries to touch her, and she refuses to answer when Gladys and Nan Dear ask her what happened.

Analysis

The flood mirrors Dolly’s implied rape by her cousin Leon. The flood will destroy the humpy, emphasising the instability of Dolly’s home. Meanwhile, Leon’s assault destabilises not only Dolly herself but also her connections to her community. Her trauma is obvious to those around her, but she refuses to share the truth with Gladys and Nan Dear, with whom she is usually so open.

Act 2, Scene 1

Summary

The women return to the humpy after the flood. Dolly, still “shell-shocked,” continues to wear the dress she wore to the ball, which is now badly torn and muddied. Nan Dear and Gladys clean up the wreckage, and Gladys finds that all her encyclopedias are ruined. She insists she doesn’t mind. Nan Dear muses that moving to the Rumbalara housing development might be a good idea for Dolly. Nan Dear and Gladys decide not to tell Papa Dear what happened to Dolly, since it’s “women’s business.” Errol arrives to apologise to Dolly, but Nan Dear and Gladys violently drive him away, assuming that he is the one who assaulted Dolly. Errol is devastated that they could think him capable of such a thing, and after he leaves, Dolly finally speaks, confirming that Errol was not the one who raped her.

Analysis

Dolly in unable to move past the horror of being assaulted, and her refusal to change her dress reflects how stuck she is in the moment of her trauma. Nan Dear and Gladys, meanwhile, try to rebuild both Dolly and the humpy. The family lives in The Flats to be with their family and community, but they are prepared to move to Rumbalara now that Nan Dear believes Rumbalara might help Dolly. Nan Dear and Gladys agree that the attack on Dolly, and the work of helping her recover, are “women’s business,” which reflects that Papa Dear might be an important activist, but he is not privy to the equally important work that goes on in his own home. Gladys’s affection for Errol is gone now that she suspects him of hurting her daughter, which emphasises how committed she is to protecting Dolly. Dolly, on the other hand, retains some loyalty to Errol, breaking her silence to make sure her family knows that he is innocent.

Act 2, Scene 2

Summary

A bulldozer destroys the family’s humpy, and they move into a sterile, small, and unwelcoming concrete house at Rumbalara. Nan Dear develops a cough, but she promises to make curtains for the new house.

Analysis

The family leaves behind the humpy, which was unstable but familiar and made by the women themselves, for the distinctly unhomelike Rumbalara housing. The contrast between the humpy and Rumbalara illuminates the injustice of needing to choose between safe housing and emotionally fulfilling housing. Nan Dear’s promise to liven up the house with curtains also underscores the importance of domestic labour in making a house a home.

Act 2, Scene 3

Summary

Gladys brings Dolly a letter from the bank, but Dolly is uninterested in the job as teller. She tells her mother that this job is Gladys’s dream, not Dolly’s, and angrily suggests Gladys “fix [her] own house.” Gladys storms out. Nan Dear pays the rent to the rent collector Mr. Coody and helps Dolly prepare a meal. They listen to the radio and are surprised to hear Gladys arguing with city councilors about building Aboriginal housing at Daish’s. Gladys is removed from the council meeting, but Dolly and Nan Dear are still proud of her.

Analysis

Though the family has left the humpy, the image of rebuilding a house returns as Dolly uses a person’s house as a symbol for their life. This metaphor emphasises the importance Dolly and her family place on home. It also signifies how the sterile Rumbalara housing is causing fractures within the home, which was previously a space where the family is united. Mr. Coody’s arrival to collect rent makes clear that the family’s home is not their own anymore; it is owned and controlled by Mr. Coody. In light of this lack of control, Gladys asserts her dignity and a level of control by refusing to let the city councillors debate her life without her input.

Act 2, Scene 4

Summary

Errol finds Gladys cutting wood outside the Rumbalara housing. They discuss the encyclopedias. Gladys doesn’t want to buy any more sets of encyclopedias, and even though she isn’t supposed to be able to cancel her contract, Errol offers to help her do so. Gladys reveals she can’t read or write, so Errol helps her compose the required letter. Gladys apologies for assuming that Errol raped Dolly, and Errol asks if he can see Dolly. Gladys gently but firmly tells him that Dolly won’t want to see him. Gladys asks Errol to teach her to read and write. She explains that Nan Dear attended school when the family lived at Cummeragunja, but the mission managers were cruel, and Gladys was sent off at a young age to do domestic work for a large wealthy family. After that, she married Dolly’s father and was too busy working and raising children to learn. Errol agrees to teach Gladys to read. He still hasn’t given up hope of reuniting with Dolly, and he hopes to prove that he “can be worthy of her.”

Analysis

Gladys has always liked Errol as a boy who might be good for Dolly, but now their relationship begins to develop on its own terms. She priorities Dolly’s emotional well being over Errol’s desire to see her, but she nonetheless treats Errol with gentle respect. Gladys is the kind of strong and caring mother that Errol never had, and she allows herself to be vulnerable with him when she reveals she can’t read or write. The reasons behind Gladys’s illiteracy are a combination of racism and sexism. She didn’t learn to read as a child because the Aborigines Protection Board removed her from her family, who were already being mistreated at Cummeragunja. By the time she was old enough to escape the most controlling parts of the Aborigines Protection Board, Gladys found herself where she is now––bound by the domestic responsibilities expected of her as a woman.

Act 2, Scene 5

Summary

The rent collector Mr. Coody comes by for the rent and finds Nan Dear cooking eggs for a heavily pregnant Dolly. He notes that he will re-evaluate their lease in light of Dolly’s pregnancy, but Nan Dear takes him to task for consistently invading his tenants’ privacy and imposing too many rules. She throws eggs at him, and he flees. Dolly and Nan Dear look at Dolly’s old family tree assignment, and Dolly shares that she plans to name her baby after Papa Dear. She confesses that she is worried she won’t love the baby. Nan Dear says that Dolly is finally a woman and thus ready to hear the truth: Gladys’s biological father is a White man who raped Nan Dear when she was a teenager. This is why Nan Dear has been so opposed to Dolly’s relationship with Errol––especially because Errol has the same surname as Nan Dear’s attacker. When Dolly shares that she broke things off with Errol because he wants to take her away from her family, Nan Dear encourages her to consider leaving. At the same time, she insists that Dolly mustn’t resume her relationship with Errol, and Dolly agrees.

Analysis

Mr. Coody represents how White authority infringes on every moment of Aboriginal life by denying Aboriginal people privacy and self-determination within their own homes. Nan Dear resists this facet of White supremacy, asserting her home as a space she and her family have the right to control. While Dolly’s belief in the importance of family led to tension with Errol, it connects her to Nan Dear, who believes the same thing. Dolly’s decision to name the baby after Papa Dear reaffirms her devotion to her family and her desire to honor her relative’s traditions and legacy. Nan Dear values tradition as well, but she also wants Dolly to make a meaningful future for herself, which is why she encourages Dolly to leave. Nan Dear’s dislike of Errol is also explained as not simply a general protectiveness of her granddaughter but a reaction to trauma that she now chooses to share with Dolly in a moment of honesty and vulnerability.

Act 2, Scene 6

Summary

Errol is teaching Gladys to read, and they practice on the Inspector’s report of Aboriginal living conditions. Gladys encourages Errol to call her “Aunty” and asks about his feelings for Dolly. He admits that he still has feelings for her, but he doesn’t know what she wants. Gladys invites Errol to a public meeting in Melbourne the following week, where Papa Dear will be speaking on Aboriginal housing. He accepts the invitation, and Gladys tells Errol that even if he and Dolly don’t end up together, she likes Errol.

Analysis

Errol loves Dolly, but that love has been hindered by a lack of understanding of where she comes from. Now Errol is developing a love for Dolly’s family and an appreciation for her community that mirror Dolly’s own loyalty to her loved ones. Gladys’s request that Errol call her “Aunty” is an invitation to join the family, which demonstrates that her understanding of family is inclusive and based on personal bonds. This contrasts Errol’s previous understanding of family as something constraining and dependent only on blood ties.

Act 2, Scene 7

Summary

Nan Dear, Gladys, and Dolly sit in the audience at the public meeting. With them is Dolly’s baby, Regina. Dolly says that Gladys should be giving a speech, but Nan Dear scoffs that speechmaking is “men’s business.” Dolly gave up on the bank interview, but she has been awarded a scholarship to a nursing program in Melbourne, and Gladys is thrilled for her. Errol arrives and sits with the women. Gladys pulls Nan Dear away to go find Papa Dear. Errol is startled to see Regina, and he finally realises what happened the night of the flood. He apologies to Dolly “for everything,” and she apologies in turn. He offers to move to the country so they can be together and stay near Dolly’s family, but she tells him that she is going to Melbourne and not ready for a romantic relationship. He respects her decision, and they agree to remain friends. Nan Dear returns, feeling ill, and Dolly hurries off to get her medicine. Errol offers to drive her to the hospital, and when she refuses, he offers to drive her home. She is touched. Nan Dear asks about Errol’s surname, and he explains that his parents adopted it after immigrating from Germany. Nan Dear is relieved that Errol is not related to the Fisher who once assaulted her. She kisses Errol on the cheek. Dolly sees this and is stunned. Papa Dear fails to arrive to present the petition, so Gladys does it in his stead. She makes a speech petitioning Queen Elizabeth to give Aboriginal Australians decent housing and to finally treat them like Australian citizens. She begins to read from the petition and includes her own opinions, demanding equal education and job opportunities for Aboriginal people. She concludes with a point that is not on the petition: she wants White people to treat Aboriginal people with respect. The audience applauds. In a fantasy sequence, Nan Dear imagines Dolly and Errol, with Regina between them, getting married. She returns to reality and tells Dolly to marry Errol. She tells Errol to call her “Nan,” and he thanks her for her blessing. Papa Dear arrives.

Analysis

Dolly has grown up from a girl to a woman. She has chosen a future for herself and will be leaving the protection of her mother and grandmother, while she herself is tasked with raising the family’s next generation. Her family has faith in her ability to pursue a career, but Nan Dear still believes that woman and men’s work are divided categories, and that public activism belongs to men. Errol makes clear his devotion to Dolly in this scene. He unequivocally apologies, recognising his own wrongdoing, and promises to prioritise Dolly’s needs over his own wants. His desire for a relationship is not hindered at all by Dolly’s baby, which emphasises that he is ready to commit not only to Dolly but to her family. When Dolly turns him down, he respects her ability to choose what she wants and is just glad to stay in her life as a friend. Errol proves to Nan Dear that despite his occasional moments of unthinking racism, he is fundamentally kind and respectful. However, she cannot let herself like him until she knows for certain that he isn’t related to her rapist. When she learns that Errol has no connection to her past trauma, Nan Dear embraces him as a good choice for Dolly. Papa Dear’s work is important, as his speech provides Gladys the foundation to begin her public activism. However, Gladys soon steps out of Papa Dear’s shadow, asserting that politics and activism are just as much a place for women as they are for men. Her concerns about housing are informed by the domestic labour she has spent her life performing, but she can also see beyond that. Her demand that White people respect their Aboriginal countrymen indicates a holistic understanding of how microaggressions, and seemingly small moments of interpersonal disrespect, are the building blocks of White supremacist ideologies that lead to racist government policies. Tellingly, the final moment of the show is centred around the intimate workings of the family rather than the broader societal issues the play also explores. Nan Dear finally breaks free of her cynicism and indulges in a fantasy sequence, like her daughter and granddaughter often do, and that fantasy inspires her to invite Errol to join the family. As Errol, Dolly, and Regina come together to form the next generation of parents and children, Papa Dear arrives as a reminder of the family’s lineage.
