Part 04

Analytical Text Response Essays

Introduction

From the very beginning of your schooling career, you have been introduced to several different methods of writing essays. Although all structures have their advantages and disadvantages, it is essential that you have a fool-proof method of writing essays for your end of year examination.
Having a solid method of writing analytical essays and practicing these throughout the year will result in a strong foundation, enabling you to tackle any essay question with confidence. The key to success lies not only in understanding the content but also in being able to present your ideas in a clear, structured, and coherent manner. With a well-practised approach, you will be able to break down complex texts, analyse them effectively, and construct a well-supported argument that demonstrates both depth and clarity of thought.
In the following, we will explore a structured approach to writing an analytical text response. By familiarising yourself with this framework, you will be better equipped to organise your ideas, manage your time efficiently, and ultimately maximise your performance in the exam.

What is Needed in an Essay

An essay is a structured piece of writing that presents an argument, analysis, or narrative on a particular topic. The purpose of an essay is to communicate a clear, well-reasoned point of view, often backed by evidence and examples. Essays are typically composed of several key parts: an introduction, body paragraphs, and a conclusion. Each section plays a specific role in conveying the writer’s ideas and helping the reader understand and engage with the topic at hand.

Introduction

The introduction serves as the starting point of the essay, setting the stage for the discussion to follow. It introduces the topic, provides necessary background information, and presents the thesis statement, which outlines the main argument or purpose of the essay. A strong introduction grabs the reader’s attention, often through a compelling hook, and establishes the importance of the topic. It should also provide a clear roadmap of what the essay will cover, guiding the reader’s expectations and providing a sense of structure.

Body Paragraphs

The body of an essay is where the main arguments or points are developed and supported with evidence, examples, or analysis. Typically, an essay contains three body paragraphs, although this can vary depending on the length and depth of the topic. Each paragraph should focus on a single aspect of the thesis statement and contribute to the overall argument.

Conclusion

The conclusion is the final part of the essay, where the writer sums up the key points made in the body paragraphs and reinforces the thesis statement. It provides closure to the discussion and leaves a lasting impression on the reader. A strong conclusion restates the main argument in a fresh way, summarising the key insights presented in the essay. It may also offer suggestions for further exploration or suggest the broader implications of the topic.

Planning

Deconstructing the Prompt

When first planning your essay, you must directly respond to the prompt that is presented to you, and consider the key authorial messages that could help you respond with insight. This involves three important steps:

  • Find the key concepts. This will most likely correlate with the key concepts you have studied throughout the unit, you should also make sure to identify synonyms of these concepts too!

  • Find any instructional verbs. These are words that instruct you to answer the prompt in a certain manner, in English the main verbs to look out for is discuss, do you agree, to what extent or how.

  • Identify any expectations/limitations. These ore words that limit what you con include in your essays and set on expectation on what you should be discussing.

Example prompt: ‘The dream sequences in Rainbow’s End are the characters’ only escape from the depressing reality of life.’ Do you agree?
This is a very straightforward prompt—you need to demonstrate a multiple-sided response to what Harrison perceives as the role of dreams and aspirations in her characters’ lives. There are no limits to what should be discussed here, but you are expected to focus your discussion on the ideas of dreams, escape, and reality as they relate to the values, identities, and choices of Nan Dear, Gladys, and Dolly. Consider the historical context of 1950s assimilationist Australia, racism, class, and the characters’ evolving sense of agency throughout the play.

Contention

Now that you understand what the prompt is asking, we can begin to think about what our interpretation of Harrison’s message is. Let’s consider the first prompt again:
‘The dream sequences in Rainbow’s End are the characters’ only escape from the depressing reality of life.’ Do you agree?
We can approach this in multiple ways. Here are three simplistic interpretations (agree, disagree, somewhere in between):

  • Harrison suggests that dreams offer the characters comfort, but they cannot change the reality of their circumstances.

  • Harrison implies that dreams are the characters’ only refuge from a society that denies them dignity and opportunity.

  • Harrison demonstrates that it is action, not dreaming, that ultimately allows the characters to escape their circumstances.

However, these interpretations are quite general and lack specificity. They don’t tell the reader exactly what you are arguing. Let’s improve them with more precise language and deeper knowledge of the text:

  • Harrison portrays dreams as both sustaining and limiting, suggesting that Gladys’s fantasies of meeting the Queen preserve her dignity while simultaneously revealing how deeply colonial ideals of acceptance have been internalised.

  • Harrison implies that, for a family denied housing, education, and respect, the imagination becomes a vital site of survival and self-worth in a society that offers no material escape.

  • Harrison critiques passive dreaming by charting Gladys’s transformation from daydreamer to activist, suggesting that genuine escape from injustice comes not from fantasy but from finding one’s voice and demanding change.

These improved interpretations use specific language, engage with the complexity of the characters’ motivations, and demonstrate a sophisticated understanding of the play’s ideas.

Specificity and Relevancy

Examiners punish vague or general statements, especially those that lack clear links to the topic or the author’s message. For example:
By emphasising the importance of dreams in the play, Harrison shows that they matter to Gladys.
This is not technically incorrect—but it is far too general and superficial. It doesn’t demonstrate insightful analysis, nor does it show a clear understanding of Harrison’s authorial intent. This kind of vague sentence would not receive full marks.
Here’s an improved version:
By emphasising Gladys’s fantasies of royal recognition, Harrison portrays how dreams function not as an escape from reality, but as a fragile shield of dignity for those denied respect and opportunity by an assimilationist society.
This is better. It is clear, specific, well-structured, and shows awareness of both the theme (dreams vs reality), character motivation, and authorial message. It also directly links to the contention: that dreams sustain the characters, even in circumstances that offer no material escape.
The issue you will face is that there is only so much you can discuss in your paragraph from here, as you have only examined one idea—dreams sustain dignity. To fix this, lets’ go back to an old friend:

Because, But, So - Make a Complex Sentence!

Too often, students fail to complete long paragraphs because they are stuck repeating themselves or have run out of things to say about their topic they introduced. Therefore, if we have two ideas in our topic sentence, we can ’hinge’ these sentences together which demonstrate complex understanding, writing and structure - all of them are key parts of the rubric you are judged on! Here’s an example:

By emphasising Gladys’s daydreams of royal recognition, Harrison conveys how, for a family denied dignity and opportunity, the imagination becomes a vital refuge from an unjust reality—ultimately suggesting that dreams alone cannot deliver change, and that true escape demands the courage to speak and act.

Now, you have two linked ideas:

  • Dreams preserve dignity and hope for the characters in an unjust society.

  • Dreaming alone, without action, cannot deliver genuine change.

This provides a clear structure for an entire paragraph and sets you up to use a hinge sentence later to develop further layers—e.g., how this ’dreaming” can also reveal internalised colonial ideals or delay confrontation with injustice.
By planning your paragraph in this way, you’ll ensure:

  • Specific and sophisticated analysis

  • Strong authorial intent

  • Complex understanding of the characters’ motivations and Harrison’s critique of assimilationist policies in 1950s Australia

Analytical Text Response Structure

Topics

There are three types of questions you may receive on a SAC or exam. These are the ’Discuss’, ’To what extent’ and ’Do you agree?’ statements. These are all tackled in a slightly varying manner.

  • Discuss : This is probably the most common type of prompt that you will encounter. When dealing with such prompts, it is important that you discuss as many aspects of it as possible. One way to improve your discussion here is to include a paragraph that rebuts the prompt, like what you do in your persuasive essays. However, since it is a discuss prompt, you do not need to make the two sides of your argument extremely distinct.

  • To what extent: These topics require you to make a decision. Never say ’it is to some extent’ in your contention. You really need to assign a portion of something. The best way to do this is to start with subordinating conjunctions like ’whilst” or ’although”. To What Extent questions rarely want you to agree or disagree - they want you to explore a shade of grey.

  • How: The ’How’ topics usually focus on the construction of a text - your evidence should contain metalanguage like ’characterisation’ for instance.

Introduction

The introduction is the first piece of your writing that will be examined. For this reason, it should be clear, direct and show your understanding of the topic. The introduction is there to show your examiner how you wish to respond to the set topic.
If your topic contains a quote, ensure that you mention it somewhere in your introduction to show you are addressing the topic.

Contention
A contention is the author’s point of view on the topic. Since you are the author in this scenario, you need to write what your position on the topic. A contention should be short, to the point, and engage with the topic. On this falls the crux of your essay.
Do not be afraid to agree/disagree with a topic. You are allowed to have a conditional contention.

Body Paragraphs

The body paragraphs form majority of your essay. The body paragraphs are the main reasons that the contention your provided is true. Your body paragraphs need to be able to demonstrate your understanding of the text and the fluency of your language. You should ensure that every sentence (and thus every word) brings something towards your argument.

Structure

Below is the structure for a body paragraph using ’Th.E. S.A.M.L.’ Method. This method of writing body paragraphs was developed in order to maximise possible marks. Only the ’TH’ and ’L’ elements of this structure need to be in the order given.

Th

Thematic-Based Topic Sentences.
Your topic sentence needs to be based upon a theme in the text.
Example:

’Norma Desmond’s obsession with herself ultimately leads to her downfall.’
vs.
’Obsession with self is what ultimately leads to the downfall of characters.’

E

Evidence.
This is where you put various quotes and instances for the text.

  • Quotes

  • Scenes

S

Symbols.
What symbols in the text relate to this theme?

  • Symbols

  • Historical and Social Context

  • Setting

A

Authorial intention.
What was the author intending?

M

Metalanguage.
Discuss the various metalanguage present regarding this theme.

L

Linking Sentence.
Link back to your contention.

Basic Example ’Th.E S.A.M’

’Obsession with one’s self is what ultimately leads to the downfall of characters. This can be seen with Norma exclaiming ‘who else?’ when Joe questions her about her script. The script itself is also a representation of Norma’s obsession as she has written several hundred pages where she stars in every scene. Wilder uses this to demonstrate that Norma is unable to move on from anything besides herself. The slow camera pan whenever the script is shown foreshadows Norma’s impending doom which links is inherently linked to her self-absorption. In this way, obsession with self ultimately leads to the downfall of several characters in Sunset Boulevard.’

Hinge Sentences

In your topic sentence, you can introduce two related ideas. This creates room for a more complex, insightful paragraph. But remember: when you shift focus from the first idea to the second, you must signal this change to the reader clearly. This is where the hinge sentence comes in.
A hinge sentence acts like a bridge—it acknowledges what you’ve already explored, then transitions to the second idea you introduced in your topic sentence. Here’s how that works:

By emphasising Gladys’s daydreams of royal recognition, Harrison conveys how, for a family denied dignity and opportunity, the imagination becomes a vital refuge from an unjust reality—ultimately suggesting that dreams alone cannot deliver change, and that true escape demands the courage to speak and act.

Evidence + Analysis of First Idea

In the dream sequences where Gladys imagines being received by the Queen, Harrison frames fantasy not as idle escapism but as a shield of dignity for a woman excluded from the “decent” society she longs to join. Yet Nan Dear’s blunt dismissal—“And what bloody good would that do? Daydreams!” (p. 154)—exposes the limits of this refuge, highlighting how dreaming leaves the family’s material circumstances untouched.

Hinge Sentence (transitioning to Second idea)

However, while Gladys’s dreams preserve her sense of self-worth, Harrison also reveals that fantasy alone cannot deliver her family from injustice.

Analysis of Second Idea

Gladys’s transformation culminates in her address to the Rodney Shire Council, where she demands “proper schooling … The white people too — they need to be educated about us, and our ways” (p. 197). Harrison complicates the notion of escape by showing that it is voice and action, not fantasy, that begin to change the family’s circumstances, suggesting that dreams are only powerful when they inspire agency.
By using a hinge sentence, you show your reader that you’re not just stacking ideas but developing an argument, deepening your interpretation, and showing the author’s nuanced message.

Proofreading

Take a Break Before Proofreading

After finishing your essay, your mind is still fresh with ideas, but it may be too familiar with the text to catch all mistakes. Step away from the essay for at least 10–15 minutes. This will help you approach it with fresh eyes and a clearer mindset when you return to review it.

Read Aloud

Reading aloud forces you to slow down and focus on each word. It also helps you hear awkward phrasing or sentences that don’t flow well. Read your essay aloud, paying attention to how each sentence sounds. If something feels off or too long-winded, make adjustments.

Check for Structure and Coherence

A strong essay is logically structured with clear arguments and smooth transitions. Ensure each paragraph has a clear topic sentence. Verify your argument flows logically from one point to the next. Ensure your introduction sets up your essay well and your conclusion summarises without introducing new ideas.

Look for Clarity and Precision

Vague or unclear language can confuse your reader, even if the argument itself is solid. Check if any sentences can be more specific or concise. Remove unnecessary filler words like ’really,’ ’just,’ or ’quite.’ Replace vague words like ’things’ or ’stuff’ with more precise terms.

Verify Your Thesis and Topic Sentences

Your thesis statement should be the central focus of your essay, and topic sentences should align with it. Double-check that:

  • Your thesis is clear and present in the introduction.

  • Each body paragraph clearly supports your thesis with an argument or evidence.

  • Topic sentences match the content of their respective paragraphs.

Proofread for Grammar and Syntax

Grammar mistakes, awkward phrasing, or incorrect sentence structure can weaken the professionalism of your essay. Read through your essay for common mistakes (e.g., subject-verb agreement, incorrect punctuation, run-on sentences). Use tools like Grammarly or even Microsoft Word’s spelling/grammar checker for a quick second look.

Check for Word Choice and Tone

The right word choice ensures your ideas are clear and impactful. Avoid overly complex words unless they are necessary for the context. Ensure you maintain an academic tone throughout the essay. Replace informal language (e.g., ’kids’ or ’stuff’) with more formal, specific terms.

Look for Repetition

Repeating the same word or idea can weaken your essay’s overall impact. Identify words or phrases that appear too often. Use synonyms or restructure sentences to avoid redundancy. Consider varying sentence structures to keep the reader engaged.

Check Punctuation and Spacing

Simple punctuation errors or inconsistent spacing can distract the reader. Verify commas, full stops, and apostrophes are used correctly. Ensure there is proper spacing after full stops and between paragraphs.

Ask Someone Else to Read It

Fresh eyes can often spot mistakes you may have missed. Ask a peer, teacher, or family member to read your essay. Be open to their feedback and use it to improve your writing.

Ensure It Meets the Task Requirements

Your essay should always answer the question and meet the criteria specified in the prompt. Revisit the question to ensure your essay directly addresses it. Check that all key parts of the task are covered and that your argument is relevant.
