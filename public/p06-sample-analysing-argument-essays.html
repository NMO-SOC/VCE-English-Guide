The Guardian
I started using heroin decades ago. The rejection of a Melbourne CBD injecting room feels like a rejection of me

Anonymous
The government seems to want teams to go scoop up drug users and put them in treatment – whether they’re ready for it or not Fri 26 Apr 2024

I’ve used heroin off and on for decades now. I still do.
Throughout my 20s, I had used drugs in ways that were often destructive to me. Back then, I worked 40 hours a week in a paid job a lot of the time, but at other times I was homeless, seemingly always in withdrawal, and desperate for dollars or drugs.
Looking back, I was incredibly destructive. I ripped off shops, crashed cars and got in trouble with the cops. I had hepatitis C (of which I have been cured), have overdosed multiple times and developed infections from injecting – often in an alleyway or an abandoned house or a public toilet or a train.
While people have issues with alcohol and other acceptable drugs that make their lives difficult, they don’t go to jail just for buying or consuming their drugs. That fact – and the drive to avoid the police, along with the awful reality of opioid withdrawal – has ensured my decision-making is sometimes focused on immediate relief or safety, rather than long-term health choices.
That is why harm reduction and injecting rooms – such as the one in North Richmond – are so crucial for keeping people alive and healthy.
When I heard the government in Victoria was not going ahead with the CBD injecting room, I felt both sad and angry. If you are using drugs and are dependent on them, you need somewhere safe to use. ’Treatment”, ’care coordination”, ’stigma-free care” and the rest of those nice words about getting people off drugs mean nothing when you are sick and hanging out and just want to get well. Injecting rooms work because they provide safety at this moment of vulnerability.

This immediate safety is something I might seek out if I was using in the city again. Then once that has been sorted, I maybe would seek other support. I have been let down and discriminated against so many times by so many health services I find it hard to ever trust them.
‘Will cost lives’: addiction experts condemn decision to scrap proposed safe injecting room in Melbourne’s CBD
For all those in the media and the CBD hoping to get rid of the people like me: I hope you know that people will continue to use drugs in your doorways or overdose in your back alleys, and know that people only care up until the point it inconveniences them. We are real people – who care, hurt, love and feel. But we are only ever defined by drug use.
It is so depressing that when I look at the list of things in the government’s ’action plan” released this week, there seems to be a focus on treatment. It looks, to a cynic like me, as though they are funding a massive team of people to go out and scoop up people and get them into treatment to be helped – whether they want it or not. Sprinkled in there are some things that may immediately save lives, like more naloxone and overdose assistance, but it really is a cold comfort next to the loss of the injecting room.
As a mate of mine said this week: ’Fuck, it’s so typical ... it was going good with methadone changes [that made it cheaper on the PBS] ... I had some money in my pocket and felt like a person again, but now they do this and make us feel like shit again.”
This feels like a betrayal at a fundamental level for someone like me, who has voted for this government and party for years.
It is not just about the injecting room. It is because it is so clear that there are votes in words like ’treatment” and ’care” – and listening to shop-owners – but not in doing difficult things for parts of the community who aren’t squeaky clean.
The rejection of the second injecting room feels like a rejection of me as a person, partly because the first injecting room here in Melbourne felt like, maybe, they really do see people like me as humans and part of society.
It feels as if I was very naive to believe that.
Even if all the other things they said they were going to do work out well, that rejection will hurt for a long time.

The author lives in Melbourne and has recieved treatement for his heroin use.

Sample Essay I

In their article for The Guardian Australia on the 26th of April 2024, titled ‘I started using heroin decades ago. The rejection of the Melbourne CBD injecting room feels like a rejection of me’, an anonymous author with a history of drug use describes how the decision not to open a second safe injecting room in Melbourne is an abandonment of drug users across the state. To an audience of mainly residents of Melbourne, but also to the wider Australian public, the author uses an upset and exasperated tone to outline how the Victorian Government’s decision to scrap plans for an injecting room in Melbourne’s CBD is essentially letting down those who use drugs and feel that safe injecting rooms are a critical part of the solution.
The author initially argues why drug users can be so hesitant to ask and accept help in regard to their drug use. Through the use of anecdotal evidence, they discuss some of the experiences they have been through while using drugs. By saying ’Looking back, I was incredibly destructive”, the author highlights to the audience how dire a situation can get for those using drugs and do not have the necessary support networks around them. As such, the audience get an insight into the mindset of a drug user and start to see things slightly more from their point of view. Thus, the audience are more inclined to develop a sense of empathy for drug users, as they can somewhat see what drug users go through without any help. Additionally, the author also appeals to the audience’s sense of fear by recounting some of the more unsavoury incidents that have occurred whilst they were on drugs. The author describes occasions of ’having hepatitis C, overdosing and developing infections”, designed to gently scare the audience into understanding some of the grim outcomes that exist with taking drugs unsupervised. The audience start to feel that this something that they do not want to see on the streets of the city, and consequently start to believe that actions need to be taken to reduce these sorts of incidents from happening out in the open. Furthermore, the author questions how users of alcohol and non-illicit drugs do not end up in jail, whilst people like them who use heroin are so quick to be arrested by police. By suggesting that users like them have a ’drive to avoid police”, the author uses reasoning and logic to demonstrate to the audience that the preconceptions by the police and the general public towards users is a major reason why they do not seek help. As such, the audience are more likely to see a different side to drug users, one that presents them as real humans. This causes the audience to feel uncomfortable and somewhat ashamed the drug users like the author cannot trust those who are wanting to help. This demonstrates how the author feels that drug users cannot ask for help without getting into trouble.
The author moves to point out how safe injecting rooms are actually a place of sanctuary and respite to drug users. They start by playing on the audience’s view of common sense by arguing that safe injecting rooms are ’so crucial” in keeping users safe. This challenges the audience’s previous ideas about the role of injecting rooms, as they may have previously seen them as facilities that only enhance addiction and increase drug use. As such, the audience are more likely to see the role of injecting rooms from a user’s perspective and start to build an understanding of why they should exist. The author also outlines some of the key phrases made by the Victorian government following their decision to not open an injecting room in the CBD. By describing these statements as ’nice words”, the author sarcastically calls out the government for their decision. The use of sarcasm helps the audience see how much drug users value injecting rooms, through their anger of the facility not opening. As a result, the audience are more likely to see the benefits of injecting rooms to users and potentially start to support them being opened in more locations across Melbourne. The author also highlights how injecting rooms provide ’immediate safety”, aimed to appeal to the audience’s sense of safety. This causes the audience to feel more sympathetic towards drug users as they now see how crucial they are when drug users are at their most vulnerable. As such, the audience are more likely to understand why drug users are so desperate for these facilities to remain in the city. This represents how crucial safe injecting rooms are viewed as by drug users, and how much they are needed by them.
The author also attacks the decisions made by the Victorian government regarding injecting rooms, suggesting that it does not properly address the major issue surrounding drug use. The tone of the article shifts to being more cynical towards the issue, demonstrated by the author describing the government’s action plan as ’depressing”. The audience are more inclined to feel more uncomfortable about the situation that drug users find themselves in and as such start to believe that the government’s policy regarding drug users may not be working in handling the wider issue. The author also includes an attack on the media, who may have sensationalised the risks that drug users present to the community. They argue that media only care about the issue until ’it inconveniences them”, causing the audience to question their trust of these media outlets and their descriptions of drug users. As such, the audience are more likely to sympathise with the author as they now have an insight into how all the perceptions and stigmas that are created by the media have a significant impact on the author, as well as other drug users. This results in the audience to further consider the type of media coverage that they might follow in regard to drug users. Additionally, the author also calls the alternative actions to the injecting rooms that government proposed as a ’cold comfort” to drug users. The combined use of a metaphor and alliteration summarises the author’s feelings regarding the abandonment of an injecting room in the CBD to the audience, resulting in them having a somewhat changed view on injecting rooms and how necessary they are to the issue of drug use. This causes the audience to not only have increased levels of empathy towards drug users and the issues that they face daily, but possibly also view the government’s work regarding drug intake as ineffective in handling this major issue. This could also result in the audience maybe losing a level of trust with the government to be effective in dealing with other issues. As such, the author is able to demonstrate how the Victorian Government has not made the appropriate decisions with handling drug use.
The controversial topic of safe injecting rooms is explored in a Guardian article by an individual with experiences of taking drugs such as heroin. Through the lived experiences and appeals at government, they argue how the decision not to open an injecting room in Melbourne’s CBD is not the right approach to take in responding to drug use. They strongly believe that injecting rooms keep users safe and healthy and should be a key part in the long-term solution to drug use.
