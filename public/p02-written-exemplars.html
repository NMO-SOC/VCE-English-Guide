Sample 1 - High Scoring Sunset Boulevard Essay I with Annotations

Topic: Sunset Boulevard reveals the dark side of celebrity and fame. Discuss. Introduction

Exposing the powerlessness of naïve ‘dream[s]’ of fulfilment against the toxicity of celebrity which has ‘seized [their] heart[s]’, (1) Billy Wilder’s quintessentially American (2) film Sunset Boulevard (1950) poignantly unveils the dark side of fame that is inextricable from Hollywood’s allure (3). Compounded by the skilful overlaying of a gothic lens, this noir mystery (4) performs as a deep meditation on the brutality of this business, and a sardonic, yet subtly sympathetic, depiction of the victims of its endless cycle of sadism (5). Through his intelligent blend of fiction and meta-cinema (6), Wilder’s iconoclastic opus emerges as a powerful vivisection of the dangerous illusions of stardom. Annotations:

(1) Beginning the piece with an embedded quote adds a degree of complexity and immediately highlights to markers that you have an understanding of the text. It also improves the poetic and sophisticated nature of the text, almost as if it is a literature essay, thus separating your piece from other purely analytical responses.

(2) Recognising the context from the outset also showcases one’s understanding of the wider societal issues and themes.

(3) An immediate reference to the topic is necessary. If the topic isn’t explicitly or implicitly addressed throughout the response, it cannot receive a high mark. Body Paragraphs

Personifying this complex world as a dystopian nightmare, Wilder satirically contextualises the hidden horrors of Hollywood (1). Immediately referencing a classic trope of film noir, (2) a disturbing amalgamation of fantasy and sobering realism is established through the eerie posthumous narration of this ‘poor dope’, with this non-linear structure exposing this inevitable tragedy from the outset. Heightening this strange sense of unease, (3) the foreboding non-diegetic string music of Franz Waxman’s pertinent score (4) warns the audience of this ensuing horror. Indeed, Sunset Boulevard itself can no longer be hopefully associated with fame and luxury; rather, its disconcerting introduction in a gutter with ‘dead leaves’ and ‘scraps of paper’ alludes to its burgeoning corruption, as literally emphasised by the titular finiteness of ‘sunset’. Ominously towering over this decrepit street, the silhouetted palm trees embody personified judges (5) of the ‘distorted’ and ‘blown out’ stories that ‘Hollywood columnists’ weave, as fortified by the intense chiaroscuro lighting (6). Similarly, (7) although swimming pools were an iconic image of glamour, the floating corpse ironically exposes that this perpetual chase of fame is a deadly enterprise. As a poignant reflection of this danger, the toxic relationship between Joe Gillis – our cynical antihero – and Norma Desmond – the stereotypical antagonist of a femme fatale – (8) is inherently doomed. This Gothic synthesis of old and new Hollywood thematically foreshadows their demise, as both are dangerously delusional: one is confined by their obsolescence, the other ensnared in the shallowness of the present. Symbolising the subordination that defines their affair, the loss of Joe’s car is a ‘matter of life and death’ that resolutely deforms him into an item of Norma’s possession, as fame is too often synonymous with prostitution. Subverting the traditional gender stereotypes of their time, Edith Head’s pertinent costumes of shared leopard print – a predominant motif of Norma’s wardrobe, which is also reminiscent of her silent-era glamour – illuminates the oppression that this pursuit of celebrity has manufactured. (9) In a house with no locks, Joe is already irreparably caught in the predatory grip of Norma’s atrophic talons, and thus, by extension, trapped by the noxious allure of Hollywood. Functioning as a provocateur to brazenly ‘bite the hand that feeds him’ (Mayer) (10) Wilder controversially deplores the deceptive façade of fame through his pioneering direction. Annotations:

(1) Open with dependent clauses elevates the piece by shifting the pacing. Adverbs are a great opportunity to briefly note tone. Every topic sentence should reference the topic.

(2) Link back to the genre to reflect your wider understanding; most students can only analyse what they see, without considering how this fits into the genre as a whole

(3) It is sophisticated to examine how this makes the audience feel (i.e. unease).

(4) Music is often forgotten in analysis. Films provide opportunities for different types of analysis. Don’t forget the soundtrack/camera angles/acting/outfits.

(5) Develop unique interpretations of symbols. Most students would ignore or neglect the palm trees, simply viewing them as a background item. When you provide unique views and analyses, markers are always impressed.

(6) As mentioned before, having an inventory of metalanguage relating to lighting is greatly sophisticated.

(7) Using linking words like ‘similarly’ underlines to markers that your evidence has been specifically chosen to flow cohesively. Listing dissimilar evidence can weaken the sophistication of your writing

(8) Showing an understanding of character tropes is sophisticated.

(9) Referencing outfits and costume choices can elevate your writing above other pieces, especially when you recognise their purpose as motifs and their significance at the time, such as in relation to gender stereotypes.

(10) The inclusion of one or two external quotes throughout your piece shows a wider contextual understanding and advances your piece to an academic or literary text. Challenging his fervent condemnation of Hollywood’s evil, Wilder manufactures a hopeful alternative that appears to exist untarnished by celebrity (1). Joe and Betty’s relationship functions as a tender antithesis to the toxically phantasmal alliance of Joe and Norma: this mystery is also an ‘Untitled love story’, saving it from merely being a lurid horror (2). Momentarily, Wilder permits Joe to escape his ‘peculiar prison’ of empty fame, returning to the corporeal world of Artie’s New Year’s gathering, an event imbued with a jollity that is crosscut against the solemn falseness of Norma’s decrepit mansion. From her entrance to the film, Betty Schafer embodies the forgotten passion for truth, ‘itching with ambition’ and presenting a virtuous alternative to Hollywood’s amorality. Illuminating her integrity, Schafer’s intelligence protects her from being deterred by the toxicity that is fundamental to this rapacious industry, as revealed when she is repeatedly misnamed by the sexist producer. Establishing a meta critique of the film industry, Wilder’s script sees this ‘sweet kid’ affirming that ‘pictures should say a little something’ rather than capitalising on being ‘glossy’ and slick’. Pertinently, whilst Norma is a victimised by-product of fame’s lethal illusion, Betty is an astute examiner who ‘know[s] all the plots’, acknowledging and embracing the deception at Hollywood’s core: she likes this ‘cardboard’ and ‘hollow’ town ‘better than any street in the world’. Further emphasising her innocence and purity, Betty’s practical attire of simple skirts and sweaters oppose Norma’s characteristic wardrobe of glamour to highlight the dichotomy between these women. Betty refuses to accept that amorality is inextricable from fame; rather, she empowers to write her own story, both literally and figuratively, reminding us that ‘life can be beautiful’. Hence, she is the perfect counterpart to Joe’s cynicism, embodying both his possible redemption as a man and Hollywood’s rehabilitation as an industry. Her ‘cubby hole’ of an office holds more promise and passion than the entire ‘white elephant’ of Norma’s mansion, highlighting the importance of truth. Their parallel storyline offers an escape for Joe, dislocating him from Norma’s persecution and empowering him to pursue his creative aspirations for motives nobler than shallow renown. Indeed, their witty dialogue – ‘Don’t you sometimes hate yourself?’ – and romantic chemistry symbolise this newfound sense of ambition, concurrently contrasting the sombre superficiality of Norma’s exchanges. Unveiling the wavering silver lining of celebrity, their classic Hollywood kiss offers a reprieve of romance. Emerging as perhaps the most admirable character of this satire, Betty’s genuineness empowers the audience to believe in an industry that prioritises creativity and love over dangerous materialism. (1) The structure of your essay is ultimately your decision, but having a paragraph that grapples/disagrees with the topic sentence can add an interesting layer of analysis. It provides an opportunity to explore other paths, characters and ideas, and differentiates your work from other responses.

(2) As mentioned previously, it is important to recognise that the film is not one-dimensional: it can be multiple conflicting genres at once.

Notice how this paragraph is based on comparisons and contrasts (e.g. Artie’s party vs Norma’s mansion, and Betty vs Norma). This is far more effective in conveying understanding and nuance than simply analysing characters and settings individually. It is important to consider their wider role and implications within the film. By the catastrophic climax of Wilder’s horrifying creation, it is overwhelmingly transparent that the dark side of fame will ultimately eclipse its idealistic counterpart, as this hope cannot survive unadulterated in the poisonous atmosphere of Hollywood. (1) Although Joe and Betty’s bond strives to pervert this amorality, even their romance eventually succumbs to the deception of this industry, for they cannot ‘get away with it’. Evidencing the moral dilapidation that is endemic to Hollywood, Betty’s chastity is defiled by the contagiousness of Joe’s shallow illusions; although she ignorantly maintains that she has ‘never heard any of this’, her newfound awareness of the toxicity of celebrity is palpable. As ominously revealed in the opening scenes, our protagonist’s flawed ‘long-term contract’ catalyses his spiritual and physical death, for the amoral motives of fame cannot be without consequence. Foreboded by the dramatic orchestral music, William Holden’s (2) purposeful characterisation of Joe’s premortem trance mirrors the enduring ignorance of Hollywood’s victims. Indeed, his final reach to his briefcase is analogous of the inherent desire of these hopefuls to continue to nourish fame’s deceptive image, even when that fatal bullet has been lodged deep in their unexpecting back. Rather than shattering Norma’s agonising delusions, this murder gruesomely precipitates her resolute descent into madness. Deprived of her moment of anagnorisis, as the role of Salome consumes her, Wilder’s use of mirrors as a motif conveys Norma’s dislocation of real and imaginary. Adorned with royal and theatrical music, Wilder’s staging of Norma’s slow-motion descent of her staircase in her bedazzled dress disturbingly typifies an embodiment of Hollywood’s ghastly underbelly erupting to the surface. With each step on her terminal pursuit of fame, this deranged ghost exits her corporeal sphere, her hyperreality finally becoming inescapable, as ‘there’s nothing else’. Surrounded by spectators yet entirely alone, as the camera closes in on her manic face – with her rigid claws and psychotic eyes blaming the culpable audience – she blurs into this phantasmal purgatory, destroying the alleged integrity of this business. As this ‘dream she had clung to so desperately’ finally ‘enfolded her’, Wilder’s writing expresses that celebrity, or the lack thereof, possesses the omnipotence to corrode a famed icon into an insane killer. Ultimately, Sunset Boulevard serves as a dire caution to the horrors of Hollywood, as the ‘price’ of this stardom will always be ‘too high’. Complexity and equivocation is important, but so is developing strong opinions.

(2) Referencing actors’ names proves that you have done extensive research.

Sample 2 - Example Contention and Body Paragraph

Topic ‘The only villain in Sunset Boulevard is Hollywood.’ To what extent do you agree?

Contention

Wilder critiques Hollywood’s self-interest, its obsession with youth and its ruthless pursuit for profit over their writers’ artistic intentions, yet he also acknowledges that succumbing to these demands, can be highly immoral and destructive. Body Paragraph

Wilder’s critique of Hollywood’s self-interest is exemplified through the decaying mansion, a visual metaphor for the discarded silent era stars. Norma Desmond, ‘the waxworks’, and Max personify how 1940’s Hollywood has relegated these former stars and directors to what resembles a gothic museum. Norma’s boudoir, draped in heavy fabrics and her sculpturally carved ‘gondola’ style bed, is a visual manifestation of her psychological stagnation, as a boat can’t move without water, just like Norma can’t move on from her former status. This is reinforced by Seitz’s camerawork of Norma who is often framed behind curtains, doors, or within mirrored reflections, signifying the prison like barriers she faces from the current era. Her Isotta-Fraschini, also a symbol of her former status, further reinforces stagnation as the car is immobile and a relic of the past. It is ironic that this old car is the only reason she returns to 1940s Hollywood, after receiving a call from Paramount studio, not from deMille whom Norma believed should have called, but Cole who wants her car as a prop ‘for a Bing Crosby film’, rather than Norma Desmond’s delusional role as Salome to be directed by deMille. Living in her delusion of a ‘return’ as ‘Salome’ in her self-written script, where she will play the lead role of a teenage girl at the age of fifty, Norma is exploited by Joe for some quick cash to pay off his car debt, demonstrating how people in the industry of Hollywood succumb to immoral actions in order to keep their dreams alive. In turn, Norma exploits Joe for his script editing and youthful addition to her life, and as a replacement for her dead chimp pet. The use chiaroscuro lighting demonstrates the interplay of light and darkness that underscores this exploitation that occurs. Thus, Wilder signals how both Hollywood of the 1940s, and former stars like Norma of the1920s, who were led to believe that stars never die because ‘that’s what makes them a star,” can lead good people to desperate immoral acts, and severe mental illness.

Sample 3 - High Scoring Sunset Boulevard Essay II

‘Sunset Boulevard shows that the pursuit of fame is a perilous journey. Discuss.” Billy Wilder’s 1950 noir film, Sunset Boulevard, scrutinises the complexity of the Hollywood machine, and through the lens of the grim, sinister underbelly of the famous and fortunate, shatters the glamorous façade fed to the people. By drawing on the personal aberrations of characters and the delusional artefacts which arise with the pursuit of fame, Wilder comments on the fragile nature of stardom, and creates a sense of distrust between the film industry and the ever-elusive entertainment realm. The film reinforces an atmosphere of entrapment silent film actors as those the wisps of her eminence, a B-list script writer from Ohio who aims to keep his distance from the flashy and aspiring master script writer who falls into the Hollywood cookie cutter. By juxtaposing characters helps Wilder reflect the paradox of human nature and portray the foreboding dangers associated with unattainable dreams in the film industry. Through his characters and film genre, Wilder asserts that notoriety is perilous and far less rewarding that what is believed. Sunset Boulevard‘s Joe Gillis, played by renowned actor William Holden acts as the perfect noir protagonist; cynical, aggrieved and pursued by a mob of sorts. However, Joe is far from a treacherous, undercover cop, black market infiltrating protagonist that Wilder offers other film offer. Unlike most films of the time, Wilder employs meta elements, having famous directors play directors, and legendary, but ageing silent actors such as Buster Keaton, to play themselves. This allows for him to abstractly comment on the brutality of Hollywood, the moral corruption kept so hidden from the public, and sets a sense of realism when inserting Joe Gillis in scenes with these characters. In order to boldly mimic the public of the personalities of fame, Wilder ties Joe’s story line based on a tragic tale as old as time; Icarus. Icarus is a tale of tragedy, a young boy who tried to escape Crete, grew arrogant, flew too close to the sun, and died in the Icarian Sea. Wilder presents Joe Gillis as Icarus, a tale of peril and hubris. By mirroring Icarus’ tragedy through Joe’s story line, with him escaping the sluggish debt collectors ‘when things were tough”, becoming Norma Desmond’s gigolo, growing arrogant and eventually a ‘has-been” with his lifeless body floating face down in a pool. Wilder showcases that danger associated with catching a ‘break” in Hollywood, with tragedy being the likelier outcome. With ‘[projects] that turned out to be night”, and the misfortunes associated with Joe’s big break into Hollywood, Wilder comments on the complex, manipulative nature of Hollywood, reinforcing the ghastly horrifying truth about his industry for individuals who aren’t old accomplished Caucasian men. While Billy Schader is by no means at fault for allegorical character, Wilder intentionally leaves her story as two dimensional and unfinished, favouring to emphasis the way that he represents most other women in the entertainment industry who do not succeed in these careers. Because of Betty’s duality in representing the unrepeatable aspect of women in Hollywood, and a child of nepotism, who spent ‘ten years” on ‘dramatic lessons, dictation and dancing” to ascend to stardom. Wilder portrays the frustration of women who want to become screenwriters, directors, and producers. Wilder, through Betty’s technique of Joe to work with her on his script ‘bases loaded”, shares his own frustration on making a break in the perilous industry of Hollywood. He embodies his own frustrations and fears through betty, with many parallels between them including the fear of both Betty and Wilder are also shunned from acting due to their looks. Their connections, modern mannerisms, and Betty’s open and vocalised mind is crafted to portray to the audience of the life that may be had should she choose to proceed without acting and instead to fill her life with purpose. Wilder shows Betty’s line to proceed with a fulfilling life not through acting but also leading a fulfilling life, unlike other individuals in the film. Wilder continues to explore this in the hazardous stardom hierarchy that many an actress is victim to, and which does not reflect the innocence and pleasantry of 1950’s woman. Norma Desmond, played by Gloria Swanson, encapsulates this idea. The narrator sets her up with golden names with silent films with ‘just her” [face!]. Through Norma’s status as the ‘greatest star” who ‘made” millions at ‘the lovely age of 17”, Wilder throws us in with Hollywood’s pessimistic, backward relationship in Hollywood, placing women stars far more than a role or a ‘vehicle”, he dehumanises and contextualised Norma as a silent film actress who was unable to ‘get to the talkies”. His account is laced with ‘100,000 dollar fan letters” which chases her youth, willing as far to kill and obtain Joe after his ‘unused” past self, yet self-deluded as she keeps the paper headlines on clips of camera technologies and lighting, including a scene in which an old crewmate of Norma turns the spotlight onto her, before being told to ‘return that to where it belongs”. The spotlight shinning down on Norma, paired with a high angle to make her look small, helps symbolise the beginning of the end. It shows Norma succumbing to years of pressure and pressure that was once enveloped in the warmth of her former years, but now has shattered the Hollywood machine. In the end, Wilder chalks up Norma as Narcissus. He shows a woman damaged by an industry that once enveloped her, a woman so afflicted by vanity, egotism, and self-delusion that she is a shell of her former self. Through a deluded Norma, playing an unlike character, and a dramatic story line, with an open end, Wilder caveats his audience of the precariousness and toxicity of the film industry. Although Sunset Boulevard is a film centred in the noir and macabre, highlighting the underworld of Hollywood, the ominous and disturbing truths of the famous and fortunate, Wilder portrays contrasting characters, with hopeful endings for some, and deserving, melancholic endings for others. These disparities allow for wilder to abstractly comment on his industry, portraying the notion that no true member of Hollywood is ever as innocent, and despite the dubious actions of his characters, draws a sense of sympathy for those who exploit others. Above all, Wilder allows for conflicting morals to take centre stage, often prompting his audience to takes sides, to showcase the complexity and multidimensional paradox of human nature. Through tragedy, and familiar tales, Wilder rewards courage, resilience and individuality, while disapproving of those embroidered into the Hollywood clothe, leading them to untimely outcomes, and others to fulfilling lives.

Sample 4 - High-Scoring Sunset Boulevard Essay III

Topic How does Sunset Boulevard explore the corrupting effects of Hollywood and the desperation it causes? Response Although a grimly cynical film, Billy Wilder’s Sunset Boulevard presents a critical portrayal of the effects selfishness and desperation can have on characters. Through its backstudio narrative, the film explores the corrosive nature of a heartless system that mistreats its subjects, and the desperate means necessary to survive in such an industry. By the end of the film, Wilder begins to envision an alternative but concludes by exposing the entrapping nature of a corrupt system. The Hollywood industry is constructed as a corrupt and heartless environment that acts as a catalyst for characters’ desperation. In the opening shot of the film a stark juxtaposition is formed between the glamour and corruption of Hollywood, the name connoting glamour and wealth, while the placement of the sign in the gutter indicating the opposite. Wilder presents Hollywood as corrupt because it promises fame and fortune but cannot deliver to the vast majority of people. From the outset of the film, the struggling screenwriter Joe experiences the cutthroat nature of Hollywood. SitÝng on his bed in a robe, Joe is ‘grinding out’ scripts in a desperate attempt to recover his career. Wilder highlights the dire nature ofhis situation, and how the promises Hollywood once made to the previously successful script writer are no longer being delivered upon. When debt collectors come to repossess his car, Joe’s perilous attempt to cling on to it serves as a parallel to his instability and financial insecurity. Joe’s statement that ‘[he needs] about two hundred and ninety dollars and [he needs] it real quick’ works to develop this idea further, highlighting his desperation due to the importance of his car that is symbolic of success and wealth. Wilder exposes Hollywood’s corruption by exploring the false promises of fame and fortune that are never delivered upon. In order to cope with the mistreatment of Hollywood and the ramifications of rejection, characters seek to survive through desperate means and selfish behaviours. Prior to his arrival at Norma’s mansion, Joe has been corrupted by Hollywood, making him utterly desperate and vulnerable. Being rejected by the industry forces him into desperate and unethical behaviours. He begins ‘rehashing’ old scripts, abandoning artistic integrity in his desperation to figure out how to succeed in the industry. Wilder examines the effects of desperation on relationships when Joe begins to exploit his contacts, using them as ‘assets’. Gillis’ deceptive nature is revealed through his exploitation of others, which is analogous to Hollywood’s commodification of its participants. Joe refers to the fact that Sheldrake had ‘always liked [him]’, displaying his intent to leverage personal relationships for his advantage. Wilder utilises Norma’s house as a reflection of Norma herself, the place being a ‘white elephant’, and a decrepit shrine to the past. Norma’s construction of this shrine is her way of dealing with the rejection of an industry that previously gave her validation and identity. Joe and Norma both create a narrative that suits their own personal interests, exploiting and manipulating one another. Norma is able to exploit Joe’s vulnerability, his need for money. She buys Joe’s companionship and loyalty, buying him a ‘vicuna’ coat, and seeing him as a young man that can make her feel young and attractive again. Joe exploits Norma’s vanity and instability to get what he believes will be easy money to pay off his debts and reestablish himself in the industry. Gillis is driven to deceive Norma by the corrupt Hollywood system that causes his desperation for financial aid. Both characters are led to manipulate the other by their desire for personal gain, acting in self-interest due to their desperation. This behaviour is analogous to the shallow values of Hollywood, living in an alternate reality and manipulating each other for their own gain. Whilst Wilder demonstrates an alternative approach to selfishly delusional living, the tragic ending of the film ultimately reveals that there is no real escape from the corrupt and Hollywood system for those that are too deeply entrenched. Wilder pictures Betty as a counterpart to Norma, she is an earlier version of Norma, one that is ‘like all us writers when we first hit Hollywood’ and has not yet been corrupted by the system. Betty represents authenticity and artistic integrity, adamant in her belief that ‘film’s should say something’. She is aware of the hollowness of Hollywood referring to it as ‘all cardboard’. Wilder presents her as the antithesis to Norma, a beacon of hope that is realistic where Norma is delusional. Wilder offers hope through Betty, an alternative to living in an alternate reality. Furthermore, her working relationship with Joe is more collaborative and transparent. She wants to ‘do it together’ and does not attempt to manipulate and leverage his talent for her own profit. Wilder contrasts Betty’s behaviour to the other characters, as she is willing to work with Joe rather than exploit him. However, although her sincerity connects with Gillis, Wilder’s portrayal of Joe’s subsequent behaviours undermines any optimism proffered by his relationship with Chafer. While Joe reconciles with reality and attempts to leave Hollywood, he is ultimately unable to extricate himself from Norma’s grasp, a representation of Hollywood’s hold over him. For Norma, Joe’s departure would mean the end of her alternate reality. Joe’s death as he leaves Norma’s entrapment underscores all characters’ inability to escape the corrupt Hollywood system. Even Joe, the character that finally realised the effect Hollywood has had on him, is not allowed to escape, with this realisation instead leading to his eventual demise. In line with the prevailing cynicism throughout the movie, Joe meets his demise just as he attempts to break free. Following Joe’s death, Norma’s descent into madness is complete. She retreats further into her fantasy world, believing that she is starring in a movie scene rather than facing the grim reality of her actions. The final words of Norma and of Hollywood itself are ‘no one leaves a star’. Her last words are a testament to the way the system entraps and manipulates its participants. Sunset Boulevard is a scathing criticism of the cruelty of Hollywood and the corrupting effects of a mistreating system that causes desperation and selfishness. Relationships based on manipulation and self-interest in addition to characters exploiting others emphasises the film’s intention the expose the deceitful nature of Hollywood. Furthermore, while some hope is generated, the corrupting nature of Hollywood overshadows any positive features, overall revealing the corrosive effects that selfishness and desperation have on characters within the industry.
