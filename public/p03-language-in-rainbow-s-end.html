Language in Rainbow’s End

Overview

Language in Jane Harrison’s Rainbow’s End is a powerful vehicle for expressing identity, class, and cultural conflict. The characters’ speech patterns reveal their personal histories and social positions, while Harrison’s authorial voice is embedded within their dialogue, reflecting her own commentary on racial inequality, resilience, and generational change. Language, therefore, functions not only as a mode of communication but also as an indicator of power, prejudice, and pride within the 1950s Australian context.

Nan Dear

Nan’s language is colloquial, humorous, and often sharply ironic. When she informs Gladys that she “won’t be needing any of them encyclops” (p.136), she dismisses education’s relevance to her family’s daily struggles, revealing both her pragmatic worldview and the limited opportunities afforded to her generation. Her unpolished speech reflects her lower-class status and lack of formal education—an outcome of systemic exclusion faced by Aboriginal Australians. Yet Nan’s language also injects comedy into the play. Her dry humour and literal phrasing create moments of levity that highlight resilience in hardship. When mocking Gladys’s excitement about meeting the Queen, Nan’s irony underscores the absurdity of colonial class structures. Through Nan’s speech, Harrison reveals both the pain and pride of a woman grounded in survival, whose humour becomes an act of quiet rebellion against social inequality.

Analysis

Nan’s dialect situates her within a marginalised social class while asserting authenticity and self-respect. Her vernacular speech becomes an emblem of identity, opposing assimilationist pressures that sought to erase Aboriginal voice. Harrison uses Nan’s language to celebrate oral tradition and defiant humour, challenging notions that education alone defines intelligence.

Dolly Banks

Dolly’s language reflects her education and confidence but also her awareness of the social hierarchies constraining her. She is direct and assertive, particularly when confronting racism. Her rebuke of Errol’s remark about “you people” (p.141) exposes his unconscious bias and the normalisation of racial division. Likewise, her challenge to her mother — “You’re always telling me to stick up for myself, but when do you, eh?” (p.162) — forces Gladys to examine her internalised inferiority. Dolly also code-switches, adopting Indigenous vernacular when she threatens to “kick Ester’s boys up to moon” (p.137), using humour and familiarity to assert belonging within her community. Her fluency, both in educated English and colloquial Aboriginal dialect, embodies her dual identity as a bridge between two worlds. Her closing assertion to Errol — that she would never trade her family or heritage for a “better life” (p.172) — epitomises pride and defiance.

Analysis

Dolly’s language signifies empowerment through articulation. Harrison crafts her speech to demonstrate that mastery of language is not assimilation but assertion. Dolly’s ability to speak for herself—and against injustice—makes her a vehicle for Harrison’s advocacy of Indigenous self-determination. Her linguistic versatility becomes symbolic of cultural strength rather than concession.

Errol Fisher

Errol’s halting and awkward speech reflects both his nervousness and his ignorance. As an encyclopedia salesman who depends on persuasion, his incompetence is ironically comic. When he awkwardly notes that Dolly’s glass is “made out of a can” (p.140), he unwittingly insults her. His language often reveals class and racial privilege cloaked in politeness — exemplified in his use of “you people” (p.140) to generalise Aboriginal Australians. His verbal missteps and tone-deaf expressions highlight his sheltered upbringing and lack of cultural awareness. Over time, however, Errol’s language softens and becomes more considerate, mirroring his emotional growth and respect for Dolly’s world.

Analysis

Harrison uses Errol’s speech to critique the well-meaning but ignorant paternalism of mid-century white Australia. His progression from patronising to empathetic reflects the broader social journey toward understanding and equality. His linguistic awkwardness thus serves as a tool of both comedy and commentary.

Gladys Banks

Gladys’s voice develops from timid dreamer to assertive activist. During the Rodney Shire meeting, her passionate declaration — “We demand proper schooling … The white people too — they need to be educated about us, and our ways” (p.197) — becomes a turning point where her speech echoes Harrison’s own political message. Her earlier language is often infused with fantasy and aspiration: daydreams of the Queen’s attention or Dolly’s graduation soften her struggles. Yet by the play’s end, Gladys has found a political and moral voice that embodies the collective frustrations of Indigenous Australians denied dignity. Her transition from domestic to public speech represents empowerment through language.

Analysis

Through Gladys, Harrison demonstrates the transformative power of speech as activism. The act of “speaking up” replaces passive dreaming with agency. Gladys’s voice becomes that of her people — articulate, righteous, and unafraid — asserting the necessity for visibility and reform.

The Royals

The Queen’s language is formal, elevated, and exclusionary. Her reference to Australia as “the birthplace of the nation” (p.124) erases 65,000 years of Aboriginal civilisation, while her statement about standing “at last among Australians” implicitly refers only to white citizens. This polished rhetoric embodies colonial arrogance. By contrast, the Duke’s language is more spontaneous and unguarded. His offhand question — “Where’s Father?” (p.127) — humanises him but also underscores the royal couple’s detachment from the lived realities of those they visit.

Analysis

Harrison employs the Royals’ diction as social critique. Their words — ceremonious, detached, and self-congratulatory — expose the gulf between imperial symbolism and the Indigenous suffering it conceals. The Queen’s non-inclusive language mirrors the nation’s non-inclusive identity at mid-century.

The Radio Announcer

The radio announcer’s language is superficially cheerful yet profoundly condescending. In describing the royal visit, he mentions a demonstration of “how to throw a boomerang” and marvels that it “really does come back” (p.128). His tone trivialises a complex cultural artefact, reducing Indigenous skill to entertainment for the royal couple.

Analysis

The announcer’s commentary encapsulates institutional racism disguised as enthusiasm. Harrison’s use of irony highlights how mainstream media perpetuated ignorance, framing Aboriginal culture as novelty rather than heritage. His words parody the way national pride excluded First Nations people.

Structural and Symbolic Language

Beyond dialogue, Harrison uses language in her stage directions and symbolism to reinforce meaning. Descriptions of the Dear family’s humpy — “waterlogged” and “mud-spattered” (pp.123, 176) — visually communicate poverty and marginalisation. The title itself, Rainbow’s End, draws on the myth of a pot of gold, suggesting the illusion of prosperity and the unattainability of a promised better life. The reference to “Somewhere Over the Rainbow” from The Wizard of Oz introduces an ironic hopefulness. Its lyrics — “the dreams that you dare to dream really do come true” — echo the family’s optimism but also question the realism of those dreams in a society unwilling to grant equality.

Analysis

Harrison’s manipulation of both literal and figurative language constructs a layered critique of Australia’s racial landscape. The interplay between hope and futility in her metaphors underscores the central tension of the play — between aspiration and limitation, imagination and reality. Language, whether spoken, written, or symbolic, thus becomes both a weapon and a wound.
