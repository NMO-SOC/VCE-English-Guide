Part 02 · Sunset Boulevard

Symbols

  • The Mansion

    • Symbolises Norma’s decaying fame and psychological isolation

    • A crumbling structure, ’out of beat with the rest of the world’

    • Reflects her detachment from reality and the passage of time

  • Black-and-White Cinematography

    • Enhances contrast between Norma’s world (dark, stagnant) and Betty’s world (bright, full of life)

    • Reinforces themes of nostalgia and decay

  • Spotlights

    • Symbolise attention, former fame, and Norma’s obsession with being seen

    • Used dramatically when Norma sits in deMille’s chair – a ghost-like figure in the light

  • Gates and Barriers

    • Represent isolation, exclusion, and entrapment

    • Norma uses her home as a prison to keep herself—and Joe—separated from the real world

  • The Monkey’s Funeral

    • Symbolises Norma’s lost career and her inability to let go of the past

    • Gothic mis-en-scène highlights the absurdity and tragedy of her delusion

  • Cars

    • Represent control, freedom, and social mobility

    • Joe’s loss of his car mirrors his loss of independence when he becomes trapped in Norma’s world

  • Close-Ups of Norma’s Face

    • Reflect her silent film origins and the over-expressiveness required in that era

    • Symbolise her inability to adapt and her clinging to outdated forms of identity and performance

  • The Salome Script

    • Symbolises Norma’s desperate attempt to reclaim her stardom and control her narrative

    • Reflects her delusion and obsession with youth, power, and seduction

    • A vehicle through which she imagines her triumphant return, though the industry has no real interest in it

  • Paramount Studios

    • Symbolises the modern, ever-evolving world of Hollywood that has left Norma behind

    • Represents both nostalgia and rejection—once her kingdom, now a place where she is merely tolerated

    • A cold contrast to the warmth of Norma’s imagined fame

  • Max’s Role as Butler

    • Max, who was once a respected director and is now reduced to being Norma’s butler,symbolises the destructive nature of Hollywood.

    • His loyalty to Norma and his willingness to play into her delusions highlight how far people can fall in the industry and how they cling to past identities.

  • Max’s Gloves

    • Symbol of Max’s servitude and control—he handles every aspect of Norma’s life with careful precision

    • Suggests a barrier between Max and reality, as he preserves the illusion with delicacy and ritual

    • Reinforces his role as both protector and enabler of Norma’s fantasy

  • Norma’s Clothes

    • Luxurious and extravagant, but styled from a bygone era—symbolising her refusal to move forward

    • Represent how Norma costumes herself in a fantasy, masking the reality of her decline

    • Reinforce the image of a star who is ’big’, but only in her own memory

  • The Pool

    • The pool, where Joe’s body is found, symbolises the false allure of Hollywood’s glamour.

    • Initially a symbol of success and luxury, it ultimately becomes a symbol of death and disillusionment.

    • Joe’s lifeless body floating in the pool illustrates the deadly consequences of chasing hollow dreams.

  • The Film Projector

    • The film projector represents Norma’s obsession with the past.

    • As she watches her old films, the projector becomes a symbol of her inability to move on from her glory days, trapping her in a loop of nostalgia and delusion.

  • The Final Scene

    • The final scene, where Norma believes she is on set filming a movie, symbolises her complete disconnection from reality.

    • It also serves as a critique of Hollywood, where the lines between reality and illusion often blur, leading to tragic consequences.
