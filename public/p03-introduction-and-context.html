Introduction and Context

Author Background

Jane Harrison, a descendant of the Muruwari people, is an acclaimed playwright, novelist, and critic. As a child, she “always loved books” but was disheartened by the absence of Aboriginal representation in Australian literature. In writing Rainbow’s End, Harrison sought to give voice to those experiences that had long been silenced or marginalised. Drawing upon oral histories from Aboriginal Elders, she captures the resilience and humour of families who lived on “The Flats” and later at Rumbalara—the First Nations housing estate in Mooroopna. Harrison’s work strongly condemns the substandard housing and systemic neglect endured by First Nations families during the 1950s. Through her writing, she positions herself as a conduit for truth-telling, giving dramatic expression to both the hardships and triumphs of Aboriginal life. Her aim, as she has stated, is to use fiction as “an important way for First Nations people to have a voice.”

Analysis

Harrison’s authorial perspective intertwines personal heritage and political conviction. By dramatizing the everyday realities of Aboriginal families, she reclaims history from the margins and rehumanises voices often reduced to statistics or stereotypes. Her compassionate yet incisive storytelling challenges audiences to confront both the past and its lingering presence in modern Australia.

Historical Context

The colonisation of Australia, beginning with the arrival of the First Fleet in 1788, unleashed devastating consequences for First Nations peoples. The ensuing Frontier Wars—violent conflicts between Aboriginal people and British settlers—resulted in the massacre and displacement of countless Indigenous communities. Throughout much of the twentieth century, government policy continued to pursue assimilation and cultural eradication. One of the most tragic outcomes of these policies was the forced removal of Aboriginal children from their families. These children, later known as the Stolen Generations, were stripped of their language, names, and cultural identity. Many were placed in institutions or adopted into white families, where they were exploited as unpaid labour and suffered emotional and physical abuse. In Rainbow’s End, Harrison threads this history through the lives of Nan and Gladys, whose trauma reflects the generational wounds inflicted by colonial intervention. Gladys’s experience of being taken from her family and made to work as a domestic servant mirrors the broader policy of forced assimilation. Her obsession with Dolly’s education becomes a personal act of restitution—an attempt to reclaim what was taken from her by systemic injustice.

Analysis

By embedding the historical trauma of dispossession within intimate family narratives, Harrison compels her audience to recognise the enduring emotional and social consequences of colonisation. Her portrayal of the Stolen Generations not only condemns past wrongs but also foregrounds the resilience and agency of those who survived them.

The Flats and Cummeragunja

Cummeragunja, founded in 1888 on Yorta Yorta/Bangerang Country, represented both refuge and resistance for Aboriginal people. Initially a self-sufficient farming community, it was later undermined by the New South Wales Protection Board, which seized control in 1915. Aboriginal workers were paid in meagre rations, and funds generated by their labour were appropriated by the Board. By the 1930s, disease and poverty had decimated the community. In 1939, the residents of Cummeragunja staged a historic protest—the first mass strike by Aboriginal Australians. Many left the mission and settled on “The Flats,” a floodplain near Mooroopna and Shepparton. Despite harsh living conditions, including humpies made from “flattened kero tins” and exposure to extreme weather, the community retained its cultural vitality and kinship ties. By the early 1950s, around 300 people lived there.

Analysis

The Flats serve as both a literal and symbolic setting in Rainbow’s End. They embody dispossession yet also community, poverty yet perseverance. Through this setting, Harrison contrasts the instability imposed by colonial structures with the cultural rootedness of Aboriginal families. The Flats become a testament to the capacity of a people to adapt, resist, and endure despite systemic neglect.
