Sample 1 - High Scoring Rainbow’s End Essay

Topic: How does Rainbow’s End suggest that everything will be alright? In the 2005 Indigenous play ‘Rainbows End’, playwright Jane Harrison suggests everything will be alright for the Dear family, despite the challenges they combat. This is shown through the portrayal of self-determination, coupled with the continued hope and optimism of the Dears. However, the heavily damaging colonial attitudes to the Indigenous community act as a constant threat looming over the community and their futures. Throughout the play, Harrison highlights the unwavering self-determination of the Indigenous community, suggesting everything will be alright due to the strong resolve of the Dear family. This is depicted most vividly through the growth of Gladys, initially reserved and acquiescent, but steadily becoming more outspoken and steadfast in her beliefs for a better future. This culminates in Gladys taking a bold stand for the indigenous community at the town meeting, stating “I’m not an interloper – I belong here! - this is my land!”. Gladys’ open defiance in the meeting, challenging cultural norms and expectations, acts as a pivotal switch from Gladys’ belief everything will be alright, to taking direct action in pursuit of a better future for her family. Similarly, Dolly’s stoicism and resilience in the face of adversity and her assault serve as an emphatic representation of selfdetermination. After her assault, Dolly’s honest and true nature in showing her baby to Errol, despite severe shame, depicts her growing as an individual through hardship and pain through these qualities of self-determination. Stating “Yes, I’m okay” when questioned by Errol about her baby, Dolly embraces her struggles, remaining determined and continuing to seek a better future for herself and her baby. Overall, Harrison suggests everything will be alright for the Dear family, despite great hardship, through the continued determination of both Gladys and Dolly. Harrison further suggests that everything will be alright due to the stalwart hope of the Dear family, embodied through Papa Dear, dream sequences and the prospect of education. Throughout, Papa Dear acts as a symbol of a positive future and hope for the family and broader Indigenous community, travelling across Australia fighting for their rights. This is shown through Nan, who states “its Papa Dears mission to make things better for the Aboriginal people”, suggesting that Papa Dear acts as a figurehead and an unseen force for good in the Indigenous community. Similarly, Papa Dear exists as a projection of hope through dream sequences with Gladys, who observes “Papa Dear, in old fashioned hat and coat, dances in, throws his hat on the hatstand, kisses Gladys on the top of the head, and dances out”. This suggests an omnipresent quality of Papa Dear as a symbol of hope for the members of the Dear family; as a constant reminder that everything will be alright. Further, the prospect of education offers hope for the future for Dolly, and is first presented in the form of encyclopaedia’s from Errol, to which Gladys exclaims “my daughter, a graduate”. This is further propagated by Dolly’s ambitions of becoming a nurse in Melbourne, as a catapult to a better and more prosperous future. Ultimately, Harrison suggests that the unwavering hope of the Dear family, through Papa Dear and the prospect of education, are a reminder that everything will be alright. However, Harrison also indicates the highly destructive nature of colonial attitudes, and their ability to prevent the Dear family from a better future, as a direct challenge to the sentiment that everything will be alright. Harrison portrays this primarily through the housing inspector, representing a hostile Australian government policy and its corrosive effect on the lives and futures of the Indigenous communities. This is demonstrated through the removal of Aunty Esthers children at the behest of the inspector, and further the repeated visits he makes to the Dear family home. Stating “your whites are so white, with river water no less”, the inspector highlights to close scrutiny of every detail of the Dear’s lives, and the constant threat to their future looming over them. Similarly, Harrison suggests the continued resentment of the broader community actively works to dissuade the Dears and Indigenous community from seeking a better future. This is displayed through the town council, whilst attempting to silence Gladys challenging societal norms, stating “the Chair does not recognise this…. Eject this interloper!”. Further, Harrison displays the negative affects of such attitudes through Dolly, exclaiming “it’ll never change…. Some things never do” after being scrutinised by Nancy at the Shepparton ball. Overall, Harrison suggests that colonial attitudes can directly challenge and threaten the hopes and self-determination of the Dear family and broader indigenous community, and further the prospects of everything being alright. On balance, Harrison displays the potential for everything to be alright for the Dear family and broader Indigenous community through the qualities of self-determination and hope, but also the constant threat of colonial attitudes to the future.

Sample 2 - High Scoring Rainbow’s End Essay

Topic: ’Harrison’s play celebrates characters who show strength through adversity.’ Discuss. Set in Victorian town, Shepparton, Jane Harrison’s 2007 play, Rainbow’s End, explores the lives of three generations of Koori women as they navigate the setbacks and challenges faced by First Nations people in 1950s Victoria. At a time where the government regulates the policies of Assimilation of the Indigenous community, these women are vulnerable to the impacts of racism, colonisation and suppression of knowledge. But despite facing discrimination at every turn, Harrison commemorates their resilience in the face of adversity. The women are able to become symbols of hope as they refuse to allow their struggles to define them. Throughout Rainbow’s End, the struggle for the Indigenous community to pursue their dreams due to racial prejudice is highlighted in order to spotlight their ability to overcome it. This is demonstrated in scene ten in the bank managers office. Gladys tries to apply Dolly for a job as the bank teller, assuring the bank manager that she is fit for the job by advocating for her brains and wits. But despite Dolly being a good candidate, the manager is hesitant about letting her apply. He argues that, “reliability is important,” implying that Dolly is unreliable despite never having met her. He makes this assumption due to his personal prejudice to people of Dolly’s heritage. This demonstrates the racial discrimination that limits opportunities of First Nation’s people. Regardless of the bank manager’s doubts, Gladys pushes for the job as she is reminded of Dolly’s words, “have you learnt not to be shamed by them?”. Through this, Harrison highlights how the women’s power to overcome setbacks comes from within themselves. She champions these women for their ability to stand up for themselves in spite of the racially biased attitudes that are meant to limit them. This theme of hope is prevalent throughout the play and intends to inspire strength from within to tackle problems. Moreover, Harrison explores the intricacies of cultural identities and belonging, highlighting the strong connection to culture despite the colonisation that the characters face. Nan Dear is a compelling example of this. Nan’s frequent allusions to spirits or warnings from her “ancestors” demonstrate the strong indigenous traditions that survive and carry on even after the effects of colonisation. Intergenerational wisdom is significant in indigenous culture and Nan Dear’s frequent references to guidance from her ancestors attempts to pass that down through her children, who may continue to succumb to assimilation but nevertheless is still deeply rooted in their cultural identities. The reminders of tradition allows Harrison to assert that cultural identity cannot be erased and is represented through characters like Nan Dear who have been through cultural erasure but continues to be a strong image of indigenous culture. Furthermore, resilience is present in overcoming adversity from the suppression of knowledge that characters throughout the play go through. Gladys is a representation of this as it is revealed to the reader that Gladys is illiterate. This was due to the assimilation policy that separated Gladys from her family and forced her into working at a young age rather than getting an education. Harrison demonstrates Gladys overcoming this in the last scene of the play where Gladys delivers a speech at the public meeting. Not only does she deliver it flawlessly, she delivers it with confidence, which Gladys has mentioned that she “gets nervous in front of people.” Harrison acknowledges the optimistic and determined attitude that Gladys uses as a tool to fight for her ambitions despite the systemic racism that has denied her basic human rights. Through Gladys Harrison aims to write about the ‘unsung’ heroes who were not expected to be a hero but was able to become one in spite of the adversity that she faces. In conclusion, “Rainbows End” champions the characters for their ability to overcome adversity due to their unwavering determination, offering a powerful testament to the power of human spirit when up against hardship. Through the use of quotes and direct references to the characters, Harrison tells a compelling story that aims to bring hope to the audience, reminding them that the difficulties that humanity faces is incomparable to the resilience that lies within each of us.

Sample 3 - High Scoring Rainbow’s End Essay

Topic: ‘Least here we do things our way- no one breathin’ down our necks’ Home is central to identity in Rainbow’s End. To what extent do you agree? Amongst the racism and oppression, it is clear that ‘home’ is of the utmost importance to the identity of Harrison’s Indigenous characters. Set against a backdrop of white Australian superiority in the 1950s, ‘Rainbow’s End’ by Jane Harrison explores the central theme of ‘home’ in the context of identity. For the three generational main characters, their ‘humpy’ alongside the riverbank of the Mooroopna flats is integral to the make up of who they are and essential for Harrison to showcase the strength and resilience needed for First Nations to fight their marginalised status. Beyond the physical ‘home’, identity is shaped by the family and culture surrounding the characters through the lens of white and Indigenous Australians. Throughout the first part of the play, ‘home’ is physically represented as the ‘humpy’, a hut with a dirt floor and hessian-covered walls. Despite the poverty of the main character’s living conditions, it isn’t the place where they sleep that is important to them as much as the culture surrounding them. For the character of Nan, "the Murray…Is your place isn’t it” due to the immense sense of belonging she feels after her oppressive experience with the Cummeragunja mission and the close proximity of her Aboriginal ‘family.’ In this way Harrison highlights the role culture and a physical ‘home’ plays in an individual’s identity. In addition, the pride taken in the appearance and homeliness of the ‘humpy’ is a further aspect of identity illustrated by Harrison. When in the play it comes time for the inspector to visit to the ‘humpy’, Harrison has Nan and Gladys ‘nervously’ straightening the cushions and draws attention to the ‘beautiful work’ of the crocheted pillows, emphasising the the importance of ‘home’ to the characters. This is brought to the attention of the audience again during the move to the ‘white and featureless’ Rumberala housing, and the characters immediately think of ‘making curtains’ as a way to reassociate their identity with the new housing. The contrast between the ‘home’ of the white Australian characters such as Errol and Nancy, and the housing in Rumbalara and the flats is reflective of the identities of the characters and how they feel entitled to act. For example, Nancy is portrayed as wealthy and privileged and therefore acts in a privileged and entitled way, whilst the main characters are substantially underprivileged and act humble, with kindness despite adversities. The playwright suggests ‘home’ is more than a physical establishment but extends into a ‘home’ found within family and culture. The notion of family and culture as a ‘home’ is a central theme throughout the play, explicitly explored between the misalignment of Errol and Dolly’s definition of a ‘home’. For the character of Errol, he believes a ‘home’ is a place to live, where only the wealth and geographical opportunities are relevant, however, Harrison utilises his conversation with Dolly to pinpoint to ideal that “a real home is where people are looking out for each other,” regardless of how many ‘presents’ are under the tree. Furthermore, Errol reflects on the differences of their families, expressing to Dolly that his Dad “expects me to call him sir” and ultimately concluding that despite the poverty of the flat’s he bets ‘you have more fun than we do’. This conversation was pivotal to underlining the impact of a ‘home’ with a supportive family and rich culture in creating a luster for life and powerful sense of identity. For the characters in the play, ‘home’ is closely intertwined with colonial suppression, the white Australian’s control over Aboriginal housing had a heavy influence on the identity of the characters. Throughout the play, Harrison makes reference to a difference in the perception of Aboriginals from the flat’s and ‘townie types of cross-over Aborigines’ depicting those who lived in town as slightly better off, with better opportunities. This differentiation underpins the impact of location on identity as the ‘white fellas’ feel the Indigenous characters need to ‘be absorbed into the community’ in order to be ‘assimilated’ and forced to act in alignment with white culture. The control that the white characters, such as the inspector, assert over housing is detrimental to the identity of the characters, it withdraws their control and forces them to abandon their culture in order to be accepted. Moreover, Harrison suggests that a ‘home’ can affect social mobility, Gladys in particular is obsessed with ‘fitting in’ and being accepted and it becomes a large part of her identity. The ‘white gloves’ and ‘Auntys shoes’ are a symbol of her need to assimilate to white culture, acting in direct contrast to the Aboriginal culture that makes up her ‘home’ and thereby her identity. Throughout the entirety of the play, Harrison brings forth the different types of ‘home’, both physical and as a culmination of family and culture. In this way it is evident to the audience that home is closely entwined with the identity of all the characters in the play, from Errol who has never experienced a ‘home’ within a family, to Dolly whom does not associate a ‘real home’ with a fancy establishment but instead with a loving family and cultural traditions. The exploration into the impacts of oppressive attempts from the ‘white fellas’ to force ‘assimilation’, displays how a ‘home’ is more than a roof over one’s head, but instead an embodiment of an individual’s unique identity as formed by relationships, culture, pride and resilience.

Sample 4 - High Scoring Rainbow’s End Essay

Topic: Harrison’s play celebrates characters who show strength through adversity. Discuss. Jane Harrison’s play Rainbows End is a powerful exploration of the lives of three generations of Indigenous women, Nan Dear, her daughter Gladys, and granddaughter Dolly, in 1950s rural Australia. Harrison presents their daily struggles with poverty, limited access to education, and systemic racism, not to victimise them, but to celebrate their resilience and determination. Through her portrayal of these women, Harrison critiques the entrenched hierarchies that marginalise Indigenous communities, while affirming the strength required to survive and resist. Ultimately, the play celebrates the capacity of Indigenous women to endure adversity, even as it recognises the costs of their survival. Harrison portrays how racial discrimination restricts Indigenous Australians’ opportunities and dreams. This is evident in Scene Ten, when Gladys visits the bank in the hope of securing Dolly a position as a teller. Gladys proudly emphasises her daughter’s academic success, noting that Dolly has completed her “leaving certificate” and is “top of her class,” in an attempt to counteract the stereotypes imposed upon Indigenous people. However, the bank manager dismisses Dolly’s achievements, questioning how she would “fit in” and whether she could be “relied on” to attend work daily, despite never having met her. Through the portrayal of the bank manager, Harrison highlights how deep-seated structural racism embedded in Australian institutions causes harmful stereotypes that prevent Indigenous communities from fulfilling their full potential in society, denying them equal access to employment. By juxtaposing the women’s ambitions with the bank manager’s prejudices, Harrison critiques the injustice of a culture that confines Indigenous families to “picking” and other forms of low-status labour regardless of merit. Yet she also affirms Gladys’ resilience, showing her refusal to abandon her daughter’s dreams despite repeated setbacks. In doing so, Harrison not only exposes how systemic racism denies equality but also celebrates the persistence of Indigenous women who continue to assert dignity and demand recognition against such barriers. Harrison exposes the entrenched poverty endured by Indigenous families to critique the systemic marginalisation of First Nations communities. Rather than presenting poverty as a personal failing, Harrison illustrates how social and economic exclusion force the Dear family into degrading conditions and low-cost labour, while simultaneously highlighting their resilience in preserving dignity and hope. Nan Dear’s ironic description of their riverbank “humpy” as “our own little palace” re-frames deprivation as pride, transforming humiliation into humour. Similarly, Gladys expresses frustration at Dolly’s reliance on “hand-me-downs,” revealing her determination to secure independence for her daughter. By infusing moments of humour and defiance into depictions of poverty, Harrison refuses to allow Indigenous identity to be defined by shame. Instead, she asserts the capacity of women like Nan and Gladys to maintain agency and self-respect in the face of adversity. Dolly’s dream sequence after her visit to the town tip symbolises a longing for a future beyond dispossession, foregrounding aspirations that colonial systems sought to deny. Through these portrayals, Harrison critiques structural inequalities, particularly the exploitation of Indigenous people as fruit pickers and scavengers, while simultaneously celebrating the resilience and strength of Indigenous women to endure and reimagine possibility. Thus, Harrison’s authorial intent lies not only in exposing the material realities of Indigenous poverty but also in affirming the power of humour, pride and aspiration as acts of survival against systemic injustice. Knowledge is presented in the play as both a pathway to empowerment and a means of representing the differences in wisdom. Gladys has a strong belief that schooling will elevate Dolly beyond ingrained disadvantage, insisting that she “get an education” so she can “be someone.” This determination highlights her strength as a mother, willing to challenge generational cycles of poverty. Harrison deliberately frames this ambition with irony: while Dolly’s academic success is undeniable, she remains judged through stereotypes and institutional racism, suggesting that education alone cannot dismantle systemic barriers. At the same time, Harrison complicates the notion of knowledge by staging contrasts between Indigenous oral tradition and Western authority. When Errol dismisses Dolly’s experience with snakes, Gladys’ clarification that it was a harmless “carpet snake” undermines the authority of the encyclopedia and asserts that lived experience offers forms of wisdom inaccessible to formal schooling. By juxtaposing these knowledge systems, Harrison critiques the way white institutions privileged their own frameworks as universal while marginalising Indigenous perspectives. Her authorial intent is doubled to expose how education in the 1950s was arranged by race and class, yet also to celebrate the resilience of families like the Dears, who embraced education without relinquishing pride in their cultural knowledge. Harrison both honours the courage of Indigenous families who value education as a tool for advancement and critiques the system of schooling planted between classes in the 1950s. Harrison’s Rainbow’s End critiques the systemic racism that restricted Indigenous Australians while celebrating the resilience of women who refused to be silenced. By exposing institutional prejudice, entrenched poverty and competing forms of knowledge, she highlights both the barriers that denied equality and the strength of Indigenous families who asserted dignity despite them. In doing so, Harrison affirms that hope, humour and cultural pride are not just survival strategies but powerful acts of resistance against injustice.

Sample 5 - High Scoring Rainbow’s End Essay

Topic: ’Rainbow’s End is a bitter condemnation of colonisation.’ Discuss. Cemented within a world enveloped by overpowering white culture, Rainbows End seeks to explore the lengths of condemnation expressed by Indigenous Australians, juxtaposed with the desire to be included. Within the play, Harrison blatantly reveals consistent resistance and dislike aimed towards white authority yet contrasts this with younger generations who desire integration and inclusion in society. By depicting Indigenous women in this way, Harrison can characterise older generations as resistant to white knowledge and eternally furious at the erasure of Indigenous culture yet depicts younger characters as open to colonisation to succeed in their corrupted society. Overall, Harrison criticises the unjust dilution of Indigenous culture, simultaneously advocating for Indigenous Australians to continue fighting to be heard over white dominance. Harrison calculatingly embeds Indigenous resistance throughout the play, through unwavering denial, which represents a resilience to maintain Indigenous culture, despite attempts to silence it. Harrison evidence this though Nan Dear showing her despise for Errol’s encyclopedias, asking him what it says, “in that there encyclops about Aboriginals”. Harrison’s lexical choice of characterising Nan through her informal and grammatically incorrect language of “that there” and “encyclops”, illustrates Nan’s refusal to submit to white assistance in education, as the encyclopedias are based off Eurocentric knowledge, maintaining her stance in continuing Aboriginal knowledge and culture no matter the state of her academics. Nan Dear also directs this hate towards Errol, as he is the delivery boy for them, and is white, as she sees him as a messenger for white authority, being cautious of letting him get too close. Harrison lambasts the erasure of Indigenous knowledge, yet cautiously condemns the anger being taken out on unrelated and innocent white individuals. Harrison juxtaposes this condemnation of colonisation through dream sequences, when Gladys begins to dream of the possibility of Dolly becoming “a graduate”. Gladys’ willingness to accept and integrate into the complexities of white society in order to provide her daughter with a proper education, contrasts that of Nan’s ideologies relating to white assistance, demonstrating the differences in generational beliefs. As encyclopedias are based on white knowledge, this further illustrates Gladys’ will to accept their knowledge as superior to succeed. The dream sequence represents Gladys’ subconscious suggestion that her daughter may never be able to be “a graduate” due to her needing to follow Nan’s beliefs as the eldest of the family, being constrained to the limitations rightful morals to uphold Indigenous independence. Harrison champions the pursuit of education despite society restraining full ability yet criticises the neglect of protecting Indigenous culture and forgetting true identity. Harrison impatiently lambasts the evident erasure of Indigenous culture, through white authorities directly and indirectly pushing it, which exposes their never-ending desire to abolish Aboriginal knowledge and existence. Harrison represents this through the humpy inspector asking if Gladys has heard of “the concept of assimilation”, as a promise that “things will change.” The contradictory nature of the inspectors’ intentions and promises represent white ignorance towards Indigenous perspectives and opinions, as the inspector didn’t let Gladys answer if she has heard of the “concept” of assimilation, continuing to talk about it as if she isn’t there. The inherent dominance from the inspector pushing for the absorption of Indigenous Australians into white society conveys the collective idea of abolishment, and then stating that “knowledge is power” continues to falsely represent the freedom of Indigenous Australians white authorities distribute. Harrison demonises the dominance of white imposed hierarchies that put Indigenous Australians at an unfair disadvantage, keeping them there without proper consultation. Harrison further bolsters this through the stage direction of Gladys and Nan “exchange[ing] worried looks”, after the inspector’s suggestion. Their “worried looks” emphasise the desire to remain where they are, away from the “mercy of the manager” which Nan Dear recalls from her traumatic days of living at a mission. The family can easily accept help from white authorities by submitting to their dominance yet refuse to so they can maintain their independence and keep their cultural heritage and identity intact, living of their own free will. Their desire to stay at their humpy and not receive better living conditions demonstrates their appreciation for the opportunity to live by themselves and the gratitude for where they are, no matter the difficulties that surround these unfavourable living circumstances. Harrison bolsters the idea of maintaining Indigenous heritage but questions the resistance towards white assistance if it is at the expense of health and safety. Harrison diametrically contradicts the stereotypical resentment towards colonisation through Indigenous youth desiring to submit to white dominance, which raises questions about the rightful path to follow when aiming to achieve Indigenous independence. Harrison depicts this through Dolly’s desire to be included in white society, when Dolly is attending a dance in Shepparton, and exclaims how “everyone’s going to be there”. Dolly being the youngest of the family is the most exposed to whitewashing and diluted Indigenous culture, hence her powerful ambition to attend dances and wear Eurocentric 1950s outfits and accessories, to be like “everyone” who’s going “to be there”. Her outfit is symbolic of the tensions between her socio-economic status and her dreams, acting as a constant reminder of her inability to completely be accepted in white society. Harrison praises the confidence of Indigenous youth to break free from limiting social and gender norms in order to achieve one’s dreams yet warns against the dangers of being denied acceptance from such rebellion. Harrison further reinforces a rebellion in the opposition to white superiority through Gladys’ dialogue when she dares to believe that Dolly can succeed by not letting “her address stop her in life”. Gladys’ rhetorical question towards Nan exemplifies the difference and experience of generations, Gladys believing Indigenous women can succeed without the acceptance of white people. She is met by Nan Dear’s cold response telling her to “get a grip”, snapping her back to reality and reminding her of the fact that they are a minority that is disadvantaged in a backwards society that will never favour them. Gladys daring to stray from the wisdom of her mother represents an underlying feeling of hope for change, despite claims and actions that proves change is unlikely to occur. Gladys and Dolly don’t harbour severe resentment towards their society like Nan Dear, as they have less experience with unwavering discrimination. It outlines their desires to blend into the white community at the expense of their autonomy, in order to gain increased access and succeed in achieving a better life than what they have. Harrison champions the unwavering objective of striving to be better in one’s life, while cautioning of the implications of aiming too high and ignoring the safety provided by one’s community. Conclusively, Harrison continuously exhibits justified resistance to colonisation of Indigenous culture yet remains hopeful that younger generations may have the courage to break the discriminatory cycle. Harrison encapsulates this through continued vilification of white knowledge and education, and the injustice of institutional authority, while postulating a juxtaposed change in views through the acceptance of colonisation within younger generations. Harrison ultimately condemns the unjust erasure of Indigenous culture and identity, while pushing the notion that the amalgamation of cultures may lead to increased opportunities for Indigenous Australians and should not be seen as an entirely demonised occurrence.
