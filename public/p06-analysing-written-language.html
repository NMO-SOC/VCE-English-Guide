Analysing Written Language

Terms and Strategies

Specific Word Choice (word connotation)

Writers choose particular words to provoke an emotional reaction in the reader or emphasise an idea. For example, if writing about abortion, the author may choose; "I read with disgust that the government is to ... kill, slay, slaughter, murder, butcher, exterminate, destroy, sacrifice, execute ... Emotive language is language that is not logical or rational - it reflects and expresses an emotional or subjective reaction. Such words help create mood. Many words have connotations or associations that the writer uses with deliberate intent to create a desired effect in the reader. Words may have positive or negative connotations. They may evoke intense imagery. You will be expected to discuss some targeted words and their relative connotations in your language analysis piece.

Particular language structure and features

Inclusive language (we, our) may be used to give the audience a sense of being part of a wider community of thought/action. Modifiers and qualifiers are words used to denote a level of impact. There is a difference between a writer choosing to use ’some’ instead of ’all’ or ’most’ instead of ’many’. Phrases such as ’very good’ or ’basically good’ also suggest different meaning to the reader. Students analysing language need to consider why specific lexical strings have been chosen by the writer as a form of persuasion.

Euphemisms

Euphemisms are words sometimes used to soften a more accurate but seemingly more offensive word or phrase. For example, euphemisms for ’die’ include; passed away, lose one’s life, kick the bucket, croak. A company may say they are ’downsizing’ instead offiring workers from the job.

Hypothetical Scenarios

Hypothetical scenarios are used to prompt the reader to imagine possible outcomes, often beginning with phrases like “Imagine if...”. These speculative examples draw attention to the consequences of actions or inactions, even if they are not real or likely. Writers use this technique to provoke reflection and emotional investment from the reader. For example: “Imagine if the world was ruled by tyrants who dominated our every thought daily...”

Repetition

Repetition involves deliberately repeating words or phrases for emphasis and to make an idea more memorable. Anaphora: repetition at the start of successive clauses. e.g. School is distracting. School is boring. School sucks. Epistrophe: repetition at the end of clauses. e.g. Follow the rules. Obey the rules. These are the rules.

Puns

Puns are humorous wordplays based on similar sounds or multiple meanings. They are often used to lighten tone or ridicule opposing views. Some puns also carry double meanings, especially in persuasive or satirical writing. e.g. “Time flies like an arrow; fruit flies like a banana.”

Irony

Irony occurs when the intended meaning of a statement contrasts with its literal meaning. Writers often use irony to highlight absurdities or criticise ideas indirectly. e.g. “Lovely day,” said during a storm.

Sarcasm

Sarcasm is a biting, mocking form of expression usually intended to hurt, ridicule, or convey contempt. It often overlaps with irony but has a more cutting tone. e.g. “Nice face. Shame about the rest.”

Exaggeration / Hyperbole

This technique involves overstating a point for dramatic effect. It can be used to emphasise importance or provoke an emotional response. e.g. “I’d rather cut off my arm than go back to work under those conditions!”

Humour

Humour engages the reader by entertaining them. It can also make controversial ideas more palatable or draw attention to flaws in opposing arguments. It is often used as a non-threatening way to critique.

Satire

Satire combines humour and criticism to ridicule individuals, institutions, or ideas. It often exposes hypocrisy or absurdity, making serious points in a humorous way.

e.g. A TV show like Frontline that uses mock-journalism to critique the media.

Oxymoron

An oxymoron combines two contradictory terms to highlight contrast or provoke thought. It is often used to emphasise complexity or irony. e.g. deafening silence, wise fool, bitter sweet

Assonance

Assonance is the repetition of vowel sounds within words or phrases to create rhythm, mood, or emphasis. Often used poetically or in slogans. e.g. “cool, calm and collected”

Simile

A simile compares one thing with another using the words ‘like’ or ‘as’. It helps readers understand unfamiliar concepts by likening them to familiar ones, often to create vivid imagery. e.g. My bedroom is like a pigsty. She is as skinny as a rake.

Metaphor

A metaphor is an implied comparison that states one thing is another, thereby transferring qualities from one to the other. Metaphors can make writing more persuasive by enhancing imagery and emotional appeal. e.g. I stepped into my pigsty, shut the door and climbed into bed. He is a pillar of strength.

Rhetorical Questions

These are questions posed for effect rather than to elicit an answer. They imply that the answer is self-evident, guiding the audience to agree with the writer’s point. e.g. Do you want to see your wife and children out on the streets in rags? Of course you don’t!

Scapegoats

A scapegoat is a person or group blamed for something they are not truly responsible for. This technique simplifies complex issues and focuses the reader’s anger on a convenient target. e.g. All our crime and unemployment problems are because of the government.

Assumptions

Assumptions involve treating beliefs or opinions as facts without proof. These are often subtle and can influence readers by appealing to what is generally believed to be true. e.g. He must be smart because he is on the computer every day.

Presumptions

Presumptions occur when statements are accepted as truths without critical examination. They appeal to common beliefs or cultural norms. e.g. If it’s natural, it’s good for you.

Analogy

An analogy compares two things that are alike in some respects to clarify a point or persuade readers that what applies to one must apply to the other. e.g. If birds can fly because they have wings, then maybe if we develop mechanical wings we can fly too. e.g. The human brain is like a computer.

Expert Opinion

This technique involves citing someone perceived to have authority or expertise on a topic. It lends credibility to an argument and can be persuasive even if the “expert” is unnamed. e.g. In the thirty-five years I was Head Surgeon at...

Case Studies and Anecdotes

Case studies and anecdotes are used to personalise or humanise an argument. Anecdotes are usually emotive and personal; case studies more formal and factual. Both aim to build credibility and emotional appeal. e.g. Pool fencing should be mandatory because my neighbour’s child drowned in an unfenced pool.

Empty Truths

Also known as tautologies, these are statements that repeat the same idea unnecessarily, often sounding meaningful but lacking substance. e.g. Boys will be boys. Enough is enough. Business is business.

Cause and Effect

This technique suggests that one event directly causes another. It is persuasive because it offers logical explanation, though causality may not always be valid. e.g. Peter crashed his car. Peter died.

Ends and Means / Means to an End

This approach justifies an action based on the outcome, even if the action itself is questionable. e.g. A criminal donates to a playground; the donation is good, but the source of funds is illegal.

Consequence of a Fact

This presents a pragmatic response to an existing situation by arguing that something should be done simply because a condition exists. e.g. Nuclear power exists, so we might as well use it.

Judging an Action by its Consequences

This technique justifies or condemns an action based on its results. It focuses on outcomes rather than intent. e.g. Stealing from the rich to give to the poor is acceptable because the poor are hungry.

Sweeping Statements / Generalisations

These make broad claims that apply to a group without considering individual differences. Generalisations can sound convincing but often oversimplify. e.g. All teenagers are irresponsible.

Cliché

Clichés are overused phrases that offer familiar shortcuts to meaning. Though persuasive due to their recognisability, they can also weaken writing if not used purposefully. e.g. What goes around comes around.

Co-existence

This assumes an automatic relationship between ideas or traits, such as assuming someone is good simply because they attend church. e.g. She goes to the local church, so she must be a good person.

Guilt by Association

This technique links people, ideas or groups with undesirable individuals or arguments in order to discredit them. e.g. Premier Kennett has behaved like Hitler since coming to power.

Questioning Motive

This involves undermining an argument by questioning the motives of the speaker, diverting attention away from the argument itself. e.g. The Health Minister’s decision to close the hospital is just an excuse to build a monument to himself.

Figures and Statistics

Numbers and data are used to give the illusion of objectivity and reliability. However, the reader must critically assess source, sample size, relevance, and interpretation. e.g. 300 people died on our roads last year...

Discredit Another Argument

This technique aims to dismantle opposing viewpoints by exposing flaws or inconsistencies in them. It draws attention away from the original argument and weakens the opposing side’s credibility.

Attack

This technique involves criticising or mocking opposing arguments or individuals. It is designed to weaken the opposition’s credibility and make their position less persuasive. Writers may use name-calling, ridicule, or other personal attacks to influence the audience’s perception. One form of attack is labelling the opposition in an attempt to belittle them.

Flattery and Praise

This technique involves listing individuals or groups who agree with the writer’s position in a positive light. By using terms that convey admiration or respect, the writer bolsters the authority of their own argument. e.g. “Leading scientists support this research.”

Use of Punctuation and Printing Style

Writers use punctuation and formatting deliberately to add emphasis, create subtext, or manipulate tone and flow. Common strategies include:

  • Inverted commas – suggest doubt or irony, even when quoting.

  • Exclamation marks – show emotional emphasis or urgency.

  • Dashes – indicate pauses, hesitation or parenthetical ideas.

  • Long sentences – may signal sophistication or rambling.

  • Short sentences – create punch, pace and immediacy.

  • Italics, CAPITALISATION, bold, underlining – all used for emphasis.

Vocabulary Use

The choice of vocabulary significantly affects tone and credibility. Writers use sophisticated or precise word choice to sound more authoritative or to target specific reader emotions. Students should be aware of:

  • Emotive connotations (e.g. slaughter vs kill)

  • Word strength and subtlety (e.g. sporadic vs occasional)

  • Replacing vague or weak terms with more accurate or academic language.

Improving vocabulary can enhance clarity and persuasiveness. For example:

  • Replace “straight away” with “immediately”

  • Replace “in the next paragraph” with “in the following section”

  • Replace “always” with “consistently”
