Glossary of Techniques

Every film and persuasive technique from the guide in one alphabetical reference.

Ad hominem Metalanguage

Attacking the person making an argument rather than the argument itself.

Adjective Persuasive technique

Describing words usually connected to a noun (naming word). e.g. The bright red house (bright and red = adjectives; house = noun) — Adds emphasis.

Aerial shot Film technique

A shot with the camera positioned directly above a subject.

Allegory Metalanguage

A narrative whose characters and events stand for a parallel moral, political or historical meaning.

Alliteration Persuasive technique

Repeating and playing upon the same letter. e.g. A back-breaking job... — Adds emphasis and reinforces meaning, especially where an idea is repeated. Often used to create a highly emotive image.

Allusion Metalanguage

An indirect reference to another text, event or figure that imports its associations into the work.

Anaphora Metalanguage

Deliberate repetition of a word or phrase at the start of successive clauses to build rhythm and force.

Anecdote Persuasive technique

A short account or story of an entertaining or interesting incident. e.g. In my experience... — Usually makes reader sympathetic and receptive to the point; can set up a character, then position reader to accept or reject that individual character.

Antithesis Metalanguage

Setting opposing ideas in balanced phrasing to sharpen the contrast between them.

Appeal to authority Metalanguage

Citing experts, credentials or institutions so the audience defers to the position.

Appeal to fear Metalanguage

Raising threatening consequences so the audience feels the risk of not agreeing.

Appeal to hip-pocket Metalanguage

Framing an issue around cost and personal finances to engage self-interest.

Appeal to patriotism Metalanguage

Invoking national identity and pride so agreement feels loyal and dissent disloyal.

Archetype Metalanguage

A character type or pattern recurring across literature that carries instant associations.

Aside Metalanguage

A short remark delivered to the audience that other characters do not hear.

Associations Persuasive technique

Words that create meanings beyond the literal meaning of the word. e.g. attractive, alluring, pleasing, charming — Suggests or influences reader response in certain ways, often subconsciously. Implied meanings need to be identified.

Attacks/praise Persuasive technique

The writer attacks or praises an opponent or idea. This is really an assertion of the writer’s viewpoint. e.g. denigrating or humiliating the person, discrediting opponents as unreliable, dishonest, suggesting unsatisfactory associations — Often effective in placing the reader in a position of agreement with the writer/speaker through forceful language and insistent claims, even without evidence.

Bandwagon Metalanguage

Suggesting most people already agree, pressuring the audience to join the majority.

Bias Persuasive technique

One-sidedness in presentation of view/opinion. e.g. Eminem’s tour promoter obviously sees no danger in what Eminem says. — Can subjectively influence the reader by intentionally only presenting one side of the argument.

Call to action Metalanguage

A direct instruction telling the audience what to do next.

Characterisation Metalanguage

The methods an author uses to construct a character: dialogue, actions, appearance, others' reactions.

Chiaroscuro Metalanguage

Strong contrast of light and shadow in a frame, often signalling moral ambiguity or noir style.

Cliché Metalanguage

A worn expression whose overuse signals lazy or manipulative reasoning — or is deployed knowingly.

Clichés Persuasive technique

Worn out, overused expressions. e.g. Fit as a fiddle, Turn over a new leaf, Pushing the envelope — Are familiar, often colloquial, so can offer a shortcut to convey meaning.

Close-up shot Film technique

Tightly frames a person or a subject. Enables the audience to see the emotions of a character or details on a subject.

Colloquial language Metalanguage

Informal, conversational wording that builds rapport and an ordinary, relatable persona.

Colourful words and descriptive language Persuasive technique

Words that are heightened, vivid, lively, and full of interest. e.g. Hot = blistering, sultry, muggy, suffocating... — Produce a picture and/or induce an emotion. Engage reader by gaining attention and often put a new slant on familiar events and issues.

Connotation Metalanguage

The emotional or cultural associations a word carries beyond its literal meaning.

Contention Persuasive technique

The main line of argument in a text. A statement of the main idea being argued or debated. Focuses on WHAT is being argued. — Pinpoints the issue and the writer’s point of view. Helps the reader identify the issue and evaluate the techniques used to persuade them to agree.

Cumulative listing Metalanguage

Piling up examples or details so the sheer accumulation feels like proof.

Diegetic sound Film technique

Sound within the world of the film — characters can hear it.

Dramatic irony Metalanguage

When the audience knows something a character does not, creating tension or poignancy.

Emotional Appeals Persuasive technique

Play on people’s emotions such as fears, insecurities, hopes, desires, and values. Can target: • A sense of justice or injustice • Family values • Tradition or custom • Patriotism • Moral values — Frequently appeal to our values and attitudes, sometimes subtly. Can manipulate reader to take notice of issues by triggering emotional responses.

Emotive Language Persuasive technique

The deliberate use of strong emotive words to play on reader’s feelings. e.g. sleazy, slimy, vicious, disgusting, outrageous — Evokes strong emotional response to coerce or force agreement. Used to stimulate strong emotional reactions and manipulate responses.

Epigraph Metalanguage

A quotation placed at the start of a text that frames how it should be read.

Establishing shot Film technique

Usually the first shot of a scene to establish the location and environment.

Euphemism Metalanguage

A softened expression that makes something unpleasant more palatable — or hides it.

Expert opinion Metalanguage

Quoting a specialist to lend borrowed credibility to a claim.

Extreme close-up Film technique

A more intense close-up that might focus on a specific feature.

Film noir lighting Film technique

Low-key lighting used to emphasise shadows and create strong contrasts.

Flashback Metalanguage

A scene set earlier than the main narrative, used to reveal context or motive.

Foreshadowing Metalanguage

Hints or clues that prepare the audience for later events.

Framing Film technique

The composition of characters or subjects within a single shot.

Framing device Metalanguage

A narrative structure that encloses the main story, shaping how it is read.

Generalisation Metalanguage

A sweeping claim about a whole group from limited cases; persuasive but vulnerable to rebuttal.

High angle shot Film technique

Subject is filmed above eye level to make them appear vulnerable or weak.

Hyperbole Metalanguage

Deliberate exaggeration for emphasis or effect.

Imagery Metalanguage

Language appealing to the senses to create vivid impressions.

Inclusive language Metalanguage

Pronouns like 'we' and 'our' that position the audience as sharing the writer's view.

Intertextuality Metalanguage

Meaning created through a text's references to or echoes of other texts.

Irony Metalanguage

A gap between what is said and what is meant, or between expectation and outcome.

Jargon Metalanguage

Specialised technical vocabulary that can build authority or exclude an audience.

Juxtaposition Metalanguage

Placing contrasting elements side by side so each sharpens the other.

Loaded language Metalanguage

Words carrying strong connotations that smuggle judgement into apparent description.

Loaded Words (Labelling) Persuasive technique

Words that are loaded with associations. e.g. capitalist, communist, salesman — Shorthand way of discrediting or praising by suggesting associations. Exploits emotional baggage of such words to sway opinion.

Long shot Film technique

Used to show a subject from a distance, establishing them within a setting.

Low angle shot Film technique

Subject is shot from below eye level to make them appear powerful, dominant, or morally superior.

Metaphor Metalanguage

A direct comparison describing one thing as another to transfer its qualities.

Mid shot Film technique

Usually shows an actor from the waist up. Focus is on the characters but also shows the environment they are in.

Mise en scène Film technique

All elements contained in a single frame (lighting, costuming, positioning, set, props).

Montage Metalanguage

A sequence of shots compressed together to convey time passing or accumulating meaning.

Motif Metalanguage

A recurring image, phrase or idea that accumulates meaning across a text.

Motifs Film technique

A dominant or recurring prop, image, or symbol (e.g. mirrors, frames, candles, chandeliers).

Narrative voice Metalanguage

The perspective and personality through which a story is told.

Non-diegetic sound Film technique

Sound added in post-production — characters cannot hear it (e.g. soundtrack, narration).

Over the shoulder shot Film technique

A medium close-up shot from behind another character’s shoulder, typically used in conversations.

Oxymoron Metalanguage

Two contradictory terms fused together to provoke thought.

Panning shot Film technique

When the camera moves horizontally from its fixed position and mirrors the motion of looking from side to side.

Paradox Metalanguage

A statement that seems self-contradictory yet reveals an underlying truth.

Pathos Metalanguage

An appeal to the audience's emotions.

Personification Metalanguage

Giving human qualities to non-human things.

Point of view shot Film technique

Mimics what a character in a scene is seeing, putting the audience into their perspective.

Pun Metalanguage

Wordplay exploiting double meanings, often to disarm or mock.

Rebuttal Metalanguage

Directly addressing and dismantling an opposing argument.

Repetition Persuasive technique

Repeated words, phrases, sentence patterns, ideas. — Gives emphasis and prominence to a point or idea; reinforces and makes reader remember the point.

Rhetorical question Metalanguage

A question posed for effect, leading the audience toward an implied answer.

Rhetorical Questions Persuasive technique

Questions that have the answer embedded in them; often use irony. — Manipulate the reader to agree because the answer is assumed. Disagreement would mean dismissing a point that commands agreement.

Sarcasm Metalanguage

Bitter, mocking irony directed at a target.

Satire Metalanguage

Humour, irony or exaggeration used to criticise people, institutions or ideas.

Setting Film technique

The environment in which the film is set. Can shift over time.

Simile Metalanguage

A comparison using 'like' or 'as'.

Soliloquy Metalanguage

A speech delivered alone on stage, giving direct access to a character's inner thoughts.

Stage directions Metalanguage

A playwright's instructions for action, delivery and staging — key evidence when analysing drama.

Statistics Metalanguage

Numerical evidence that lends an argument an air of objectivity and precision.

Straw man Metalanguage

Misrepresenting an opposing view in a weakened form so it is easier to knock down.

Symbolism Metalanguage

Objects, settings or images that carry meaning beyond their literal presence.

Three-point lighting Film technique

Technique using key light, back light, and fill light to evenly illuminate a subject.

Tone Persuasive technique

Refers to the voice of the writer; the writer’s attitude to the subject and reader. Tone can be emotive. e.g. angry, sympathetic, sarcastic — Reflects writer’s attitude, which can influence the reader to agree or reject. Tone can signal a new direction or perspective in the writing.

Tone shift Metalanguage

A deliberate change in tone mid-text that redirects the audience's response.

Tricolon Metalanguage

A three-part list whose rhythm makes a point feel complete and memorable.

Two shot Film technique

A medium shot showing two actors in a single frame.

Understatement Metalanguage

Deliberately downplaying something so its true scale lands harder.

Vernacular Metalanguage

Everyday or regional language that grounds characters in place, class or community.

Zoom in / Zoom out Film technique

A change in focal length during a shot to magnify or reduce the subject.
