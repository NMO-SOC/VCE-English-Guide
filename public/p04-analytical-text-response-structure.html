Analytical Text Response Structure

Topics

There are three types of questions you may receive on a SAC or exam. These are the ‘Discuss’, ‘To what extent’ and ‘Do you agree?’ statements. These are all tackled in a slightly varying manner.

  • Discuss : This is probably the most common type of prompt that you will encounter. When dealing with such prompts, it is important that you discuss as many aspects of it as possible. One way to improve your discussion here is to include a paragraph that rebuts the prompt, like what you do in your persuasive essays. However, since it is a discuss prompt, you do not need to make the two sides of your argument extremely distinct.

  • To what extent: These topics require you to make a decision. Never say ‘it is to some extent’ in your contention. You really need to assign a portion of something. The best way to do this is to start with subordinating conjunctions like ‘whilst” or ‘although”. To What Extent questions rarely want you to agree or disagree - they want you to explore a shade of grey.

  • How: The ‘How’ topics usually focus on the construction of a text - your evidence should contain metalanguage like ‘characterisation’ for instance.

Introduction

The introduction is the first piece of your writing that will be examined. For this reason, it should be clear, direct and show your understanding of the topic. The introduction is there to show your examiner how you wish to respond to the set topic. If your topic contains a quote, ensure that you mention it somewhere in your introduction to show you are addressing the topic.

A General Line of introduction

Too often our introductions lack any insight and mainly begin with author+text+background information which has nothing to do with the actual topic. We should attempt to be relevant with our opening statement and discuss why this concept is important.

Introduce the Text, Author and Contention

Now we introduce the text and author and the audience. Then state how it relates to the topic specifically. These need to be connected fluently, when writing we should be relying on connectives to make sure there is a logical flow in our expression. Depending on the prompt itself, it might be a wise idea to link either/both of characters and setting to the topic of the essay prompt. We can do this as such: Linking a Character to a Topic:

N.B. that the name of the book is always underlined. I.e. Sunset Boulevard or Rainbow’s End. Contention A contention is the author’s point of view on the topic. Since you are the author in this scenario, you need to write what your position on the topic. A contention should be short, to the point, and engage with the topic. On this falls the crux of your essay. Do not be afraid to agree/disagree with a topic. You are allowed to have a conditional contention.

Body Paragraphs

The body paragraphs form majority of your essay. The body paragraphs are the main reasons that the contention your provided is true. Your body paragraphs need to be able to demonstrate your understanding of the text and the fluency of your language. You should ensure that every sentence (and thus every word) brings something towards your argument.

Structure

Below is the structure for a body paragraph using ‘Th.E. S.A.M.L.’ Method. This method of writing body paragraphs was developed in order to maximise possible marks. Only the ‘TH’ and ‘L’ elements of this structure need to be in the order given.

Th
Thematic-based topic sentence

Your topic sentence needs to be based upon a theme in the text, not a character.

✗ ‘Norma Desmond’s obsession with herself ultimately leads to her downfall.’
✓ ‘Obsession with self is what ultimately leads to the downfall of characters.’
E
Evidence

Quotes and scenes from the text that support the theme.

S
Symbols

Symbols that relate to this theme — including historical and social context, and setting.

A
Authorial intention

What was the author intending?

M
Metalanguage

Discuss the metalanguage the author uses around this theme.

L
Linking sentence

Link back to your contention.

Only the Th and L elements need to appear in this order — the middle elements can be woven through the paragraph.

Basic Example ‘Th.E S.A.M’

‘Obsession with one’s self is what ultimately leads to the downfall of characters. This can be seen with Norma exclaiming ‘who else?’ when Joe questions her about her script. The script itself is also a representation of Norma’s obsession as she has written several hundred pages where she stars in every scene. Wilder uses this to demonstrate that Norma is unable to move on from anything besides herself. The slow camera pan whenever the script is shown foreshadows Norma’s impending doom which links is inherently linked to her self-absorption. In this way, obsession with self ultimately leads to the downfall of several characters in Sunset Boulevard.’

Hinge Sentences

In your topic sentence, you can introduce two related ideas. This creates room for a more complex, insightful paragraph. But remember: when you shift focus from the first idea to the second, you must signal this change to the reader clearly. This is where the hinge sentence comes in. A hinge sentence acts like a bridge—it acknowledges what you’ve already explored, then transitions to the second idea you introduced in your topic sentence. Here’s how that works:

By emphasising Gladys’s daydreams of royal recognition, Harrison conveys how, for a family denied dignity and opportunity, the imagination becomes a vital refuge from an unjust reality—ultimately suggesting that dreams alone cannot deliver change, and that true escape demands the courage to speak and act.

Evidence + Analysis of First Idea

In the dream sequences where Gladys imagines being received by the Queen, Harrison frames fantasy not as idle escapism but as a shield of dignity for a woman excluded from the “decent” society she longs to join. Yet Nan Dear’s blunt dismissal—“And what bloody good would that do? Daydreams!” (p. 154)—exposes the limits of this refuge, highlighting how dreaming leaves the family’s material circumstances untouched.

Hinge Sentence (transitioning to Second idea)

However, while Gladys’s dreams preserve her sense of self-worth, Harrison also reveals that fantasy alone cannot deliver her family from injustice.

Analysis of Second Idea

Gladys’s transformation culminates in her address to the Rodney Shire Council, where she demands “proper schooling … The white people too — they need to be educated about us, and our ways” (p. 197). Harrison complicates the notion of escape by showing that it is voice and action, not fantasy, that begin to change the family’s circumstances, suggesting that dreams are only powerful when they inspire agency. By using a hinge sentence, you show your reader that you’re not just stacking ideas but developing an argument, deepening your interpretation, and showing the author’s nuanced message.

Conclusion

If you think of a conclusion as less of summarising everything, and more of combining all of these conflicting ideas and messages into one overall conclusive point. We can do this by using the following structure.

Structure

The structure for a conclusion can follow the same structure as an introduction. However, instead of introducing the text, you are concluding your discussion. Here is an alternative method: Topic:

‘The dream sequences in Rainbow’s End are the characters’ only escape from the depressing reality of life.’ Do you agree?

Conclusion Structure for Analytical Essay Writing
Purpose in ConclusionExample Sentence
Overview of how the whole text demonstrates the key concepts in the essay topicFundamentally, Rainbow’s End explores the tension between dreams and reality, particularly how fantasy can offer dignity, but not deliverance, to those denied opportunity by an unjust society.
Summary of the complications of this topicThroughout the play, Harrison complicates the notion of escape by showing that while Gladys’s daydreams preserve her sense of self-worth, they leave her family’s circumstances unchanged, challenging the romanticisation of hope without action.
Focus on the key authorial message in conclusive languageTherefore, Harrison critiques a society that forces its most marginalised to retreat into fantasy, asserting that genuine change lies not in dreaming of acceptance but in finding the voice to demand it, as Gladys does before the Shire Council.
