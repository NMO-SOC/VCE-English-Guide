Dolly’s dressThe Miss Mooroopna-Shepparton ball, Nan made it for Dolly, but was of fabric from Daish’s which were Nancy’s family’s curtains- Represents her dreams and desire to be included in white society  but demonstrates Indigenous struggles and exclusion through it being made of tip fabric that Nancy recognises and makes fun of. - Tension between socio-economic status and dreams.“My dress? Courtesy of the town bloody tip?” – Dolly, page 166
Gladys’ flowersGladys holding very wilted flowers after coming back from trying to see the Queen- Crushed hope and disappointment - Energy drained, reflecting in the flowers - Care of collecting flowers met with exclusion (hessian) mirrors Gladys’ optimism and faith in wanting change being eroded.“The hessian they lined the road with.” – Gladys, page 127
The cork treesNan tells Dolly to go to school but warns her to not go near the cork trees because of her drunk cousins- Rough trees  Dolly’s rough future, dreaming to escape from the inevitability of her fate - Cork trees aren’t native to Australia, and it being the cousins hangout can symbolise displacement, hence Nan preventing Dolly from going near it, so she’s protected from negativity drawing her there. Tries to maintain Dolly’s will and persistence for life. - Juxtapose the innocence of school with the sorrow of losing it - Symbolises bad future and bad consequences“But keep away from them cork trees.” – Nan, page 126
Que sera, seraA song that repetitively appears at the front, throughout and at the end of the book, meaning whatever will be will be.- Acceptance of fate, almost encouraging them to continue resigning to their circumstances. Yet highlights their resilience to persevere through hardships. Reminds them to endure and the fact it’ll pass. - Familial bonds  their song - Tension between hope and resignation - Powerlessness in the face of fate“That’s it then, Que sera sera. Please don’t tell mum about the cork trees.” – Dolly, page 188.  Close encounter with negativity, doesn’t want to think about it, is recentered with supportive family  The song before talking about her rape implies it was inevitable, as she “knew that’d happen”. – page 168

Biyala treeNan mistakes Dolly’s school project of the family tree for her talking about the Biyala tree- Constant connection and reminder of country and ancestors - Resilience of culture, as it’s an old tree - Symbolises life and renewal  reminder things will be better - Spiritual bond to ancestry, always on Nan’s mind.“The Biyala? Spirit tree with the low hanging branches?” – Nan, page

The hessianIndigenous housing being blocked off by hessian from the government when the Queen comes to visit so she can’t see them- Being covered by hessian demonstrates neglect and disrespect for Indigenous Australians  embarrassment of them, yet they’re the ones who put them at a disadvantage. - Coarse fabric  harsh realities of being hidden, silenced and discriminated against - Render them invisible, they’re ashamed of them - Façade of respectability for the Queen“Stop the likes of her seeing our humpies.” – Gladys, page 127 “But hessian! Like a bandaid over a sore-“– Gladys page 127 “What good is it if I get het up?” – Nan, page 128
The humpyTheir houses made of materials they sourced, such as from the town tip, they have no electricity or water- Representative of socio-economic status and view within their society  viewed as unworthy - Stark contrast between marginalisation and wealth - Symbolises hardships and resilience even in the face of nature - Connection to land, they love what they have and refuse to leave it, especially from white help  represents what’s left of their independence - Reminder of neglected injustice - Hope for change“A real home?” – Dolly in response to Errol, page

Title: Rainbows EndThe title is Rainbows end which is the meaning of the housing they get transferred to, Rumbalara- Rainbows represent hope, renewal, better future. Can symbolise possibility of a brighter future for Indigenous Australians and hope for social progress and recognition. - Naming the new housing ‘Rumbalara’ can symbolise the end of Indigenous freedom, as it means ‘rainbows end’ the end of the rainbow (happiness), they’re going to be controlled again and will lose their freedom. Nan “knowing what it means” shows she understands what will happen again, being at “the mercy of the manager”. - The rainbow’s end is a mythical destination that can never truly be reached, the characters dreams often feel distant and elusive. Characters dream of a better future, but practical obstacles and social barriers make these dreams seem unattainable“they called it rumbalara” “I know what it means”

BleachAjax Bleach is advertised on the radio twice for its ability to whiten things- White people advertising the bleach  bleach gets rid of things, erases them/colour = white people advertising the erasure of Aboriginal culture, subtly advocating for assimilation/abolishment through the radio, publicly. - Bleach cleans, implying Aboriginal people are dirty, to be clean like white people“Honestly, they’re mad about whiteness.” – Nan, page
SongsSongs repetitively appear during dream sequences and throughout the book- Catch a falling star: dreams, never stop chasing them, always have hope with you. - Que sera sera: continue living, things will pass. - A girl like you: resilience of love no matter social barriers. - I’ll be home: grateful for home no matter the issues or hardships. - Somewhere over the rainbow: there are better times to come, dreams may be out of reach, but hold hope. - The old rugged cross: the cross as a symbol of strength during Nan’s health scare“the assembly singing The old rugged cross very faintly” page 194
The colour whiteWhite is mentioned multiple times, such as the inspector talking about how white the clothes were, the characters talking about their white clothing, and Nan reading about skin bleaching products- Infatuation with white can symbolise overbearing white culture, ingrained into everything: bleach advertised by the Dyer’s, Gladys looking for her white gloves, Dolly looking for her white shoes. Subtly reminder of who is dominant in their society.“Where are my white gloves?” “Honestly, they’re mad about whiteness.”

CharacterisationCharacterises Nan as viewing things rationally, for the purpose of utility and survival. Characterises Dolly as intent on blending into white society despite Indigenous heritage and reveals infatuation with white conventions. She desires to live a life bigger and better than her current one. Characterises Gladys as a dreamer, who wants more for her daughter and wants her to dream, because dreaming will lead to achievement of crazy goals. Characterises Errol as a rare non-discriminatory white male, who is oblivious to the discrimination and erasure towards Indigenous Australians. Wants to learn about their culture, can symbolise reconciliation. Characterises Papa Dear as a powerful inspiration and influence to his family, despite his brief/rare appearances, and as a glorified role model in the Indigenous community for fighting for their rights constantly.“Saved our skins many a time.” “Everyone’s going to be there.” “A peplum.” “Gawd daught, where do you get these highfalutin ideas from?” “She needs to know the world is bigger than just this.” “Er…um…it says that…Well, I’m quite sure there’s a full and very enlightening entry in the complete set.” “Getting a meeting with the busiest Aboriginal around!”
