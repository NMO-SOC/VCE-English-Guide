The rules of the task

  • The title is fixed. You cannot change or modify it; assessors ignored any additions. Choosing a title selects its Framework — no mixing and matching titles, Frameworks or stimulus sets. If your shaded box and your title disagreed, assessors followed the title.
  • Write the title at the start and shade both the Framework and stimulus boxes.
  • Stimulus ideas must come from your selected Framework's stimulus material, though how you use them is open.
  • Mentor texts don't need referencing, and there is no preferred form, style, purpose or audience — responses could explain, express, reflect or argue, and could adapt or subvert any prose form.

What lifted responses

  • Using the title as the focus while exploring Framework ideas purposefully through a cohesive text.
  • Making deliberate structural and linguistic choices that amplify ideas — the annotated high scorers moved economically between place and time, used imagery to imply location, and chose words that carried complex emotion.
  • Establishing a voice that connects strategically with the reader — one exemplar built "a thoughtful, confiding reflective voice" from its opening lines.
  • Trusting an adult reader to infer meaning, and guiding that inference deliberately.

What limited responses

  • Using stimulus literally (a story of children playing a board game with no other connection to the Framework or title).
  • Rambling texts with no observable purpose; simplistic or barely-related ideas; relying on plot alone.
  • Contorting a pre-prepared piece to fit the exam title, or writing for yourself rather than a reader.
  • Sensationalised content, cloying sentiment and clichéd language — assessors said these "were rarely the most powerful option". Remember an unknown adult reads this for assessment.
