Part 04 · Analytical Text Response Essays

Prompt Types: Discuss, To What Extent, Do You Agree, How Does…

The instruction word in a topic tells you what kind of essay to write. Below are the four main prompt types with the approach for each, using Sunset Boulevard examples — the same skills transfer directly to Rainbow’s End.

1. The ‘Discuss’ prompt

‘Sunset Boulevard suggests that Hollywood destroys those who depend on it.’ Discuss.

‘Discuss’ asks you to explore multiple aspects of the idea, consider different perspectives, present a balanced argument, and develop a clear but nuanced contention. You do not have to strongly agree or disagree, but your response should show complexity.

Approach

  • Step 1 — Key words and main ideas. Here: the Hollywood industry, destruction/harm, dependence on fame, and the characters affected.
  • Step 2 — Form a nuanced contention. e.g. Billy Wilder’s Sunset Boulevard suggests that Hollywood can destroy those who rely on its approval, yet the film ultimately reveals that personal delusion and the industry’s exploitation together lead to tragedy.
  • Step 3 — Plan body paragraphs. 1. Hollywood abandons former stars (Norma). 2. The industry exploits desperate individuals (Joe). 3. Delusion and self-deception worsen the damage.
Sample essay — ‘Discuss’ prompt

Billy Wilder's Sunset Boulevard presents Hollywood as a seductive yet destructive environment that consumes those who depend on its approval. Through the tragic figure of Norma Desmond and the cynical narrator Joe Gillis, Wilder exposes an industry that celebrates success but quickly discards those who no longer serve its interests. However, while Hollywood contributes to the characters' downfall, the film ultimately suggests that personal delusion and moral compromise also play significant roles in their destruction.

One of the clearest examples of Hollywood's destructive influence is seen in Norma Desmond's tragic isolation. Once a celebrated silent film star, Norma has been forgotten by an industry that has moved on to new technologies and new faces. Wilder portrays her mansion as a decaying monument to the past, symbolising how Hollywood abandons those who no longer fit its commercial needs. Norma's desperate belief that the world still adores her reflects the cruelty of an industry that once elevated her to fame but now ignores her entirely. When she declares, "I am big. It's the pictures that got small," Norma reveals her inability to accept Hollywood's rejection, highlighting the devastating impact the industry has had on her identity.

Similarly, the film demonstrates how Hollywood exploits individuals who are desperate for success. Joe Gillis arrives in Hollywood hoping to become a successful screenwriter, yet he quickly discovers the industry's indifference to genuine talent. His scripts are repeatedly dismissed by producers who prioritise profit over creativity. As a result, Joe becomes financially desperate and morally compromised, eventually agreeing to live off Norma while pretending to work on her screenplay. Wilder uses Joe's cynical narration to expose the harsh realities of the film industry, where ambition often leads to humiliation and compromise.

However, Wilder also suggests that the characters themselves contribute to their own destruction. Norma's downfall is not solely the result of Hollywood's cruelty but also her refusal to confront reality. Her obsession with fame prevents her from adapting to the changing world around her. Likewise, Joe willingly participates in the illusion that sustains Norma's fantasy, despite recognising its absurdity. His decision to exploit Norma's wealth ultimately entraps him in a toxic relationship that leads to his death. Through these characters, Wilder implies that the destructive power of Hollywood is intensified by the individuals who choose to remain trapped within its illusions.

Ultimately, Sunset Boulevard portrays Hollywood as a glamorous yet unforgiving industry that can destroy those who depend on it. Through Norma's tragic delusion and Joe's moral decline, Wilder reveals the devastating consequences of a culture that values fame above humanity. Yet the film also reminds audiences that the greatest danger lies not only in Hollywood itself but in the human desire for recognition and success at any cost.

2. The ‘To what extent do you agree?’ prompt

‘Norma Desmond is more a victim than a villain.’ To what extent do you agree?

You must clearly state a position on a spectrum and support it, partially agree or disagree, and evaluate both sides of the argument. This type of prompt requires qualification and complexity.

Approach

  • Step 1 — Take a qualified position. e.g. While Norma Desmond is responsible for her destructive actions, Wilder portrays her primarily as a tragic victim of Hollywood’s cruelty and her own psychological isolation.
  • Step 2 — Plan paragraphs. 1. Norma as victim of Hollywood. 2. Norma as psychologically fragile and manipulated by illusion. 3. Norma as morally responsible for Joe’s death.
Sample essay — ‘To what extent’ prompt

In Sunset Boulevard, Billy Wilder presents Norma Desmond as both a tragic victim and a dangerous figure capable of manipulation and violence. Although her obsessive behaviour ultimately leads to Joe Gillis's death, Wilder encourages audiences to view Norma primarily as a victim of Hollywood's cruelty and the isolating effects of fame. Consequently, while she bears responsibility for her actions, Norma is more convincingly portrayed as a victim than a villain.

Norma's victimhood is most clearly established through her relationship with Hollywood itself. Once adored by audiences during the silent film era, she has been completely abandoned by the industry that once celebrated her. Wilder emphasises this rejection through Norma's decaying mansion, which functions as a metaphor for her fading career. The grand but neglected house reflects how Hollywood discards its stars once they are no longer profitable. Norma's desperate longing to return to the screen illustrates the emotional damage caused by this rejection, suggesting that her delusions are partly the result of an industry that first glorified and then ignored her.

Furthermore, Norma's psychological fragility reinforces her position as a tragic victim. Living in near total isolation with only her loyal servant Max, she exists within a carefully maintained fantasy that she is still admired by millions. Max secretly writes fan letters to sustain this illusion, preventing Norma from confronting reality. Wilder's portrayal of these fabricated letters reveals how Norma's fragile mental state has been manipulated and protected rather than challenged. As a result, her delusion grows stronger, ultimately distancing her from the real world.

However, it would be simplistic to view Norma solely as a helpless victim. Wilder also depicts her as capable of manipulation and cruelty, particularly in her relationship with Joe Gillis. Norma effectively traps Joe in her mansion by exploiting his financial desperation and showering him with expensive gifts. When Joe attempts to leave her, Norma reacts with possessive rage, ultimately shooting him in a moment of emotional instability. These actions demonstrate that Norma is not entirely innocent; she actively contributes to the toxic dynamic that leads to tragedy.

Despite this, Wilder's final portrayal of Norma encourages sympathy rather than condemnation. As she descends the staircase believing she is performing for the cameras, her famous line "All right, Mr DeMille, I'm ready for my close-up" captures the full tragedy of her delusion. The moment reveals a woman who has completely lost her grasp on reality, consumed by the very fame that once defined her identity. Rather than presenting her as a villain, Wilder depicts a broken individual who has been destroyed by a world that once worshipped her.

Therefore, while Norma Desmond cannot be entirely absolved of responsibility, she is ultimately portrayed as more victim than villain. Through her tragic decline, Wilder exposes the devastating consequences of fame and the emotional damage inflicted by an industry that values success but offers little compassion for those it leaves behind.

3. The ‘Do you agree?’ prompt

‘Joe Gillis is responsible for his own downfall.’ Do you agree?

You must take a clear position (yes / mostly / no) and present a clear argument. Unlike ‘discuss’, this usually requires stronger judgement.

Approach

  • Step 1 — Decide your position. e.g. While Joe’s moral compromises contribute to his downfall, Wilder suggests that Hollywood’s exploitative culture and Norma’s instability play equally significant roles.
  • Step 2 — Plan paragraphs. 1. Joe’s moral choices. 2. Hollywood’s exploitation. 3. Norma’s control and obsession.
Sample essay (shortened) — ‘Do you agree’ prompt

In Sunset Boulevard, Joe Gillis appears to be the architect of his own downfall, willingly entering a morally compromising arrangement with Norma Desmond. However, Billy Wilder's film ultimately presents Joe's fate as the result of multiple pressures, including the harsh realities of Hollywood and Norma's dangerous obsession. Consequently, while Joe bears some responsibility for his choices, it would be inaccurate to view him as solely responsible for his tragic end.

Joe's personal decisions undeniably contribute to his downfall. As a struggling screenwriter facing financial ruin, he chooses to exploit Norma's loneliness in exchange for comfort and security. Rather than leaving the mansion when he recognises the unhealthy nature of their relationship, Joe remains because he enjoys the luxury and stability it provides. His cynical narration reveals that he is fully aware of the moral compromises he is making, yet he continues the arrangement for personal gain.

Nevertheless, Wilder also portrays Hollywood as an environment that pushes individuals like Joe toward desperation. Throughout the film, Joe struggles to sell his scripts, repeatedly encountering producers who dismiss his work. The industry's focus on commercial success rather than creativity leaves Joe financially unstable and emotionally disillusioned. In this sense, Hollywood indirectly contributes to his downfall by creating the circumstances that lead him to Norma's mansion in the first place.

Finally, Norma's unstable obsession with Joe significantly accelerates the tragedy. Her intense need for affection transforms their relationship into one of emotional control and dependency. When Joe attempts to leave, Norma's inability to cope with rejection leads to violence. This moment demonstrates that Joe's fate is not entirely within his control; it is also shaped by Norma's psychological fragility.

Ultimately, Wilder portrays Joe Gillis as a flawed but sympathetic character whose downfall results from both personal choices and external pressures. While he cannot be completely absolved of responsibility, his fate reflects the broader corruption and illusion that define Hollywood in Sunset Boulevard.

4. The ‘How does…’ prompt

How does Billy Wilder depict the destructive nature of fame in Sunset Boulevard?

Some VCE English questions begin ‘How does Wilder…’ — these require you to focus, even more than other prompt types, on the methods and techniques the author uses to convey the message to the audience. You must explain what the director is showing, how the director shows it, and why the director presents it that way.

Key elements in this prompt: How → explain techniques · depict → representation · destructive nature of fame → theme.

Approach

  • Step 1 — Identify the key idea. Fame is destructive: it creates delusion, causes isolation, leads to moral compromise.
  • Step 2 — Identify the techniques. Characterisation (Norma), setting (the mansion), cinematography, symbolism, dialogue, narration (Joe).
  • Step 3 — Form a contention. e.g. In Sunset Boulevard, Billy Wilder depicts the destructive nature of fame through Norma Desmond’s tragic delusion, Joe Gillis’s moral compromise and the decaying world of Hollywood that traps its characters in illusion.
  • Step 4 — Plan paragraphs. 1. Norma represents the destructive effects of fame. 2. Joe shows how ambition leads to compromise. 3. Hollywood itself as a corrupt ‘dream factory’.

Body paragraph structure for ‘How does…’

Each paragraph should follow: Argument → Evidence → Technique → Effect. For example: Wilder portrays Norma Desmond as a tragic symbol of fame’s destructive power → this is evident through the decaying mansion → the setting symbolises… → through this imagery, Wilder suggests…

Quick strategy: answer three questions for any ‘How does…’ prompt — What idea is the author exploring? What technique do they use? What effect does this create for the audience?
e.g. Norma’s mansion → symbolism → shows the decay of fame → effect on the audience. That is analysis.
Sample essay — ‘How does…’ prompt

In Sunset Boulevard, Billy Wilder presents Hollywood as a seductive yet deeply destructive world in which fame ultimately isolates and destroys those who pursue it. Through the tragic character of Norma Desmond, the moral decline of Joe Gillis and the decaying environment of Hollywood itself, Wilder exposes the devastating consequences of a culture obsessed with celebrity. By combining striking visual symbolism with sharp dialogue and cynical narration, the film reveals how the pursuit of fame traps individuals in illusion and prevents them from confronting reality.

Wilder most clearly illustrates the destructive nature of fame through the character of Norma Desmond. Once a celebrated silent film star, Norma now lives in isolation within a vast, deteriorating mansion that symbolises her fading glory. The setting functions as a powerful visual metaphor for the decay of her career and mental state. Surrounded by photographs and relics from her past, Norma remains trapped in a world of nostalgia and denial. Her famous declaration, "I am big. It's the pictures that got small," reveals her inability to accept that the film industry has moved on without her. Through Norma's delusion, Wilder demonstrates how fame can become inseparable from identity, leaving individuals unable to cope when their celebrity disappears.

Similarly, Wilder uses the character of Joe Gillis to show how the pursuit of success in Hollywood leads to moral compromise. As a struggling screenwriter facing financial ruin, Joe reluctantly agrees to live in Norma's mansion and pretend to work on her screenplay. His cynical voice-over narration emphasises his awareness that the situation is absurd and exploitative. Nevertheless, Joe continues the arrangement because it provides comfort and security. This moral compromise illustrates how Hollywood encourages individuals to sacrifice integrity in pursuit of success. Wilder's use of narration allows the audience to recognise Joe's internal conflict while also highlighting the corrupt values of the industry.

Furthermore, Wilder depicts Hollywood itself as an environment built upon illusion and superficiality. Throughout the film, the glamorous image of Hollywood contrasts sharply with the loneliness experienced by its characters. The film studios, producers and aspiring writers represent a system that constantly searches for novelty while discarding those who are no longer profitable. Norma's desperate hope that director Cecil B. DeMille will revive her career reflects the cruel nature of an industry that once celebrated her but now ignores her existence. By exposing this contradiction between glamour and reality, Wilder critiques the film industry's relentless pursuit of fame and youth.

Ultimately, Sunset Boulevard portrays fame as a powerful but destructive force that traps individuals within illusion. Through Norma's tragic delusion, Joe's moral compromise and the corrupt culture of Hollywood, Wilder reveals how the pursuit of celebrity can lead to isolation, self-deception and tragedy. In doing so, the film offers a powerful critique of a society that values fame above authenticity and human connection.
