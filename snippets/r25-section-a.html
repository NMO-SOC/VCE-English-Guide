Part 12

Section A: Analytical Response to a Text

The 2025 topics were deliberately narrow — specified ideas and values rather than broad prompts — and this shaped how responses needed to be structured, sequenced and evidenced. Students with topic-interpretation strategies handled both familiar and unfamiliar phrasings well.

Topic types

  • Propositional topics offered a proposition with a command term: Discuss, Do you agree? or To what extent do you agree? You may challenge the proposition where warranted, but never the topic's conceptual focus itself — a topic linking love and destruction requires discussing both, though "inseparable" can be contested.
  • Quotation topics paired a quotation with a proposition. There was no obligatory way to use the quotation, but how you used it revealed your understanding of what it conveys in context.

The verbs

The verb in a topic carries the author's purpose, and flattening every verb into a generic "presents" was unwise. Assessors also warned that verbs are often nominalised or implied, and new ones appear each year.

Implications: where the top marks lived

There was a direct correlation between recognising a topic's implications and higher scores. Watch for: absolute terms ("there are no right choices"), connotation ("easy to overlook"), what a proposition is silent about, comparison ("requires more than individual effort" — the 2025 Rainbow's End topic), an included quotation, and the verb itself.

What high-scoring responses did

  • Interpreted before writing — considered what the topic implies, not just what it states.
  • Sequenced strategically — used opening and closing summary sentences in each paragraph to make ideas and their connections explicit, guiding the reader through the reading of the text.
  • Substantiated with range — drew on linguistic elements, characters, structure, plot and allusion, and discussed how these interplayed; assessors noted high scorers took "a more philosophic consideration of the ideas, rather than a literal consideration".
  • Resolved the topic — used the conclusion to actually answer the question, showing the interplay of context, audience and purpose.
  • Used language to communicate. Sophisticated vocabulary scored only when it carried sophisticated ideas; "poorly applied polysyllabic words" were called out explicitly.

What limited responses

  • Discussing a favourite theme adjacent to the topic instead of the topic (e.g. writing about patriarchy when the topic was about truth).
  • Addressing only half the topic — villainy but not victimhood in the Sunset Boulevard topic "individuals can be both victims and villains".
  • Substituting prepared material for the terms actually on the paper, or having no strategy for unfamiliar words.
