Part 12

Key Takeaways from the 2025 Assessment Report

A summary of what the VCAA assessors reported from the 2025 English examination, and what it means for your preparation.

On this page

  • Section A: Analytical response to a text
  • Section B: Creating a text
  • Section C: Analysis of argument and language

General observations

Average scores sat in the middle of the range across all three sections (5.4, 5.4 and 5.5 out of 10), so there was substantial room at the top for well-prepared students. Across every section, assessment was holistic against the published criteria and expected qualities (EQs): the quality of your ideas sets the challenge, how you structure and substantiate them comes second, and control of language third. Crucially, the EQs are always applied in relation to the topic — strong ideas, evidence or expression that do not answer the set question cannot score highly.

Section A: Analytical response to a text

The 2025 topics were not broad prompts. They were narrow, specifying particular ideas and values — and this shaped how responses needed to be structured, sequenced and evidenced. Assessors highlighted several preparation points.

  • Know your topic types. Topics came as propositions (with command terms like Discuss, Do you agree? and To what extent do you agree?) and as quotation topics, where a quotation accompanies the proposition. You may challenge a proposition where warranted, but you can never dismiss the topic’s conceptual focus itself.
  • Attend to the verb. The verb in the topic carries the author’s purpose — 2025 topics used verbs such as celebrates, challenges, condemns, mocks, highlights and suggests, and introduced new ones including depicts, glorifies and warns. Ignoring the verb, or flattening every verb into a generic “presents”, was unwise. Expect new and nominalised verbs.
  • Hunt the implications. Higher scores correlated strongly with recognising a topic’s implications: absolute terms (“no right choices”), connotation, what a proposition is silent about, comparisons (“requires more than individual effort” — a 2025 Rainbow’s End topic), and the role of an included quotation.
  • Everything through the topic. Quotations and textual knowledge only count when they serve a relevant discussion of the set topic.

Section B: Creating a text

Protest was by far the most popular Framework (47% of students), followed by personal journeys (25%), play (17%) and country (10%) — average scores were nearly identical across all four, so choose the Framework that suits your ideas, not the one that seems popular.

  • The title is fixed and it rules everything. You cannot change or modify it, and choosing a title selects its Framework — no mixing and matching titles, Frameworks or stimulus sets. Write the title at the start of your response and shade the Framework and stimulus boxes.
  • What lifted responses: using the title as the focus for exploring Framework ideas; building a cohesive, purposeful text; making deliberate structural and linguistic choices that amplify ideas; establishing a voice that connects strategically with the reader; and trusting an adult reader to infer meaning.
  • What limited responses: literal, surface-level use of stimulus with no real connection to the Framework; rambling texts without an observable purpose; simplistic ideas; relying on plot alone; writing for yourself rather than a reader; and contorting a pre-prepared piece to fit the exam title.
  • Tone matters. Assessors noted that sensationalised content, cloying sentiment and clichéd language were rarely powerful. Remember the assessor is always a secondary audience, and exam responses are not private texts.
  • Mentor texts do not need to be referenced.

Section C: Analysis of argument and language

The 2025 text was a letter to a community newsletter from a “local resident”, proposing a change to an annual fireworks celebration. The background information was vital: inferring the regional-town audience, the author’s altruistic credibility and the unhurried, evolutionary framing of the proposal enabled the most focused analyses.

  • Identification is not analysis. Most students could identify arguments and techniques; far fewer could explain how the sequencing of ideas served the purpose, or what a technique actually did to the intended audience.
  • Interplay is the discriminator. The strongest responses explained how argument, written language and visuals worked together as a cohesive whole — e.g. how the image of a frightened dog developed the argument, not just that it appealed to emotion.
  • Select strategically. Discussing every element is impossible in the time available and was not expected. Choosing the elements most central to the persuasion correlated strongly with success.
  • Read inferentially first. Use the background information to establish context, audience and purpose before you write — the analysis flows from it.
