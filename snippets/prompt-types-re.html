Part 03 · Rainbow’s End

Prompt Types: Discuss, To What Extent, Do You Agree, How Does…

The same four instruction words, applied to Rainbow’s End. The skills are identical to the Sunset Boulevard version — what changes is the evidence: as a play, Rainbow’s End asks you to analyse dramatic techniques rather than film techniques.

1. The ‘Discuss’ prompt

‘Rainbow’s End is a bitter condemnation of colonisation.’ Discuss.

‘Discuss’ asks you to explore multiple aspects of the idea, consider different perspectives, and present a balanced, nuanced contention. You do not have to strongly agree or disagree — but you must engage the topic’s conceptual focus (here: condemnation, and colonisation) rather than dismissing it.

Approach

  • Step 1 — Key words and main ideas. ‘Bitter’ (how harsh is Harrison’s critique, really?), ‘condemnation’ (of what exactly — policies, attitudes, individuals?), and what the topic is silent about: the play’s hope, humour and celebration of resilience.
  • Step 2 — Form a nuanced contention. e.g. While Harrison unflinchingly exposes the injustices of assimilation-era Australia, Rainbow’s End is less a bitter condemnation than a celebration of the Dear women’s resilience — its critique is delivered with warmth, humour and hope.
  • Step 3 — Plan body paragraphs. 1. What the play condemns: institutional racism and assimilation (the inspector, the bank manager, the town meeting). 2. How the tone complicates ‘bitter’: humour, family warmth and dream sequences. 3. What the play affirms: self-determination, education and hope (Gladys’s speech, Dolly’s nursing ambition).

2. The ‘To what extent do you agree?’ prompt

‘Rainbow’s End exposes the racist underbelly of white Australia.’ To what extent do you agree?

You must state a position on a spectrum and defend it, evaluating both sides. Qualification is the skill: agree, but establish precisely how far, and what else the play is doing.

Approach

  • Step 1 — Take a qualified position. e.g. To a large extent, Harrison exposes the racism embedded in 1950s institutions and everyday attitudes; yet the play’s deeper concern is with the strength of the women who endure it — the exposure serves the celebration.
  • Step 2 — Plan paragraphs. 1. Institutional racism exposed (the housing inspector’s scrutiny, the bank manager’s assumptions). 2. Everyday racism exposed (Nancy at the ball, the town meeting’s ‘interloper’). 3. The qualification: the play’s focus on resilience, family and hope reaches beyond exposure.

3. The ‘Do you agree?’ prompt

‘Harrison points an accusing finger at the weaknesses of mainstream Australia.’ Do you agree?

Take a clear position (yes / mostly / no) and argue it. This instruction usually requires stronger judgement than ‘discuss’ — commit, then prove.

Approach

  • Step 1 — Decide your position. e.g. Largely, yes: Harrison indicts the ignorance and complacency of mainstream Australia — but the accusation is aimed at systems and attitudes rather than at individuals, as the sympathetic portrayal of Errol shows.
  • Step 2 — Plan paragraphs. 1. The accusation: assimilation policy and those who enforce it. 2. Its breadth: ordinary characters’ casual prejudice. 3. The limit of the accusation: Errol’s growth suggests mainstream Australia can learn — the finger points at ignorance, not at people beyond redemption.

4. The ‘How does…’ prompt

How does Rainbow’s End suggest that everything will be alright?

‘How does…’ requires you to focus on the playwright’s methods: what Harrison is showing, how she shows it, and why she presents it that way. For a play, that means dramatic techniques — the theatre-specific choices a director and playwright make.

Approach

  • Step 1 — Identify the key idea. Hope and reassurance: through self-determination, family, and the prospect of education.
  • Step 2 — Identify Harrison’s dramatic techniques. Stage directions (Papa Dear’s dances in the dream sequences); dream sequences as a window into hopes and fears; song (the play opens with ‘Que Sera, Sera’ — ‘whatever will be, will be’); symbols (the humpy and its crocheted pillows, the encyclopedias, the white gloves); dialogue and vernacular (Nan’s voice); the three-generation character structure.
  • Step 3 — Form a contention. e.g. Through the hopeful undercurrent of its dream sequences, the framing song and the growth of Gladys from hesitancy to public defiance, Harrison suggests that — despite the constant threat of colonial attitudes — the Dear family’s self-determination will carry them through.
  • Step 4 — Plan paragraphs. 1. Self-determination staged: Gladys’s arc to the town meeting. 2. Hope embodied: Papa Dear, dream sequences, education. 3. The counterweight: colonial attitudes as the looming threat the hope must answer.

Body paragraph structure for ‘How does…’

Exactly as for Sunset Boulevard: Argument → Evidence → Technique → Effect — but your technique column now reads stage directions, dream sequence, song, symbol, vernacular where the film essay would say camera angle, lighting, mise-en-scène.

Quick strategy: answer three questions for any ‘How does Harrison…’ prompt — What idea is Harrison exploring? What dramatic technique does she use? What effect does this create for the audience?
e.g. Papa Dear’s dream-sequence entrances → stage directions → hope made physically present on stage → the audience feels the family is watched over. That is analysis.
