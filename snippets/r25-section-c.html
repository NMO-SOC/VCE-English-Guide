Part 12

Section C: Analysis of Argument and Language

The 2025 text was a letter to a community newsletter from a "local resident" proposing that a regional town replace its annual fireworks with an alternative celebration. Students found it highly accessible — which made the discriminators sharper.

Background information is not decoration

The publication details and the author's "local resident" persona had to be used: inferring the regional audience and its values, the author's altruistic credibility, and the unhurried "evolution, not urgency" framing enabled the most focused analyses. Read the background inferentially before writing — the analysis flows from it.

The argument structure most students saw

  • Establishing a shared value: love of the celebration in its beautiful setting.
  • Raising previously unconsidered problems — environment, frightened pets (with a photo), noise-sensitive residents, cost — aligned with community values.
  • Rebutting reasons to keep the status quo.
  • Presenting the alternative as a "win-win" (with an inspiring second visual).

What separated skill levels

  • Identification is not analysis. Most students could list arguments and label techniques; far fewer could explain how the sequencing of ideas served the purpose, or what a device actually did to the intended audience.
  • Interplay was the discriminator. The strongest responses explained how argument, written language and visuals worked together — nearly everyone noticed the frightened dog photo, but skill showed in explaining its contribution to the developing argument, not just "appeals to emotion".
  • Strategic selection beat exhaustive coverage. Discussing every element was impossible in the time and wasn't expected; choosing the elements most central to the persuasion correlated strongly with success.

Language worth recognising

Assessor-noted elements included appeals to parochial pride, social justice and economic responsibility; strategic characterisation ("beloved pets", "little Fido or Fluffy", "cheaper and smarter"); escalation ("a single wayward spark ... devastating consequences"); reflection-prompting rhetorical questions; inspirational collective language ("Together we have the opportunity ... to lead the way"); and closing the door on alternatives ("a no-brainer").
